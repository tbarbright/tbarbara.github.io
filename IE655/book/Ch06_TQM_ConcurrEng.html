    Purpose

    Within a globally collaborative, sustainable green quality framework, the purpose of concurrent / simultaneous engineering, or in other words, integrated product / process design methods, is to cut product, process, and service process development lead time, to reduce waste, reduce the number of changes during the design process, to improve product and process quality, and to create a lean workflow from concept to integrated product / process design, manufacture, assembly, maintenance, and after-sales support, meaning a total life-cycle approach, based on analytically established customer requirements, and process models.

    The purpose of this chapter is to offer an:

    • Introduction and overview on concurrent / simultaneous (CE), or parallel engineering, or integrated product / process design engineering, (for our purposes all meaning the same), within a globally collaborative, sustainable green quality framework, and
    • Valuable rules, on how to assure global, collaborative integrated product / process design, and manufacturing / assembly / TQC process standards (based on Boeing's principles, as well as based on other mission critical product / process providers, such as Rolls-Royce, BMW, IBM, Raytheon, the International Quality Standard, and others),

    ... and to illustrate the importance of this subject area with real-world and R&D challenges, and cases.

    Why Do We Need Concurrent/ Simultaneous Engineering Integrated with a Total Quality Systems Approach in a Globally Collaborative Design Team?

    Quality must be designed into every product and process. Concurrent, or simultaneous, or parallel engineering, within a globally collaborative, sustainable green quality framework, enables this wonderful concept to be implemented.

    There are many methods and solutions to innovate and develop new products and processes, to cut down waste and to improve an organization, a design office, a manufacturing enterprise, or a product. One major discipline is Concurrent Engineering (CE). CE is also widely known as Simultaneous, or Parallel Engineering, or Integrated Product and Process Design (IP2D) within a TQM system.

    Concurrent Engineering represents a structured (or in IT terms object-oriented), logical framework which supports a systematic approach to the integrated, concurrent design of products, and their related processes, including manufacture, and support.

    It is essential to understand that none of the Concurrent Engineering, with green PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) , with Computer Integrated Manufacturing, and integrated supply chain management methods work in practice unless they are:

    • Applied to all levels of the enterprise,
    • Introduced gradually,
    • Managed professionally by people who understand and fully support them.

    In order to promote this thought, CIM, or Computer Integrated Manufacturing, is now often referred to as the lean, or agile, or adaptive, or reconfigurable manufacturing and design system, preferably implemented in a networked digital factory, in which every major process, and resource is a web-page (for more on this topic please read the article on the web in ADAM with IT...)

    The aims of globally collaborative CE, or integrated lean, or agile, or adaptive, manufacturing and design, within a TQM framework, are very clear: get rid of waste, organize your knowledge, and focus on new opportunities driven by the customer.

    These goals can be achieved by integrating

    • Information flow, meaning objects, and object classes communicating with each other, including humans who can trust each other,
    • Material flow, meaning actual material or information about material being moved in the factory, as well as between factories, and these processes are highly visible, because visual factories show waste very quickly to everyone,
    • Processes, such as design, manufacturing, marketing, service, quality control, and others, in our factories (i.e. the internal and external supply chains) in order to improve quality and productivity, and create wealth for several product life-cycles.

    In other words, Computer Integrated Manufacturing, adaptive supply chain management, and collaborative Concurrent Engineering, integrated with the TQM systems approach address the entire enterprise, including humans and machines, business systems, product design, process planning, manufacturing planning, the shop floor, packaging and maintenance, for several life-cycles, via internal, as well as external supply chains.

    To summarize, CE should be of interest to individuals and managers, concerned with minimizing lead time, project development cost and time to market new products, and / or services. The key concept here is that the desired product quality should be designed into the product at an agreed cost and time frame.

    The Continuous Quality System at Boeing, Focusing on Product / Process Design Improvement Aspects

    Continuous quality improvement is a fundamental principle every global collaborative team should follow. As Boeing states to its internal customers, as well as to its global suppliers, in order to do business with Boeing, ...'the organization shall develop and document a continuous improvement system.

    This system is to include performance measures, such as measures of waste, quality, cycle time, and customer satisfaction.

    The organization shall conduct periodic management reviews of the system, paying close attention to the performance measures, and modifying improvement activities as necessary'.

    Boeing claims, that procedures are needed to make the product / process system design system a reliable part of the supplier’s processes.

    These suppliers can be internal, as well as external.

    In its most advanced form, the TQM embedded CE procedures should take into account enterprise-wide needs and be applied in a way that provides consistency and standardization of improvement efforts.

    To achieve such good integrated product / process design results, the Boeing Continuous Quality Improvement System, as it is the case in most large, quality corporations, focuses on maintaining well documented procedures, therefore they categorize continuous quality procedures as follows:

    1. Procedures exists, but do not cover all Continuous Improvement requirements.
    2. There is a Continuous Improvement system in place, it is developed and well documented.
    3. The documentation of the Continuous Improvement system is consistent and integrated.
    4. The Continuous Improvement system documentation is updated and revised on a regular basis, and manufacturing operations use Continuous Improvement procedures.
    5. Every department in the enterprise practice Continuous Improvement procedures, because it is deployed in every department in a consistant and standard format.

    In order to help internal and external suppliers, Boeing, as many other companies, set up Continuous Quality Improvement system rules, and sets of questions, that help suppliers to conform to these rules.

    As part of our discussion on TQM in CE / integrated product and process design and related Continuous Improvement systems, we'll discuss some of these rule-sets, and the suggested solutions to satisfy them.

    Continuous Improvement Rules on Globally Collaborating Team Procedures

    • Following the ISO9001:2015 quality standard, procedures are needed to make the continuous improvement system a reliable part of the supplier’s processes.
    • The procedures should take into account enterprise-wide needs and be applied in a way that provides consistency and standardization of improvement efforts.
    • Quality documentation is essential, because the contained knowledge can then be shared by others.

    Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

    • Do the company procedures include continuous improvement for integrated product and process design?
    • Is continuous improvement documented in one place, or is it spread through the procedures?
    • Is continuous improvement methodology consistent throughout design and manufacturing, or perhaps the entire enterprise?
    • How do such procedures influence / control enterprise functions, such as design, manufacturing, assembly, and others?
    • Where are, and how are the documents (i.e. CE knowledge) stored?
    • Who is responsible for updating documents, and how frequently is this done?

    Look for Evidence of Implementation as Follows:

    • Look for procedures, that define the continuous product / process improvement systems. (Make sure, that there is a feedback control system between manufacturing / assembly / TQM and design).
    • Look for procedures, and check if they are clearly written, and understood by all involved.
    • Are procedures all in one place, or include easy cross-referencing? (Hyperlinking in electronic documents can help a lot...)
    • Are procedures consistent across all manufacturing elements, and
    • Across the enterprise?
    • Does the documentation follow a standard format?
    • Is it shared across the company, including relevant suppliers and customers?

    Continuous Improvement Rules on Collaborative Design and Customer Relationship Procedures

    • Suppliers often find that quality challenges can best be found in the engineering design phase.
    • Many manufacturing products and business processes are not capable of meeting minimum requirements even when they are operating at maximum effectiveness and efficiency. The fundamental cause is often found in the lack of integrated product / process design of the product itself.
    • Customer / Supplier relationships are fundamental to quality systems, but especially important to the success of variation management in design.
    • The best design solution will occur through trusted communication between customers and suppliers. Suppliers generally have unique knowledge and experiences that are critical to the best design solution.
    • Designers must have critical process knowledge, a good idea of product functionalities required by the customer (by means of QFD, CORA requirements analysis tools).
    • Engineering data must include critical reference points, and datum points and surfaces to ensure robust manufacturing and test, and to reduce the possibility of any variations.
    • By defining each stage of the fabrication and assembly process, the integrated product / process design team minimizes variation in the outcome of the processes.
    • In almost every design requirement there is a variation-dependent attribute or Key Characteristic (KC) that must be identified and controlled, either by robust design or by process control.
    • It is critical that each process design is influenced by all potential customers (meaning internal, as well as external customers), so that the variation-sensitive elements can be identified, and controlled.

    Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

    • Is there a process model, and a followed communication procedure between product design and process design, between suppliers and customers?
    • Do designers have access to customer requirements analysis (like QFD / CORA) details? Do they understand these results well?
    • Does communication on design requirements and concepts occur more often than that of the contractually agreed minimum?
    • Are product and process design changes communicated and updated? How is this process (and new knowledge) documented?
    • How are engineering reference points, and Key Characteristic data coordinated among design, manufacturing and inspection / test? At what stages does this coordination take place?
    • Is there a formal process to ensure, that this coordination takes place?
    • Have there been measurable cost or quality improvements to your customers due to the input of suppliers?
    • Has the company realized product and process improvements resulting from collaborative efforts with customers and suppliers? How was this success measured?
    • How do the given requirements affect the fit, performance and/or the multiple - service life of the customer’s product?
    • Have the top-level key characteristics provided by the customer been well understood and documented?
    • Is there a well defined process, that ensures the co-ordination of reference points, and datum points and surfaces from design to manufacture and inspection / test?

    Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

    • Are there records showing, that key suppliers participate in milestone product / process design meetings? Is there any evidence of meaningful collaboration with customers and suppliers?
    • Is there any evidence of the QFD / CORA analysis, and data to be considered at all levels during the design / manufacturing / test process?
    • Is there a systematic procedure in place for the integrated product / process design team to work with customers and suppliers?
    • Are there any records indicating the effectiveness of design improvement suggestions submitted by second tier suppliers?
    • Are there any records of cost or quality improvements to customers due to design inputs?
    • Are the Key process Characteristic communicated to all involved?

    Continuous Improvement Rules on How Manufacturing Process Capabilities Influence Product / Process Design

    • Manufacturing process capabilities influence contracting and design rules, in that however good the design is, if manufacturing is NOT capable of producing the required tolerances, surface finish, consistant quality, etc. the design will fail...
    • Manufacturing process capabilities must be part of the contract between integrated product and process designers.
    • The goal of this requirement is to ensure that all the information that is needed by a large number of functions related to the product is defined.
    • These functions can include manufacturing, quality, inspection and test, the end user, and even the maintained of the product. The preferable way to define this information is to use a cross-functional team.
    • The goal of having a standard manufacturing process is to minimize product variation due to differences in interpretation. The earlier manufacturing process capability is defined and acknowledged by design, the more influence that information will have on achieving a robust design, which in turn reduces product variability.
    • In addition to improving product flow, lean manufacturing tools and concepts can help reduce variation by standardizing processes.
    • Furthermore, cellular manufacturing improves internal customer / supplier communication, which in turn stimulates the identification of improvement opportunities.
    • The reduction of work in process (WIP) inventory at all levels offers additional improvements and savings.

    Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

    • Is there a process to capture statistically based process capabilities from local and external suppliers?
    • Are process capabilities and yield being captured on all processes?
    • Is there a process to allocate engineering tolerances per process capabilities?
    • Is there a database of process capabilities, that designers can access directly from the CAE (Computer Aided Engineering) workstations?
    • When is manufacturing process information generated? Before, during, or after the design?
    • Are there cross-functional design / build teams in place, communicating with suppliers and customers?
    • Are the learned lessons stored in a system history / knowledge base, so that future design projects could benefit?
    • Are manufacturing instructions clear enough to avoid different interpretations of the identified tasks and their sequence?

    Evidence of Implementation to Check Typically Include the Following:

    • Are there records of process capabilities and yield information available to the integrated product / process design team?
    • Are there consistant records of process capabilities offered by key suppliers?
    • Is there a procedure within the integrated product / process design team on how to allocate dimensioning tolerances in relationship to process capabilities, so that engineering tolerances on product drawings match process capabilities in the factories where they are built?
    • Is there a procedure within the integrated product / process design team on how to choose fasteners, and standard components, such as bearings, and others in relationship to process capabilities, so that engineering tolerances on product drawings match process capabilities?
    • Are there any records showing when manufacturing process information is generated with respect to the design phase?
    • Is there a database of lessons learned, and is this incorporated into the manufacturing and inspection instructions?
    • Look for records showing the fact, that customer requirements are clearly interpreted in the resulted design, as well as manufacturing / assembly/ service / IT, etc. specifications.

    To summarize the message offered by the above rules, by identifying, understanding and managing interrelated processes as a system contributes to the organization's effectiveness and efficiency in achieving its objectives.

    Global Collaborative Design is the First Step in Global Collaborative Product Manufacturing / Assembly

    Every design decision, if not considered carefully can cost unnecessary design, collaborative management, networking, marketing, manufacturing, quality and other loss. The case is even more complex in globally collaborating teams...

    Just consider the huge Toyota recall case in 2010, costing Toyota about $10 Billion! And as we can see in this not narrated video clip, even the 2011 Toyota floor mat design is incorrect... lessons should be learned from poor designs...

    A product design must be modular, and should be carefully tuned to flexible assembly and manufacturing technologies operated in a lean CIM environment in order to fully realize the achievable quality, productivity, assembly and maintenance improvement.

    The product and the manufacturing system should be designed with total productive maintenance in mind. By using these concepts the product should be manufactured with minimum waste. (Robot gripper example-1, example-2, and example-3.)

    There are many internal and external technical, human and legal factors that put increasing pressure on a globally collaborative team (including the design, manufacturing, quality and maintenance engineers, accountants, lawyers, vendors and potential customers) when designing a new product, process, or service.

    Due to international competition, and the desire for high quality products at low cost, the design engineering team must work significantly closer with all those involved in the creation of a successful product (i.e. the customers, the marketing, manufacturing, quality, service, IT, and maintenance engineers).

    Design for Manufacturing / Assembly (DFM, DFA) is a systematic step that one needs to carefully analyze for each part or sub-assembly.

    In particular, in the case of complex parts (example-1, example-2, example-3) the questions to be answered include: What rapid prototyping, and manufacturing processes? Manual or robotized manufacture and assembly, or mass customization...? (Robot placement example video.)

    Waste reduction, should be an important aim, in particular in globally collaborating teams in which it is often difficult to identify and locate waste. Waste reduction, in this sense can mean one or more of the following:

    • Products that have unnecessary functions (i.e. functions that don't add value for a certain market); this can easily happen if the collaborative, international team has no cultural input from local user groups (example-1, example-2)
    • Many parts that could have been eliminated, are actually not eliminated maybe due to the lack of design for manufacturing / assembly skills, or because of the focus of employing as many workers as possible, or a
    • Large number of assembly directions that need several additional operations and even perhaps more robots than necessary, again maybe due to the fact that the design team doesn't really understand manufacturing processes and systems, including what robots are capable of (example-1, example-2),
    • Parts that have to be manufactured to unnecessarily tight tolerances, costing more than necessary,
    • Parts that do not comply with international standards, or
    • Parts, that do comply with some international standards, nevertheless many companies ignore them... (e.g. health and safety, sustainability, pollution prevention, toxic materials in products and processes, and green engineering standards are good examples for this), and
    • Others.

    Design for Recyclability / Reusability (DFR, or DfR) should also be considered by the globally collaborative team. DfR considers the materials from which a product is manufactured and assembled. 

    If DfR is applied during a product's conception phase and carried through to its design, manufacturing, assembly, reuse, remanufacturing and disposal phases, these criteria can be effective to minimize waste and maximize the reuse of materials. (You can see here poor, as well as better examples: example-1, example-2, example-3, example-4, example-5.)

    Our Design for Disassembly (DfD) criterion below should guide a design team to create less complicated products and manufacturing / assembly / inspection / maintenance processes.

    These are some of the DfR rules a global, collaborative design team should carefully consider and follow:

    • Use recyclable, reusable, and if possible even biodegradable materials.
    • Design products using materials that can be recycled and for which material collection and recycling technologies are available and commonly used.
    • Generally, metals are easier to recycle than non-metals, and thermoplastic resins are more desirable than thermo set plastics. Alternatively, one should set up an effective and safe material collection system and focus on alternative products too (e.g. recycled automotive tires can serve well in roads).
    • Use recycled materials. Select materials that contain a high percentage of recycled content.
    • Reduce the number of different parts and materials used within an assembly. 
    • Reduce the number of materials used to manufacture a component or assembly.
    • Reducing the number of materials also simplifies the separation process and supports recycling. (Example-1, example-2.)
    • Mark parts for simple material identification. Mark all materials with standard material identification codes. Although this process is most feasible for plastic parts, it can be expanded to metals, composite materials, and coatings.
    • For more complex parts and assemblies use RFID technology for accurate identification. RFID also helps when certain parts must be replaced in a system due to regulatory requirements, or batch failure, such as in the case of an automotive recall.
    • Use compatible materials within an assembly. 
    • Select materials that do not need to be separated for recycling.
    • Generally, mixtures of dissimilar plastics cannot be recycled. Similarly, nonferrous metals (e.g., aluminum, chromium, or zinc) can contaminate and thus decrease the recyclability of ferrous metals (i.e., iron and steel), and vice versa.
    • Layers of paint or plated metal over a base material also represent contaminants not compatible with recycling.
    • If a certain type of coating on metal cannot be removed, the paint or the metal plating will be a contaminant that decreases the metal's recyclability and/or the applications for which the recycled metal can be used.
    • Make the designs easy to disassemble. (Example-1)

    • Analyze and review the actual processes and the way the process flow is integrated. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method from concept to product. To understand total quality and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us...)
    • Analyze potential failure modes, and alternative solutions
    • Analyze the Total Quality Management aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service.
    • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of each process/ solution.
    • Design alternative integrated product / process design TQM methods, processes based on what you have experienced/ seen, and learned.
    • Design an integrated TQM system, based on what you have analyzed.

    • Understand the boundaries of TQM, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and developments by working on the cases presented. (Realize that in order to survive and win, you must add quality value).
    Concurrent Engineering with green PLM is intended to ensure that developers consider all elements of the product life cycle from concept through re-engineering, and final disposal, including quality, cost schedule and user requirements, and therefore avoid major mistakes...

    The amazing truth is, that there is nothing new about this principle: please review by clicking on our image gallery of the USS Constellation, built in 1854...

    Of course what has changed is the complexity of our product designs, and also the tools we can use today to make sure that they are fully tested in the digital domain BEFORE they are actually built... at the time when the USS Constellation was designed and built, such advanced CAD, CAM, CE and PLM tools did not exist... still a very impressive design.
