    Purpose

    The purpose of this Chapter is to open up the critical facets of designing, operating, and managing Concurrent Engineering design teams, as well as working in teams, that follow TQM principles, and blend well, and can create a new enterprise TQM culture.

    In this Chapter we offer the key concepts for designing and managing teams, that:

    • Conform to International, and / or U.S. total quality standards,
    • Explain the reasons why organizations must follow quality standards, and
    • Illustrate the importance of this subject area with real-world and R&D challenges.

    Why Do We Need to Work in Teams?

    The success of any hardware, or software engineering, or engineering management activity, such as the introduction and/ or continuous management of TQM in any organization, largely depends on able and knowledgeable people, and the often complex technologies selected and implemented.

    This activity is mainly a management, a team-oriented communications and problem solving issue, therefore we MUST WORK IN TEAMS!

    Although the requirements for building teams depend on the people involved, what the task is, and what the constraints are (i.e. schedule, budget, and quality goals within the given complexity), there are several generic rules for building effective teams. We summarize these rules with examples in this Chapter.

    As discussed throughout this eBook, the challenges, and the resource requirements in every TQM team typically include the following. (Note: as you read this list, consider the team aspects of every issue we discuss):

    • Analyze the CE/TQM needs and the requirements the demonstrated processes satisfy. To accomplish this task with good results, you need able team members, who have experience in identifying key processes, to perform process analysis (with CIMpgr, or other methods), who know how to interview people and interpret situations and processes, and then extract the necessary 'needs' and 'wants', to be able to reason over what is truly required.
    • Analyze the actual CE/TQM methods presented, find out the core methodologies, the mathematical models, the underlying engineering (and/ or other) science foundation. In terms of team member resources, depending on the job at hand, you'll need industrial engineers, subject area specialists, as well as engineering managers who'll be able to blend all the methods with the available technologies to resolve the challenges at this stage.
    • Analyze the CE/TQM technologies involved. (How is science turned into a practical solution/ engineering and/or computing technology? Learn that science and technology are integrated processes.) Team members at this stage should express interest in technology, and be able to realistically assess the best fit methods and technologies for the particular job.
    • Analyze and review the actual processes and the way the process flow is integrated. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method, like CIMpgr, with UML, from concept to product. To understand total quality and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us...). The critical team skill here is specific process related knowledge, therefore involve subject area experts from the pool of people who 'live with the processes' every day.
    • Analyze potential failure modes, and alternative solutions. The best skill here is broad experience in problem solving complex, systems level processes. The other comment we should make, is that to tackle this stage, the team needs members, who can judge process failure risks with an objective mind, as well as have the necessary methods, and technology base to resolve the identified risky processes.
    • Analyze the Total Quality Management aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service. In Chapter 2 of this eBook we have discussed the eight ISO TQM standard principles. These principles have been created by many knowledgeable and experienced professionals, therefore the best advise here, is to follow these rules and principles, and adopt them to the actual task. In other words, when identifying the attributes for each TQM process, the team should ask itself: How does this process satisfy the eight TQM principles? How could they satisfy them better? What do we need to change, improve? What are the cost and risk implications? How do such changes affect customer requirements and the process flow?
    • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of each process/ solution. This stage is a kind of ROI (Return On Investment) analysis in TQM terms. Again, as in most cases, an object-oriented process model, such as CIMpgr, and a class model in UML (Unified Modeling Language) will help here, in that by comparing the 'as is' versus the 'to be' process models, the difference between the two models, or in other words the benefits and the disadvantages will become very clear, and analytically quantifiable.
    • Design alternative CE/TQM methods, processes based on what you have experienced/ seen, and learned. The skills the team has to poses at this stage are the most complex, since you need to be able to have the experience of alternative solutions that would fit well in the particular case. The team needs broad-based experienced members here, as well as subject experts who posses detailed knowledge of methods, and technologies too. (Vendor involvement helps here, nevertheless you have to make sure, that they don't sell you something you don't need!)
    • Design an integrated CE/TQM system, based on what you have analyzed in this case. The new integrated system design is a challenging team effort. It involves everybody's skills, because the integrated system must work without any flows... in integration with the emerging, or already established enterprise culture. The team should have good systems engineering, systems integration, web-based/ intranet skills, networking as well as database expertise in most cases, since these technologies help and often enable integration by design, i.e. without any major additional cost (e.g. consider the web, as an integration platform).
    • Work in a multi-disciplinary CE/TQM team and exchange ideas. This is a general rule, that emphasizes the importance for the team members to openly exchange their various expertise.
    • Understand the boundaries of CE/TQM, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and developments by working on the cases presented. (Team members at each stage MUST realize that in order to survive and win, they ALL must add quality value).

    Key Aspects of Building Effective Teams

    1. Make sure, that team members are prepared and willing to communicate with each other. (Note, that not every team member is a friendly, open communicator. You have to develop communication skills, as well as have the willingness to communicate, and actively participate in the team's work.)

    2. If team members trust each other, they are open, and will communicate to each other even when stress levels are high.

    3. Set up an open and friendly communication channel between team members, as well as team leaders for resolving group process challenges, and conflicts.

    4. Use conflict in a constructive way. Stressful situations can often result in new ideas!

    5. Make sure, that every team member understands the mission of the team, as well as agrees with the plans. (Note, that process models, requirements analysis and risk analysis models, and affinity diagrams, developed by the team together, can help a lot to pull the team together into a 2+2=5 creative force).

    6. Professional teams are carefully planned, by letting them work through various diagnostic, and problem solving workshops, and provisions are made for after-the-event group, and self-evaluation activities. (Before starting the project, you might want to consider investing in such workshops; the payoffs can be tremendous!)

    Rules for Creating a Team, and then Brainstorming in a CE Team

    Every team needs time to develop respect and get acquainted with each other, therefore when forming a team it is a wise idea to follow these four stages:

    • Forming, meaning, that the team members get together for the first time to begin to develop the essential respect and 'chemistry' that makes excellent teams unique. Potential team members at stage are in an observant mood, trying to listen, feel, see and absorb... because they are assessing each other's capabilities, roles and relationships to each other... The best advice to succeed at this stage is to try to organize this very first session on independent grounds, for example in a nice hotel, or country club, so that the every-day-pressures are removed, phones are disconnected (even cell / mobile phones!), and everybody can start to develop that essential chemistry starting from the same level... (Good meals, a relaxed atmosphere, joint sport activities, and others help here, even it accountants disagree with this, when they see the bills...)
    • Storming, is the common phrase to describe the process when team members drop formal behavior, and begin to act like themselves... this is very important, to create an open atmosphere, and respect not only in 'friendly times', but also when the 'heat is on' and things can get a bit ugly...
    • Norming, is the commonly used terminology in team management to identify the stage, when the team becomes 'cohesive'. At this stage team members have learned how to cope and co-operate with each other... at this stage the team begins to form its own identity, that allows differing ideas, opinions and even some not too serious fights amongst team members.
    • At the performing stage team members are working hard, and paying back all the nice country club investment they have received earlier... At this stage the team has matured, and it is operating at a high speed. There is respect, chemistry, body language (that is not misunderstood anymore...), team identity and morale, that helps active and effective problem solving.

    For teams to succeed, they need a

    • Common mission, they need to be able to develop
    • Openness and trust for each other, they need to have a variety of
    • Complimentary skills, and be able to
    • Take decisions quickly and effectively.

    This overall process is helped a lot by understanding each other, by developing respect for each other, by discussing issues together, by brainstorming, and others. Since brainstorming is a critical method here, let us re-visit this technique in this context again.

    Brainstorming in an active team, or as part of an Internet-based virtual team, or chat-group is an excellent opportunity to come up with ideas that the TQM team should be able to employ.

    Since you'll be probably working in a small real, or virtual team over the web, make sure that everyone in the team understands the basics of brainstorming. These are the brainstorming rules, that are worthwhile to follow:

    1. Clarify the objectives. (In this case focus on TQM).
    2. Identify all the tasks you have to accomplish as individuals as well as a team.
    3. Put the agreed tasks into a logical order.
    4. Estimate the time and additional resources you will need (e.g. web searching and research time) to accomplish the job.
    5. Allocate responsibility.
    6. Review the entire plan before you start and adjust as necessary.

    Having accomplished the above plan, when brainstorming, focus on the following rules:

    1. Keep a relaxed atmosphere by choosing a comfortable, undisturbed environment.
    2. Choose a team leader who has some experience in how a session will operate.
    3. Organize the appropriate team of 3 to 5 or perhaps up-to 7 people. (If you have too few, or too many the intensity of the discussion will suffer!)
    4. Summarize the problem / challenge definition again (see list above).
    5. Generate as many ideas as possible, even if many of them are totally off-beat. (Free wheeling is encouraged! Do NOT discuss or evaluate any of the ideas at this stage because it will divert the focus of the team)
    6. Give everyone equal opportunity to contribute to the new idea generation process. (Let them warm up, relax and roll...)
    7. Write down every idea... even if it sounds crazy at the time...
    8. Allow time for ideas to incubate. Brainstorm in sessions of 30 minutes, or an hour with perhaps a few days between them if the team still has some steam... Don't wait for the steam to run out...

    If you are an engineering/ science student make sure, that you understand and document the following:

    • What are the analytical / mathematical aspects of the illustrated CE/TQM teamworking challenges and solutions? Upon what socio-scientific principles do teams operate? How can you ensure, that the scientific principles are clear to each team member?

    		 If you are a CE/TQM management science/ marketing student focus on how the illustrated team-building ideas should be...
    • Cost-justified to a variety of marketing, design, manufacturing, service, and user communities.
    • Based on our Case Based Marketing methods and ideas, design a team, a business plan, and an engineering management project plan that can be customized for each customer interested in purchasing, leasing, machines / processes / services.

Discuss these ideas with your engineering and computing science team members, as well as selected, potential customers. (Try to set up your own business and implement your ideas!)
