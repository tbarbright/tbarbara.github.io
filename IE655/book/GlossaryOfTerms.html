Acknowledgment In data transmission, after transmitting a data block the transmitting terminal sends a check character, (ACK, or acknowledgment character) based on the contents of the data block. The receiving terminal (or computer) compares this character with its own computation of the check character.

If they are the same then no transmission error has occurred and the receiving terminal sends the control character ACK to the transmitter to signify that the communication was error free.

If the check characters differ, then an error has occurred and the receiver sends a NAK (negative acknowledgment) character to indicate an error to the transmitter. The transmitter then repeats transmission of the same block and the process repeats.

Acoustic Coupler A device that converts electrical signals into audio signals, and vice-versa.

Backbone network A typically high-speed central network, that connects a number of typically slower networks into a system for the purpose of linking networks together with a high-speed communications facility.

Sub-networks branch off the backbone network.

Backbone router A type of router, that connects compatible networks together through a backbone network, so that networks can be linked in a non-serial manner.

Background Tasks Functions performed by the computer while allowing the computer to be used for other activities.

Background functions are not always apparent to the user.

Back up To copy the contents of a disk, directory, or file to another hard or floppy disk, or CD-ROM.

A backup file or disk is made to protect the data in case the original becomes damaged.

Bandwidth, or Data Transfer Rate The range of signal frequencies (or transmission frequencies) which can be carried by a transmission channel subject to specified conditions of signal loss or distortion.

As an example, consider the following: a

A relatively slow Internet communication using a 28.8 modem offers 3.6 kilo bytes per second data transfer rate.

An ISDN connection (Integrated Digital Services Network, on dual lines) offers much more, 16 kilo bytes per seconds (= 2x64 kilo bits per seconds).

Furthermore, consider the fact that a relatively slow (i.e. 2x, or dual-speed) CD-ROM drive gives 300 kilo bytes per seconds transfer rate (=2x150) and a more reasonable CD-ROM, 4x gives 600 kilo bytes per seconds, meaning 166.66 times faster than the 28.8 modem linked Internet!

In comparison a so called T1 line has a bandwidth of 1.544 Mbps (Megabits per second).

Note, that the above analysis of transfer rates is of extreme importance, when delivering high quality, storage intensive interactive multimedia in particular.

bit The smallest computer data unit. A bit can have a value of 1 (one), or 0 (zero).

Combinations of bits build up strings of bits. One of the most commonly known string of bits is the byte, occupying 8 bits (e.g. 10101011).

One of the most fascinating thing about digital computers is that at the heart of the machine everything is represented by strings of zeros and ones, even color photos, and moving images!

Bridge A hardware device that connects two similar networks (zones) so that they appear to be a single, larger network.

Bridges extend networks to expand the resource pool, such as printers, backup storage devices and others, and control network traffic by forwarding only transmissions intended for a different zone.

Broadband A computer network, that can transmit many signals simultaneously.

A broadband communications channel can accommodate broadcast and other services on it.

As an example, consider a broadband network, that uses one channel for digital video, another one for data, another one for voice messaging, etc. - all simultaneously.

There are several different, multiple computer and communications (e.g. voice, video) networks in operation. Some of which are controlled by telephone companies, some not.

Maintaining several different networks and services is a major problem, thus the solution is to develop Broadband ISDN, or B-ISDN (Integrated Services Digital Network) with ATM (Asynchronous Transfer Mode) technology, meaning that the communication is not synchronous, in other words it is not tied to a master clock.

The B-ISDN technology offers on-line live television, video on demand, full screen interactive multimedia and electronic mail, CD quality voice/ sound and music, and fast LAN connections (1 Gbps, one Giga bits per second) for computer networking. The cost is huge!

Broadcast networks One of the two types of transmission technologies (the other one is "Point-to-point networks").

In the case of broadcast networks there is a single communication channel that is shared by all machines on the network.

Messages, called packets (or short messages) are sent by any machine and are received by all other machines.

In a similar fashion to the human communications world, machines ignore packets on the network, unless they are addressed for their attention.

Broadcast traffic A network transmission technique in which packets are sent to all nodes on a network concurrently.

Browser A program, such as Netscape navigator, or Internet Explorer, that enables users to interact with data / information on the Internet, the World Wide Web (WWW).

Buffer A buffer is used as a temporary data store to compensate for the difference in data transmitting and receiving rates between devices.

For example, a printer may receive data at 9600 bps, but if processes it at only 300 bps; a buffer would catch the data and keep it until the printer could use it.

Bulk sampling The use of special procedures to obtain random samples from a large lot, such as a car load of plastic components.

Bus A network topology (or layout) that uses a single cable to connect all devices in a sequential line.

Messages are broadcast along the whole bus, and each network device, or node, listens for and receives messages sent to its unique address.

A signal route to which several items of a computer system may be connected in parallel so that signals can be passed between them.

A bus is also called a trunk in the USA, and a highway in the UK.

byte Usually a group of 8 bits (e.g. 10010011)

The storage capacity of a computer is measured in its number of bytes.

It generally takes one byte to store a single character.

NAK, or Negative Acknowledgment In data transmission, after transmitting a data block the (ACK) transmitting terminal sends a check character, based on the contents of the data block. The receiving terminal compares this character with its own computation of the check character.

If they are the same then no transmission error has occurred and the receiving terminal sends the control character ACK (acknowledgment) to the transmitter to signify that no error occurred.

If the check characters differ, then an error occurred and the receiver sends a NAK (negative acknowledgment) character to indicate an error to the transmitter. The transmitter then repeats transmission of the same block and the process repeats.
