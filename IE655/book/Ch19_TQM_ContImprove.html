Purpose

The fundamental purpose of this Chapter is to understand how to assure, that a product design effort, or a TQM, or TQC, or Six-sigma, or a lean manufacturing business process re-engineering program, or a quality improvement program does not stop at the time of submitting the reports, and receiving accreditation...

This is because, quality must be part of every design.

This also means, that quality must be designed into every product, process and service and even needs a continuous improvement process to encourage overall performance improvement as a permanent, long-term objective of the organization.

.

Why Do We Need to Continuously Improve Product Designs, Processes and Total Quality Service Systems?

In Chapter 2, we have discussed the eight ISO TQM Principles, and we have shown ISO TQM Principle 6: Continual Improvement. Let us re-visit this important set of concepts and methods.

The fundamental purpose of this principle is to assure, that a Six - Sigma, or a lean manufacturing business process re-engineering program, or a quality system program does not stop at the time of submitting the reports, and receiving accreditation... it is a continual improvement principle for the organization, to encourage overall performance improvement processes as a permanent, long-term, continuous objective of the organization.

As a reminder, the Key Drivers and Achievable Gains with the introduction and execution of this principle include the following:

  • Performance advantage through improved organizational capabilities.
  • Alignment of improvement activities at all levels to an organization's strategic intent.
  • Flexibility to react quickly to opportunities.
  • A consistent organization-wide approach to the continual improvement of the organization's performance.
  • Employees should be offered training in the methods and tools of continual improvement.
  • Continual improvement of products, processes and systems should be an objective for every individual in the organization.
  • Employees and management should establish goals to guide, and measure to track continual improvement.
  • Management should recognize and acknowledge improvements.

As a result of this, large organizations, in particular those dealing with mission critical applications and systems, such as health care, aerospace, automotive and transportation product manufacturers, energy generation companies, and others have adopted continuous quality principles and practices with great success.

Companies, that continuously improve their quality have gained international recognition, respect, productivity and major profits, as a result of their focus on continuous quality improvement.

Furthermore, these companies became famous brand names, and benchmarks for others to follow, such as Disney, Rolls-Royce and Bentley, BMW, Boeing, Honda, Toyota Lexus, Mercedes Benz, Apple Computers, Fanuc Robotics, ABB, Singapore Airlines, Virgin Airlines (as an example of service systems), and many others.

Our Generic, Continuous Quality Improvement Methodology

As you research and review each of the listed TQM Continuous Improvement (CI) Rules, on the left hand side, as an implementation strategy, try to follow our proven methodology, by

  • Analyzing first the TQM / CI needs and the requirements,
  • Analyze the actual TQM / CI methods required by the company; find out the core methodologies, the mathematical models, the underlying engineering (and/or other) science foundation that will enable you to consider the best improvement methods and, then
  • Analyze the possible TQM / CI technologies involved. (How is science turned into a practical solution/ engineering and/or computing technology? Learn, that science and technology are integrated processes. Find the best technologies, that will help your processes to improve.)
  • Analyze and review the actual TQM / CI processes and the way the process flow is integrated. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method (e.g. CIMpgr, with UML) from concept to product. To understand total quality improvement and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us. By modeling such processes we can understand them better.)
  • Analyze potential TQM / CI failure modes, and alternative solutions. (Make sure, that processes, that carry the highest levels of RPNs, or Risk Priority Numbers are looked into as serious candidates for continuous improvement opportunities.)
  • Analyze the TQM / CI aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service. (This should be done in integration with the process model, the requirements analysis and the risk analysis models.)
  • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of the TQM / CI process/ solution. (This should be done in integration with the process model, the requirements analysis and the risk analysis models.)
  • Design alternative TQM / CI methods, processes based on the requirements, and your budget.
  • Design an integrated TQM / CI system, based on what you have analyzed, and your budget.
  • Work in a multi-disciplinary TQM / CI team and exchange ideas with team members.
  • Understand the boundaries of TQM / CI, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and development opportunities by working on the cases presented. (Realize, that in order to survive and win, we all must add quality value on a continuously improving and evolving basis).

The Continuous Quality Improvement System at Boeing

Best companies don't just continuously improve themselves. They also insist on the continuous improvement of their suppliers.

As an example for a quality, mission critical product / process / service company we discuss Boeing (USA) methods, as well as others, such as BMW, Rolls-Royce, FANUC, GM, Mercedes-Benz, Rockwell Automation, and others.

Boeing states to its internal customers, as well as to its suppliers, in order to do business with Boing ...'the organization shall develop and document a continuous improvement system.

This system is to include performance measures, such as measures of waste, quality, cycle time, and customer satisfaction.

The organization shall conduct periodic management reviews of the system, paying close attention to the performance measures, and modifying improvement activities as necessary'.

Boeing claims, that procedures are needed to make the Continuous Improvement system a reliable part of the supplier’s processes. These suppliers can be internal, as well as external.

In its most advanced form, the procedures should take into account enterprise-wide needs and be applied in a way that provides consistency and standardization of improvement efforts.

To achieve such good results, the Boeing Continuous Quality Improvement System, as it is the case in most large, quality corporations, focuses on maintaining well documented procedures, therefore they categorize continuous quality procedures as follows (note, that number 5 is the best in this list):

  1. Procedures exists, but do not cover all Continuous Improvement requirements.
  2. There is a Continuous Improvement system in place, it is developed and documented.
  3. The documentation of the Continuous Improvement system is consistent and integrated.
  4. The Continuous Improvement system documentation is updated and revised on a regular basis, and manufacturing operations use Continuous Improvement procedures.
  5. Every department in the enterprise practices Continuous Improvement procedures, because it is deployed in every department in a consistant and standard format.

In order to help internal and external suppliers, Boeing, as many other companies, set up Continuous Quality Improvement system rules, and sets of questions, that help suppliers to conform to these rules.

As part of our discussion on Continuous Improvement systems, we'll discuss some of these rule-sets, and the suggested solutions to satisfy them.

.

Continuous Improvement Rules on Procedures

  • Procedures are needed to make the continuous improvement system a reliable part of the supplier’s processes.
  • The procedures should take into account enterprise-wide needs and be applied in a way that provides consistency and standardization of improvement efforts.

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • Do the company procedures include continuous improvement?
  • Is continuous improvement documented in one place, or is it spread through the procedures?
  • Is continuous improvement methodology consistent throughout design, manufacturing / assembly, or perhaps the entire enterprise?
  • How do such procedures influence / control enterprise functions, such as design, manufacturing, assembly, and others?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • Procedures define the Continuous Improvement systems.
  • Procedures are clearly written, and understood by all involved.
  • Procedures are in one place, or include easy cross-referencing. (Procedures are preferably accessible via the company's secure intranet.)
  • Procedures are consistent across all manufacturing elements and processes.
  • Procedures are consistent across the enterprise.
  • Procedures are consistent across the entire supply chain of the enterprise.

Continuous Improvement Rules on Strategic Performance Measures

  • Improvement activities should be tied to the strategic performance measures, guided by top management, and
  • Applied to both manufacturing and supporting business processes.

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • How are improvement activities prioritized?
  • How are they tied to performance measures? (Note, we can control only those processes and procedures, that we truly understand, and are preferably measurable.)
  • How does management link performance with improvement activity results?
  • How are supporting business processes tied into continuous improvement?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • Improvement activity priorities exist within departments.
  • There is a prioritized list of improvement activities for the whole enterprise.
  • Supporting business processes are tied-in with the enterprise processes.
  • There is a clear representation of how activities impact performance measures.
  • There is a clear record of management guidance and requirements.

Continuous Improvement Rules on Top Management Involvement

  • Top management must lead continuous improvement activities to ensure proper allocation of people and supporting resources.
  • Continuous quality improvement activities must be part of the regular business review.
  • Continuous quality improvement activities must be an enterprise-wide activity from 'shop - to - top', including the supply chain. (Note, many automotive recalls happen because of suppliers' faulty components, such as seat-belts, or ignition systems, and others, therefore suppliers should be part of the TQM / CI process.)

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • Does top level management review continuous improvement activities on a regular basis (within the company, as well as the supply chain)?
  • Do these reviews cover statistically evaluated data?
  • Are performance measures reviewed and associated with continuous improvement activities?
  • Have any changes in the performance measures been attributed to the continuous improvement projects?
  • Have any performance measures been modified as a result of reviewing improvement activities?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • Management holds periodic, well documented reviews to probe and evaluate performance measures, and control them as needed.
  • Management runs periodic, well documented reviews of Process Failure Risk Analysis (an FMEA method) to probe and evaluate risk measures, and control them as needed.
  • There is evidence, that top management's review agenda includes continuous improvement activities
  • Management reviews include metrics and/or project details, derived on the basis of metrics relating to continuous quality improvement
  • The system history database holds well documented enhancements, or modifications to quality performance measures.

Continuous Improvement Rules on Knowledge Development

  • The organization should develop well educated, and trained quality personnel who have experience in how to introduce / conduct/ document / maintain continuous quality improvement projects.
  • This effort should be part of the organization’s culture.

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • Is there an improvement specialist assigned?
  • What are his / her skill levels? How can he /she get on with people and teams?
  • Can he / she mentor and educate others?
  • What tools are available? Do people understand how to use them? Do they use them? What do they do with the results?
  • Is there an established data flow to deal with complaint resolution during the ongoing TQM / CI process? Is the generated new knowledge captured?
  • Are employees made interested in participating in improvement activities?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • There is an improvement specialist assigned.
  • His / her skills are better than average, and he/ she can mentor and educate others.
  • Up-to-date tools (preferably computerized tools) are available, and employees understand how to use them; and they actually use them, on a regular basis.
  • The generated new knowledge captured and fed back to various departments for consideration, as part of the established TQM / CI routine.
  • Employees are rewarded for, and are interested in participating in improvement activities via quality circles, via discussion forums, suggestion lists, and others.

Continuous Improvement Rules for the Purpose of Reducing Product / Process Variation

Since one of the fundamental purposes of TQM and TQC is to reduce process variation, these rules are very important. This is how you can get there:

  • As a result of the analysis of products and / or processes, or during the implementation of improvement projects, or customer defined Key Characteristics may indicate that process variation management activities are required in your organization. As we have seen, statistical process control charts can be an effective means of controlling variation and obtaining process knowledge, because they will help to establish mistake proof processes, develop upstream process controls, contribute to process and product redesign, and eventually reduce the reliance on control charts. The key issue, is to be able to identify, control, and optimize processes.
  • If a process is out-of- control, then this condition must be investigated and corrective action must be taken. If the process is out-of- control, because it is not capable, either because of operator training issues, or because of old / faulty technology, or other reasons, the organization must identify and control sources of variation and take corrective action.
  • In every factory, in every product, or part, or process, there are features whose variation have a strong effect on quality and waste. By means of process mapping, and risk analysis, these features should be identified as key characteristics, and variation in these processes should be controlled, and preferably reduced. (The reduction of variation in these features should lead to higher quality, lowered waste levels and costs, and greater customer satisfaction).

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • Have the key characteristics been identified?
  • Have the parts or processes been evaluated for variation sensitive features?
  • Has a product / process risk analysis tool been employed to identify key characteristics?
  • For every key characteristics: if a key characteristics has been found, is it a part specific issue, or can be treated as a process issue?
  • If it is a process issue, does it effect other parts too?
  • Can part families be identified as a step to move towards process control?
  • Are key characteristics analytically related to process parameters by means of correlation studies? Are the data sets of such studies stored in a database for further, and future analysis (i.e. data mining)?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • The key characteristics have been identified.
  • The parts and/or processes have been evaluated for variation sensitive features.
  • A product / process risk analysis tool has been employed to identify key characteristics.
  • Part families have been identified as a step to move towards process control.
  • Key characteristics are analytically related to process parameters by means of correlation studies.
  • The data sets of such studies are stored in a database for further analysis and reporting.

Continuous Improvement Rules for Project Implementation

Valuable advice: First, identify the most important areas of implementation, and start with a low risk project, that builds confidence and success. Involve all key people and processes. (See more in Chapter 17.)

  • Continuous quality improvement projects create value for both the supplier, as well as the customer.
  • Make sure, that all involved, including the entire supply chain, support the improvement projects, by participating in procedure standardization, by providing accurate data, and by attempting to standardize on technology.
  • Well managed projects that incorporate the appropriate improvement tools are essential parts of every successful continuous improvement program.

Continuous Quality Improvement System Questions to Ask:

  • What improvement activities are in progress, or have been completed based on the initial requirements analysis of products and processes?
  • How are these activities related to your company's performance measures?
  • How are these activities related to the supply chain companies' performance measures?
  • Have you been able to demonstrate an improvement in your performance measures related to your improvement projects and activities?
  • Do you have a method, a process (such as our style eBooks, eLearning, presentations, and others) for documenting and communicating the purpose and results of each project?
  • What is your process for sharing the results, and disseminating new knowledge resulting from each project with other related cells / product lines / divisions / sites / supply chain companies / partners?

Evidence of Implementation Include the Following:

  • As a minimum, a list of ongoing and completed quality improvement projects.
  • Evidence that TQM / CI activities /projects positively impact performance measures.
  • Information that shows how the improvement activity was determined or identified.
  • Identified team members who understand the project objective and why it is important.
  • Project documentation and communication methods and tool that are being used by project managers.
  • Project documents and reports are consistently updated and accessed by multiple parties.

To summarize this section, remember, that a weak link, or process in any project might look small, nevertheless can bring a large project downto its knees... The best method to avoid this situation, is to reduce 'garbage in', so that 'garbage out' can be prevented...
