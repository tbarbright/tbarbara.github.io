Purpose

CE is a complex process because it reaches every corner and object of the globally distributed modern enterprise. Therefore CE processes, including design processes must be professionally modeled before any money is spent on a 'great idea'...

Process modeling, and dynamic simulation methods enable engineering management to understand the static and dynamic interaction of systems, and their components. The goal in CE, as always, is to reduce waste, to reduce the number of changes, and simultaneously improve quality and customer satisfaction.

In other words, these methods enable management to 'see the future', and to 'learn from the past', by means of analyzing data-sets, using statistical methods, and by means of data mining processes, using IT methods. The purpose of this chapter is to:

  • Offer an introduction and overview of CE / TQM related system process modeling methods,
  • Explain the reasons why we have to follow process modeling methods, and standards,
  • Illustrate the importance of this subject area with real-world and R&D challenges, and examples.

The 21st Century Undoubtedly Will be the 'Knowledge Driven Century'

The 21st Century undoubtedly will be the 'knowledge driven century', meaning that information that can be turned into value adding knowledge will represent more power than ever before. One of the key implementation vehicles for this is the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT); a subject for several other publications in Dr. Ranky's Technical Video and eBook Library series.)

Most of this new data will be gained directly from smart, networked sensors integrated into devices, such as automobiles, machines, processes, as well as from people operating these machines. The key here is to assure, that the data collected from various processes is accurate at source.

All of the results will then be integrated into feedback controlled, real-time total quality systems of cells, lines, and digital factories, that can be monitored, and controlled to stay within the pre-defined levels of variation.

In terms of the robust client-server architecture, these resources will be networked web-pages of secure, company intranets, and eventually the Internet, linking digital factories and systems (like service organizations) on a global basis.

The Internet helps to reduce waste, as well as acts as a systems integration and collaborator enabler for creating new ideas that can be turned into product, and then sold to a global economy, quicker, and at a lower cost than ever before.

In this new economy, leading companies are discovering that besides minimizing design and manufacturing costs and maximizing quality, they can achieve competitive advantage by introducing new, innovative products that satisfy individual consumers on a global basis.

This is possible because of the highly integrated collaborative opportunities of product and process design, information technology and management.

Gains of such 'hot new products' can not only increase the company's market share but it can create an entirely new market category, in which the company is first, therefore enjoys the efficiency gains by orders of magnitude.

Innovation in integrated design and manufacturing coupled with enterprise management systems contributes to further cost reductions and sustainable growth.

This is because Concurrent Engineering, Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) activities and software systems, in integration with Enterprise Resource Planning and Management (ERP&ERM) with Supply Chain Management (SCM) coupled with Customer Relationship Management (CRM), and dynamically adaptive Supply Chain Management Systems represent a very powerful methods base.

These systems can be used on a real-time basis to fine tune a business, continuously learn, innovate and satisfy customer needs, therefore sustain growth and make profit on a global basis.

The primary delivery mechanism for the above outlined system is e-business via extranets/ intranets and the Internet. At the beginning, the key value of e-business is cost savings, customer satisfaction and revenue growth.

As soon as the factory, or organization understands the benefits of web-enabled enterprise processes that share quality assured resources, applications and data, the opportunities for further waste reductions and growth are even more dramatic. This is the stage when the e-business gradually becomes a network of connected lean e-businesses of total quality managed design, manufacturing, remote quality control, maintenance, distribution, and other processes.

To summarize, the key is that by understanding market drivers based on mutually beneficial collaborative problem solving processes enables companies to move beyond traditional TQM, design, manufacturing and trading mechanisms to new and different ways of solving challenges and capturing new markets.

Those companies and individuals that will be able to capture the essence of this new 'knowledge driven', often real-time economy, and then take decisions, based on accurate, real-time quality controlled data sets gained from elementary processes up to high level activities, will be tomorrow's digital factory leaders.

The potential of the outlined systems is enormous, but works only if there is an

  • Underlying systems architecture model, and if the
  • Data-flow is real-time quality assured.

This is why we have to deal with system modeling, and process modeling in CE within a TQM context.

The ISO Quality Management Principles 4 and 5 Revisited: A Process and Systems Approach to CE and Design Management

As a reminder of the importance of our systems-wide, process-oriented thinking, this section re-visits two of the eight quality management principles on which the quality management system standards of the revised ISO 9000:2000 series are based. We do this for the purpose of developing TQM system modeling skills, using our object-oriented process modeling methods, CIMpgr.

(Note, that our discussion follows the guidelines, the spirit and the terminology, that the eight quality management principles define in the ISO 9001:2008 documents, entitled Quality Management Systems Fundamentals and Vocabulary, and in ISO 9004:2000, Quality Management Systems Guidelines for Performance Improvements).

ISO TQM Standard Principle 4: Process Approach: A desired result is achieved more efficiently when activities and related resources are managed as a process.

Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

  • Lower costs, and
  • Shorter cycle times can be achieved, through effective and controlled use of resources.
  • Improved, consistent and predictable results can be offered to both internal, as well as external customers.
  • Focused and prioritized improvement opportunities become reality, since each process step is identified and controlled (in a similar fashion to an object, with it's identifier, attributes, and behavior, when following object-oriented system modeling principles).
  • Activities are defined systematically, therefore the desired result can be obtained realistically.
  • Clear responsibility and accountability can be established for managing key activities in literally any organization.
  • The capability of key activities can be analyzed and measured
  • The interfaces and communication paths of key activities within, and between the functions (or in other words classes of objects) of the organization can be identified.
  • By focusing on factors such as resources, methods, and materials key activities of the organization can be improved.
  • By evaluating process failure risks, consequences and impacts of activities on (internal, as well as external) customers, suppliers and other interested parties becomes possible, and measurable.

ISO TQM Standard Principle 5: Systems Approach to Management: Identifying, understanding and managing interrelated processes as a system contributes to the organization's effectiveness and efficiency in achieving its objectives.

Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

  • Processes that will achieve the desired results will become better integrated and aligned
  • Management and process owners will have the ability to focus their efforts on the key processes
  • Since the consistency, effectiveness and the efficiency of the organization will grow, the confidence of interested parties in the organization will grow too
  • System structuring and tuning will become possible to achieve the organization's objectives in the most effective and efficient way
  • Understanding the interdependencies between the processes of the system will yield good results
  • Structured (and object/ component-oriented) modeling approaches that harmonize and integrate processes will become reality
  • The resistance created by cross-functional barriers will be reduced, providing a better understanding of the roles and responsibilities necessary for achieving common objectives
  • Organizational capabilities, and the establishment of resource constraints prior to action will be better understood by all involved (and mostly by all those who have created the models)
  • Targeting and defining how specific activities within a system should operate become reality
  • Continually improving the system through measurement and evaluation becomes possible, due to the analytical and quantifiable approach of the process models

    What is a Process?

    A process can be defined as the transformation of one or more inputs (data, raw materials, customer requests, etc.) into outputs (processed data, processed materials, satisfied customers: as a service, etc.)

    The transformation involves the addition or creation of value (i.e. we always must add value!), typically by processing data, improving on cycle time, reducing failure rate, increasing robustness, reducing cost, changing place, changing form, or shape, or others.

    Comment 1: Note, that our CIMpgr methodology refines the way we look at processes, in that it separates inputs, outputs, controls and resources, as well as offers layering for complex projects. (See these discussed below).

    Comment 2: Unfortunately many managers think that when quality engineers talk about process improvement, they only think about manufacturing, or assembly processes... not really! Any process can be improved, including design, manufacturing, marketing, legal, service, financial, administrative, and others. The opportunities for savings are huge in most cases.

    Benefits of Simultaneity with an Integrated Total Quality, Concurrent Engineering, and Lean production System Focus

    When integrating the total quality approach, with Concurrent Engineering and Lean Production systems, simultaneity, or concurrency, or parallelism (for the purpose of our discussions meaning exactly the same) compared to the classical, or sequential, or linear approach, offer striking benefits.

    As an example, imagine an assembly line with one and only one inspection station at the end, versus in-cycle, in-process, real-time sensory-based quality monitoring system testing and actively assuring product quality, making sure, that every product is defect free...

    The new concept in advanced factories, systems, and service organizations is clearly to simultaneously satisfy important requirements, such as

    • On-demand customer requirements,
    • Product / process functionality, that customers need / want / desire,
    • Reliability,
    • Produceability,
    • Marketability concerns,
    • Reduce the product development time (i.e. lead-time) and cost, by creating parallel, versus the traditional sequential processes,
    • Achieve higher product quality,
    • Added value, to beat competitors.

    The key to be able to achieve the above listed requirements, is that the factory, or organization, or service, must be analysed as a series of processes, including the requirements and the embedded risks of each of them, in integration. In practice, after system analysis (i.e. the 'as is system') comes system design (i.e. the 'to be system').

    The resulting new architecture model must take into account the various information, embedded into components, objects, and classes of objects.

    The first major step towards achieving the above outlined 'integrated whole' is the creation of a well designed, object -oriented TQM information system model, and as part of that, a process model. (Note, that this documentation effort pays off again, when a corporation applies for ISO9000:2000 certification).

    There are three vital points to recognize in this context:

    1. Design, including the product, process and the information system model design, is the first step in product manufacture ('product' meaning hardware as well as software)

    2. Every important design decision must be considered with great care at the earliest stage by a team of

    • System analysts,
    • System designers,
    • Integrated product and process designers,
    • Manufacturing engineering/ software engineering and
    • Marketing experts,
    • Hardware and software quality, and
    • Maintenance engineers, as well as the
    • Customers.

    3. Product design needs careful analysis and 'fine tuning' to take advantage of the available and skillfully applied design and analysis methods and technologies.

    To summarize, the fundamental reason for modeling a TQM system is to be able to analyze, design, test and validate a new, novel, unknown system, or improve a process, or several integrated processes for the purpose of further analysis and improvements by means of optimization.

    A Total Quality Information System Architecture and a Design Process Modeling Method

    In order to be able to design the CE model, and within this model the products and processes, we need to create an information system architecture, a framework as well as various user led processes and electronic tools that are going to enable communication across departmental, factory, and even continent lines.

    The basic approach we follow is to analyze and design objects, that have

    • Attributes that dictate the behavior of the objects,
    • Navigation between these objects, often meaning the information flow (and the material flow if these are physical objects),
    • Processes (that basically represent transfer functions that can turn the state of the object from one to another), in integration, rather than isolation.

    First let us list some methods, tools and technologies relating to data acquisition, that support the analysts to develop a TQM process, and information system model.

    Information flow related methods widely used in practice include the following:

    • Traditional flowcharts (good for sequential, mathematically well definable data/process/knowledge representation, e.g. the design of the front bearing system of an automobile, or a sequential financial transaction in a bank).
    • Data Flow Diagrams (DFDs are good: Example-1, Example-2, Example-3, Example-4, if applied in an OO, meaning object-oriented fashion, nevertheless don't give a detailed view of the attributes of the objects as CIMpgr; see below).
    • Structured Analysis and Design Technique, IDEFo (good if applied in an OO fashion, else can be confusing).
    • Object-oriented CIMpgr visual process models (included as templates in this eBook in the 'ProcModelTemplates' directory). This method is very good because CIMpgr explains the attributes of the objects, as well as the navigation scheme between objects within the same classes, and as a methodology integrates well with UML, Unified Modelling Language.
    • Object Oriented QFD (Quality Function Deployment), or OO_RA (Requirements Analysis, included in this eBook), applied to individual classes of objects, as well as to entire systems of objects (e.g. in the physical world an automobile, in the software world, a web-page).
    • Value Engineering (good, if the value adding capabilities of the objects of a system are analyzed).
    • Activity Based Costing (ABC) (can be extremely valuable, if coupled with CIMpgr process models, because processes can be costed, and the lowest cost path can be identified).
    • Dynamic modeling, including simulation (essential if dynamic behavior is important).
    • The Taguchi (DOE, Design of Experiments) method; ideal for designing statistically sound experiments, in particular if integrated with OO_RA (Object Oriented Requirements Analysis method, see our CORA tools, Component-oriented Requirements Analysis tools, and eBooks on the subject), when the value of the parameters of certain engineering solutions is not easy to be defined)

      How to Model Quality CE Systems and Processes with CIMpgr ?

      When modeling systems, static models are used for system definition. At this stage, CIMpgr models are object-oriented, static models.

      Static models become the frameworks around which to build the dynamic models which are used to simulate the system's operational performance.

      Dynamic models are used to identify behavior, or in other words, all the important situations the system can "get itself into" after a period of time in operation.

      As illustrated, when designing and drawing CIMpgr diagrams the following should be noted:

      1. Start by preparing the 'story'. This should be based on a sequence of events, that are typically mixed linear, and parallel, just like real life, or results of interviews told by others close to the processes effected. In this 'story' look for verbs, that indicate actions. These actions should then be turned into processes in your CIMpgr diagrams.
      2. Start with the identification of the major actions, or processes, described as objects and their processes in the CIMpgr diagrams. You can do this in a team by putting down a list of objects and their processes in any priority and then by organizing the list in priority order. Then identify the dataflow, classified into: Inputs, or Outputs, or Resources, or Controls entering and leaving the processes.
      3. Continue by creating the data sources and sinks. Keep in mind that these represent the model boundaries. (No model, or project is ever complete, full or "done"; we must accept this else we'll never finish...)
      4. Conclude with the data flow (the navigation path between objects), and the data stores. Data stores are data bases, or data repositories, where you can deposit data so that different processes can access them with some restrictions (as defined by the system designers)
      5. Finally keep in mind that you'll have to iterate a lot as you build such models. (Teamwork here is a must.) Also keep in mind that you want to keep these models as simple as possible and then, and only then, "dress them up" with detailed extensions.

      CIMpgr is a graphical method for 'modeling' a system

      A complete CIMpgr model is a set of diagrams which systematically illustrate the object-to-object relationships between the objects, their functions and entities.

      The diagrams are often arranged in a layered, tree-like fashion, illustrating a parent-child relationship, which together can be thought of as a collection of carefully coordinated objects, describing a system under investigation, or design (if it is a new system).

      Following the layered approach of decomposing/ identifying objects in a complex system, at the top of the tree there is a very high-level description of the entire system and further down the tree one goes, so the descriptions of the individual parts (objects) of the system become more detailed.

      For example, the parent diagram can describe a quality process in a machine shop as an activity which converts materials and an engineering drawing into the finished component by the use of engineering expertise and the appropriate production machine(s).

      Decomposing this, the child diagrams then can detail the information in the parent diagram by identifying the major activities involved in manufacturing the particular component, e.g. studying the engineering drawing, determining the manufacturing process(es) required and manufacturing the component. Thus the overall activity described in the top diagram is gradually detailed by the child diagram in this layered architecture.

      Similarly, each box (or object) on the child diagram can be described in greater detail by further child diagrams of its own. This process of showing greater and greater detail (until the required level of system description is reached) is called decomposition.

      It is important to mention, that if inheritance is on, attributes effecting objects in higher level classes (layers) get inherited by lower level child objects. This is a good feature because OO (object-oriented) visual programs, or object/ class diagrams (like CIMpgrs diagrams) don't become crowded, making them difficult to interpret.

      To summarize this section, making a complete CIMpgr model of a system is an iterative process.

      To understand this, consider the situation where an industrial system of some sort is being modeled:

      • The first iterations will be carried out by the model author as he / she learns more about the processes and the data flows of the system and reviews his own work, then the
      • Diagrams will be discussed and circulated around the relevant departments where readers will review them critically and judge how accurately their parts of the system have been modeled, and then
      • Reader criticisms will be fed back to the author and amendments made as appropriate; and this process gets
      • Repeated, as, and when the system changes.

      The CIMpgr Method - Structuring Concepts

      As with most enterprise modeling methods, CIMpgr follows the well established sequence of critical processes, including the following:

      1. Establish the strategy (and the behavior of objects that dominate your decision space).
      2. Analyze requirements (and the behavior of objects that dominate your requirements space).
      3. Analyze and review existing systems (i.e. their objects and the behavior of these objects).
      4. Design and investigate different options (of selecting objects, as well as navigation schemes between objects).
      5. After an iterative sequence, create a conceptual design. and then the appropriate design of the new system.
      6. Analyze the cost benefits (most importantly analyze "value added versus non-value added" processes and activities).
      7. Plan training.
      8. Possible future developments.
      9. Plan the implementation/ integration.
      10. Maintenance activities.

      CIMpgr - Tools & Technologies

      CIMpgr is an integrated system analysis, design (i.e. system modeling) methodology, relying on industrial consulting experience as well as on well-known (e.g. DFD and IDEFo) and new interfaced/ integrated object-oriented graphical system modeling tools, standards and technologies, such as UML (The Unified Modeling Language), that support the consultant / system analyst/ system designer in his, or her work.

      Note, that the first layer (i.e. the top layer) diagram always illustrates the basic, top layer CIMpgr object with the attributes of the object, grouped into Inputs, Outputs, Controls and Resources (I, O, C, R).

      The second layer diagram(s) typically explain more about "what is what". Note, that each process (or in other words object having behavior) can have not just inputs and outputs, as attributes, but also a clearer understanding of under what conditions does the process "fire", or in other words, become an active object and perform.

      The CIMpgr method represents an integrated enterprise model, that can focus on Total Quality, Concurrent Engineering, Lean Manufacturing / Assembly, Six Sigma, UML, and even Activity-Based Costing, in one integrated model, and for each of these focus points (or views) offer

      • Analysis,
      • Design,
      • Implementation methodology,
      • Business services,
      • Design, quality systems, manufacturing planning and the shop floor,
      • Information flow,
      • Processes,
      • Material flow (as appropriate),
      • Several existing and new tools,
      • Standards and technologies.

      In CE system models layering is crucial, since it explains the way different design teams work together. It shows responsibilities, who does what, with what inputs, outputs, under what constraints (e.g. emission standards), what output they produce and what are their tool (i.e. resource) needs. Very powerful, and a great opportunity to save design time, and reduce the number of changes.

      Note, that layering is always a powerful methodology, because it follows our natural human behavior in terms of 'digging deeper and deeper' as we explore the real-world processes we want to model for clarity and control. (Also note, that in IT terms, these layers represent objects in classes.)
