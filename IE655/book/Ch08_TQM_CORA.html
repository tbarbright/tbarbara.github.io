    Purpose

    The purpose of this Chapter is to provide an analytically established method to find out what customers want, need, and / or require, and to offer an introduction and overview of how this Concurrent Engineering design method fits into our TQM focused world.

    Furthermore, this Chapter illustrates further our novel approach to applying process modeling, requirements analysis, and risk analysis as an integrated approach to problem solving in the CE context.

      Customer Requirements Analysis Modeling

      Requirements, or customer needs analysis is considered to be one of the most important features of any design, R&D, manufacturing, service, or educational project, because if done professionally, it helps to specify and then research and develop the appropriate product / process design features and processes that sponsors/ customers need/ want.

      In this Chapter we focus on a CE/TQM-focused needs and requirements analysis method, that helps to understand customer requirements, including our associated internal and external 'customers' in quality organizations.

      Please note, that the subject of Requirements analysis (CORA, Component -oriented Requirements Analysis, and QFD, Quality Function Deployment) is featured in more detail in several eBooks in our Case Library.

      Our Methodology and the Implemented and Validated CORA Tool

      Component Oriented Requirements Analysis (CORA), represents a systematic, customizable method, based on QFD (Quality Function Deployment) and other methods, developed by Ranky to analyze product/ process, TQM / TQC needs, including product, process, IT, service, and other needs.

      As industrial experience has shown, the method can be applied to a wide range of subjects, including user interface requirements design for controllers, disassembly operator screens, and others, offering the analyst and the software engineering team accurate design specifications, that yield in software, that satisfies customer requirements within a competitive environment.

      Since approximately 85% of IT projects fail, because over 60% of them don't satisfy customer requirements the way the customers imagined them at the time of signing the contract, this, and other requirements analysis methods are important.

      CORA is a Team Oriented Problem Solving (TOPS), method, aimed at minimizing dissatisfaction, and financial loss due to inadequate requirements specification, and poor communication between customers and providers.

      In terms of our methods base, our solution gives an open source, web-enabled environment with a graphical interface and embedded 3D web-objects in a library, that run on literally any multimedia PC / Win, Mac, Linux, Solaris, UNIX, etc. computer in the world.

      Our CORA code is integrated in four modules, these being:

      • User requirements,
      • Engineering solutions,
      • Parameter calculations, and
      • Benchmarking.

      Following TQM practice, these modules interact via a correlation matrix that has values based on team assessment, and then via various algorithms offer the best possible optimization in terms of

      • How we should satisfy our customers' needs, 'wants', desires,
      • What the critical priorities are,
      • What the critical parameters are,
      • What are the actual values or ranges of values of these parameters,
      • How do others satisfy the same needs (i.e. how do our competitors, if any, satisfy our customer requirements),
      • What are the absolute and the relative importance values for each ranked requirement,
      • What are the critical actions (processes) we should take to satisfy our customers' requirements, and
      • Others.

      CORA is applied during the electronic support system planning stages of a product / process development project, as well as later for TQM / TQC and other purposes, and then updated on a regular basis to document changes in requirements and/or best practice engineering and management solutions.

      CORA is an iterative technique that promotes systematic engineering team thinking when a new process and/or product, or system design is developed. (Note, that the team relationship aspects of CORA can be clearly identified, using the Affinity Diagraming method, introduced in this publication in Chapter 10).

      The CORA Team's core mission is to study the customer and the future user base very carefully, and then extract, collect, analyze, and reason over customer requirements using the team's experience, documented with our spreadsheet-based tools.

      CORA is a Matrix-based Method, Similar to that of QFD (Quality Function Deployment)

      CORA is a matrix-based method, similar to that of QFD (Quality Function Deployment) practices, developed for user requirement need analysis.

      Note, that since the user requirements, their priority ratings, (at the left hand side of the CORA matrix), the selected engineering solutions (at the top of the matrix) and then the correlation values (1, or 3, or 9) linking requirements and engineering solutions in the actual matrix (middle section of the spreadsheet), we enter into a CORA spreadsheet, are on a relative scale, therefore should be determined by the local CORA Team, as well as by the local, customized standards they follow.

      The major challenge for a CORA Team when developing a case is to

      • Understand the requirements customers face; then,
      • See the solutions they have come up with, and then
      • Apply the learned problem solving skills to other, new challenges.

      As a generic, object-oriented problem solving method, as with all cases in our library of cases, the CORA Team should be committed to the following problem solving approach:

      • Analyze the needs and the requirements.
      • Analyze the actual TQM methods presented, find out the core methodologies, the mathematical models, the underlying engineering (and/or other) science foundation.
      • Analyze the TQM technologies involved. (How is science turned into a practical solution/ engineering and/or computing technology? Learn, that science and technology are integrated processes).
      • Analyze and review the actual processes and the way the process flow is integrated. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method from concept to product. To understand total quality and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us...)
      • Analyze potential failure modes, and alternative solutions.
      • Analyze the Total Quality Management aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service.
      • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of each process/ solution.
      • Design alternative TQM methods, processes based on what you have experienced/ seen, and learned.
      • Design an integrated TQM system, based on what you have analyzed.
      • Work in a multi-disciplinary TQM team and exchange ideas.
      • Understand the boundaries of TQM, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and developments by working on the cases presented. (Realize that in order to survive and win, you must add quality value).

      As hinted earlier, there are several key aspects of our generic methodology one should respect and truly understand, before beginning to work on a particular study.

      These being:

      1. Define scope, function and purpose

      Meaning, that the team should define the system or sub-system, or any product, process, or service system design to be analyzed. (When 'greening' a product, one should find a suitable candidate.)

      It is important to realize, that the scope and the function of CORA changes as it is applied at different levels and stages of the production system, or product design process, or other processes.

      Furthermore, they must agree on how far they want to go, when they analyze the selected product / process, or service.

      2. Identify user / customer requirements

      This means that the team should collect all realistic and accurate user requirements and enter them in the left hand side of our CORA spreadsheet.

      Some of the best knowledge elicitation methods include the following:

      • Interviews with social groups, potential customers, existing cutomers, maintenance engineers, marketing specialists, and others
      • Focus groups
      • Surveys... (but watch the accuracy carefully)
      • Reports and reviews (again, some reports are false!)
      • RP - Rapid Prototyping (i.e. screens, objects, devices, systems)
      • Observing users (e.g. video taping user actions, behaviors, what they do when the system fails, etc.)
      • Have engineers become users (The kitchen tool designer should also be a cook in a kitchen and use the same tools...)
      • Role playing (social interactions)
      • Competitive customer reviews (e.g. ask your customers to switch product and review for free)
      • Past cases (Returned products / maintenance feedback)
      • Rejection rate statistics with accurate causes
      • Kaisen teams (i.e. continuous improvement teams)
      • Ask high-end customers (because this is your core group)
      • Digital simulation (because it allows quick changes at very low cost...) 360 degree panoramas, 3D objects, animations are good examples here.
      • Social networking groups (understand the culture and the environment into which your product goes)
      • Sci-fi movies (yes, Hollywood is a great inventor!)
      • Genchi-gembutsu (get involved and stay involved with your customers; do not get arrogant and believe, that you know it all...)

      3. Prioritize potential requirements

      This activity means, that all requirements should be ranked depending on their importance, by the cross-functional team as early as possible.

      In order to achieve the highest level of customer satisfaction with the end product, this activity focuses on the key issues and provides a traceable path from the customer down to the most detailed processes, throughout each stage of the product development cycle.

      In order to quantify the above, correlation values are set in the CORA matrix: 1, meaning 'low level correlation', or 3, meaning 'medium', or 9, meaning 'high level correlation'. These values could be subject of discussion, therefore should be determined as part of a CORA Team activity.

      The Absolute Importance Rating (AIR) values are calculated on the basis of the requirement priority level, and the correlation values, therefore the team should quantify the level of importance, in terms of how an engineering solution responds to each specified customer / user need.

      This means that if the AIR value is high, the CORA Team should trace back the column of the matrix, and

      • First, at every correlation value of 9 (meaning 'high' correlation, or in other words 'very important correlation') check the customer requirement, and make sure that it is satisfied, since these are the most important customer requirements, then
      • Second, do the same at every correlation value of 3 (meaning, 'medium' level importance).

      The Absolute Importance Rating values are calculated for each column as follows: for each row, in which there is a requirement, and for each column where there is a correlation value of 1 (low), or 3 (medium), or 9 (high), the importance rating value is multiplied by the correlation value, then the result is added to the sum of the next row, until there are no more rows left.

      For each system or product / process design, CORA represents a dynamic, living document that needs to be kept up-to-date. It should not be retrospective, but a valuable source of information for current as well as for future products/ services.

      Keeping these rules in mind, we strongly recommend the team to document the user interface design needs, as they go along, and store the CORA document and information in a widely accessible format (preferably, both electronically, such as our spreadsheet and optionally on paper based media) for the current as well as for future CORA Teams.

      To summarize, our CORA Tool can be very useful to a variety of different audiences because it is presented at a very practical level. It is particularly useful for:

      • Product/ process designers, marketing managers, service managers, and others, who will find quality sources for quality decisions, in well developed CORA studies,
      • Best practice documentation of cases,
      • Clear graphical, as well as numerical warnings of risky operations, processes and services,
      • Customizable methods,
      • Source code (spreadsheets) for calculating disassembly costs, and documenting processes for Total Quality Certification, and
      • Disassembly process optimization,
      • Disassembly operators will enjoy the demonstrations and the documentation of disassembly processes,
      • The entire case will be used for education and training the workforce, using our 3D multimedia format,
      • eCommerce engineers will find great 3D files of harvested objects, they can put into their object-databases for marketing and resale,
      • Deliver presentations without additional effort,
      • Designers will study end-of-life disassembly processes and object/ component state for new designs,
      • Undergraduate and graduate students, as well as faculty will focus on
        • processes,
        • best practices,
        • cost analysis,
        • maintenance,
        • risk analysis, including decision, hardware, software, and service,
        • job safety and,
        • other practical aspects, as well as research opportunities.

      Furthermore, by employing our extended Taguchi methods, if needed, design of experiments (DOE) can be set up and loss functions can be calculated to refine absolute and relative importance ratings in our CORA matrices.

      This method minimizes the risk associated with adding engineering solution parameters to the matrix, that are not based on accurate user requirements, or scientifically justified data.

      Major CE/TQM Review Questions and Team Project Ideas for Engineering, Computing and Management Science/ Marketing Students

      You are most welcome to review this Chapter again, scroll up and down this page, as well as enjoy the media resources used to explain the challenges and solutions.

      Analyze your TQM needs by using our generic Requirements Analysis Spreadsheet Template to perform formal user requirements / needs analysis.

      What are the potential quality failure risks of the proposed processes? (Use our Process-oriented Failure Risk Analysis Spreadsheet Template to perform this task formally).

      Please make sure that you follow our proven assignment / project documentation guidelines) when you develop your reports / presentations.

      Design CE/TQM questions, that cover the discussed material, and evaluate your team's performance using our Evaluation Spreadsheet Template.

      If you are an engineering/ science student make sure you understand and document the following:

      • What are the analytical / mathematical/ material science aspects of the illustrated requirements analysis challenges and solutions in the CE/TQM / TQC context? Upon what scientific principles do they operate?
      • What are the major CE/TQM processes that make the machines / services operate as illustrated? (Create an object-oriented process model showing how objects / modules interact with each other).
      • How are these processes integrated and controlled from a quality perspective? What are the process parameters and the limitations? How can you measure the process parameters, and then total quality control them?
      • What are the major CE/TQM application areas of the machines/ processes discussed in this case?
      • How do you see the future of quality designs, from a concurrent product/ process, modular, reusable-component design point of view? In other words: As a CE/TQM manager in charge, how would you assure, that quality is designed into the product / process / service?

      If you are a computer science/ computer engineering student focus on the quality aspects of software of the machines and/ or processes / services illustrated.

      • On the basis of object oriented process models try to develop a software architecture that could make the illustrated devices / processes work without any crashes, and software bugs.
      • Try to create the object/ class diagram of a digital communication-enabled product, linked to a digital service model, controlling and operating devices (on-site, or remotely) following CE/TQM principles.
      • Try to come up with CE/TQM product / service operation control algorithms and document them in pseudo-code, or by using a programming language, such as C++ or Java.
      • Create user quality interface designs, networking (intranet/ Internet) and test procedures for your software project. Using our spreadsheet templates, show how customer satisfaction quality can be assured.
      • How could you turn networked machines / processes / services into web-pages and work with them locally and remotely for the purpose of monitoring their quality behavior? What are the benefits of having machines / processes / services networked in a CE/TQM client-server architecture? Think about this idea and model different client-server solutions and applications (e.g. remote operation control, remote maintenance, remote quality control, and others).
      • What operating system would you chose for satisfying CE/TQM requirements, considering the fact, that robustness, and fail-safe operation is essential? (No time to reboot when running at 65 miles per hour...). Please explain why, considering quality, development, cost, run-time, maintenance, and other issues.
      • How do you see the future of quality software from a computer science and software design point of view? (How would you increase the quality of software? How would you be able to justify such development costs?

      		 If you are a management science/ marketing student focus on how the illustrated requirements analysis methods, and solutions in terms of machines / processes / services, should be...
      • Marketed/ advertised nationally as well as internationally over the Internet following CE/TQM principles, PLM, Virtual Product Demos and other ideas, and by other means (e.g. quality trade shows and special events),

      • Furthermore, create a spreadsheet that helps potential customers to cost justify the purchase, lease, and other options of the above CE/TQM solutions and services with all necessary 'what-ifs' that individual salesmen can customize and present professionally at meetings or over the web.
      • Based on our Case Based Marketing methods and ideas, try to come up with a business plan, and an engineering management project plan that can be customized for each customer interested in purchasing, leasing, etc. the illustrated machines / processes / services.
      • How would you offer your services as a CE/TQM sales consultant? Create a business plan and marketing plan for this idea.
      • How would you apply CE/TQM devices for security / safety applications? On what basis would you sell such application opportunities? Develop a business plan.
      • How would you approach designers, and insurance companies with CE/TQM options in modern automobiles?
      • Based on the current culture of the organization, create a CE/TQM plan and a presentation that evaluates and justifies the above illustrated processes and the way they could be integrated into an integrated quality product/ process design strategy for a small, a medium and a large organization/ company, that specializes retrofitting existing products / services with various levels of CE/TQM solutions.
      • Create a CE/TQM transition plan, that includes educational as well as e-commerce aspects of the illustrated machines and processes (e.g. for selling the machines over the Internet, or selling services by sharing them with networked customers over the web, and others).
      • How do you see the future of CE/TQM from a management and marketing point of view? Would this be a cost effective solution? How about reliability, and quality of service? Present a strategy to the board.
      • Gap analysis: Conduct a marketing study that could lead to the identification of quality gaps in a product / service, or process. What is the need that is not fulfilled at present? Discuss these ideas with your engineering and computing science team members, as well as selected, potential customers. (Try to set up your own business and implement your ideas!)
