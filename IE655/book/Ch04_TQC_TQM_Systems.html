    Purpose

    The purpose of this Chapter is to offer an introduction to the complex architecture of CAD (Computer Aided Design), and CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing) and Computer Aided Engineering (CAE) systems in general, and some quality design standards, that CE Teams should follow.

    As we have discused earlier, the fundamental goal of the Concurrent Engineering design process is to create a new product easier, more efficiently, and resource effectively, and at a lower cost, than with traditional methods.

    By using CAD/CAM/CAE and other simulation and analysis software tools to speed conception design and simulation, to plan and validate machining, inspection, assembly, maintenance, and other strategies, the entire cycle becomes more streamlined.

    The specific options of different software choices will vary the possibilities somewhat, but as a whole nowadays if the process can be done in the real world, it can be simulated in software, and even more, in software designers can drive systems over their limits, and experience the consequences.

    Depending on the piece or assembly being created, the graphic and rendering requirements can be quite high, which is why most CAD/CAM software uses powerful networked workstation hardware and software, with plenty of memory and storage included.

    (Please note, that in our discussion we'll focus on generic system aspects versus technology, that changes rapidly.)

    What is CAD?

    Computer Aided Design tools are any software solution that replace traditional hand drafting practices with 3D part modeling to create, improve, and/or analyze an engineering design / document. 

    The basic idea of working in the environment is to use 2D sketching and drafting principles, then extruded or intruded into a 3D volume via a plane in a virtual environment.

    The primary four functions of CAD are as follows:

    • To elevate productivity of the designer
    • To elevate the standard of the design
    • To elevate quality of the design documentation
    • To develop a manufacturing database

     

    What is CAM?

    Computer Aided Manufacturing software allows the user to test an entire manufacturing process of a product from start to finish. CAM packages often include assembly planning, robot programming, quality / test-programming and other analysis and simulation tools.

    In recent years they deploy powerful graphics simulation tools in photorealistic 3D, and with many interactive features. These features make them easier to use, as well as offer valuable insight to robot collision detection, or setup and fixturing issues, and others.

    CAM systems use a database of available machines and machining processes to plan the entire production cycle, optimize it, then simulate it before a single part is even clamped into a CNC machine, or robot work table. CAM systems:

    • Simulate machining and other process cycles.
    • Optimize machining cycles by order of process, choice of machine, cost of production, surface finish, quality requirements, and others.
    • Safely explore and experiment with limitations of available machines, tools, clamping and fixturing devices, and materials, using powerful simulation.

    CAD/CAM streamlines design and simulation of manufacturing. Errors become apparent sooner, sharing of information is much faster and more accurate because electronic drawings in vector formats are being shared rather than physical paper sketches and schematics.

    Also, since CAD files can be exchanged between workstations, human errors of mislabeling or forgetting a dimension on the schematic are no longer as severe. If the CAD drawing is sent, the new user can inquire about any dimension they like.

    With the introduction of CAD systems, engineers were able to demonstrate concepts to teammates and clients that were completely real and accurate, yet without the cost and resources necessary to rapid prototype or machine real-parts.

    CAD System Architecture Overview

    The following is the functional outline of a generic CAD software:

    • Geometric Modeling
      • Models exist as wire frame models, surfaces with texture, or a combination, or solid
      • Models may be animated and cross-sectioned
      • User may perform View options on the model (ex. Pan, zoom, move)
      • Models exist in 2D or 3D environment
    • Engineering Testing
      • Thermal, electrical, dynamic load, impact analysis, and others
      • Physical properties (mass, inertia, volume)
      • Kinematical testing of range of motion
      • Clearance, interference and dimensional tolerance testing
      • FEA testing to analyze stresses
    • Design Appraisal & Assessment
      • Automated Dimensioning
      • Modeling of discrete forces
      • Testing of potential problems
      • Virtual prototyping and machining
    • Automated Drafting and Visualization/ Animation
      • Schematics can be created efficiently and accurately

    Groups Who Use CAD/CAM

    Nowadays, the range of people who use CAD systems is as diverse as the applications of these engineering aids, and can be listed in one of four categories.

    Also keep in mind that the number of people working on such a team can also vary greatly, from a single individual in their home, hoping to finish a patent, to multiple department in an international corporation, working together via virtual collaborative software.

    Industrial Design / Marketing

    This group is mostly artists and conception designers, a group whose highest priority is the shape, form, and aesthetic quality of a product. When presenting a product to a client or market, the ‘look’ will in many cases make or break the buyer’s decision, rather than the inner technical workings.

    The qualities of a CAD package which they look for the most would be features like “Photo-realistic Rendering, Surface and Texture generation engines”, and “Intuitive shape and form sculpting”.

    Engineering Design

    The engineering teams behind a product don’t just design the layout of the inner workings, but conduct the testing of the product before it can comply with department, company, or government regulations.

    At first CAD options for this group were limited to 3D part design and assembly integration, but more modern software also has integrated analytical options including thermal and electrical conductivity testing, dynamic impact and vibration testing, and finite element analysis.

    Manufacturing Systems Engineers, and Engineering Technology Engineers

    CAD is a useful tool for this group, but CAM will more often take the spotlight. The greatest looking part in the world will have no use if there are physically no surfaces on which to clamp it into a CNC, or if the part just technically isn’t possible to create.

    Design engineers should understand machining processes and ‘Design to Manufacture’ but the truth is that in the real world a lot of time is wasted by this group sending the CAD models back because the Design team didn’t test the machining cycle.

    With a CAM system the part machining cycle can be tested, planned, pre-programmed, and experimented on.

    The following are just three examples of questions that CAM systems can answer:

    • Can we use another machine for this process?
    • Will is damage the machine/bit/piece to work on the part this way?
    • What machines do we need for this cycle?

    Quality Management / Maintenance

    Total Quality Management uses CAD systems to double-check the design engineers, as well as examine options for customization and maintenance. Using CAD the knock-on effects for design alterations, as well as added cost and increased time to product can be visualized.

    Management can use facts that compare structural analysis and added cost per option to make decisions, rather than just an educated guess.

    The next design step can be planned out downstream by simulation, and custom options (ex. Signature of a client on custom 18 inch rims) can be analyzed for the same stresses and machining processes as any other part in the assembly.

    Training

    As part of modern engineering practices, knowing how to use at least one CAD system is crucial to successfully co-operating with a design team. But even for someone who is not directly related to that part of the product design, understanding what the advantages and limitations of the company’s CAD/CAM system is important.

    CAD/CAM Examples

    This section outlines two examples of how CAD/CAM systems have improved current processes and avoided possible problems by simulation before implementation of the original design.

    Sheet Metal Plasma Cutting

    Inefficient use of resources brings overall productivity down. By not properly planning the cutting patterns and order, high levels of scrap increase material costs and time to production with manually repetitive tasks.

    Orientation of the parts to be cut, and smart nesting organization can save on material cost by utilizing maximum sheet area, reduce scrap, and shorten lead times.

    Multi-Surface Milling

    Errors during prismatic and multi-surface milling are some of the more expensive manufacturing problems to repair. Damaged tips, bits, and grinding heads increase manufacturing costs along with poorly planned machining process modeling.

    By efficiently checking the order of operations and simulating the possibilities the manufacturing engineers can establish the best process order while also pre-programming the machines themselves to save time.

    CAD/CAM Software Examples

    ProE (Parametric Technologies Corporation, USA)

    PTC was started in 1985, out of ComputerVision. At the time of writing, there are over 33,000 customers in over 39 countries each year.

    One of the top 10 software enterprises in the world, ProE is only the base package in a series of options that can cover all parts of the engineering concept, design, and testing process. PTC offers add-ons to ProE like ProMechanica, a stress analysis and animation software, as well as thermal and electrical analysis and ventilation design and layout just to name a few.

    Minimum system requirements:

    • 2 Gigabytes of memory
    • 500 MHz CPU speed, or faster
    • 256 megabytes of RAM
    • 1024 x 768 video resolution with 16 bit color

     

    Catia (Dassault Systems)

    Catia V5 offers options ranging from CNC machining simulation to styling and systems engineering.

    Dassault systems is an internationally proven software provider, many of their own studios and facilities using their software. Aerospace, defense, and automotive industries are just three that use Catia as their primary design software.

    An intuitive interface, and artistic styling features make learning Catia relatively easy.

    Minimum system requirements:

    • 2 Gigabytes of memory
    • 256 Megabytes of RAM
    • 1024 x 768 video resolution with 24 bit color
    • Needs hardware texturing capability included

    Catia has software options for the entire start-to-finish product life process – from conceptualizing a design, to planning for downstream applications.

    With access to a single common database, managing multiple solutions at different release levels and translating data from one application to another is a trouble-free.

    AutoDesk started in the early 1980s with the original version of AutoCAD.

    AutoCAD is one of the most popular CAD software choices because it has had the benefit of a lot of experience.

    CAD/CAM Total Quality Control Subsystems

    Quality should be designed into the product and maintained throughout its manufacturing and total life cycle, including maintenance.

    In order to be able to implement a smoothly functioning Total Quality System, we need an infrastructure, called the Quality System, built of integrated sub-systems.

    A quality sub-system can be built of the following modules (or classes of objects, in IT terms):

    1. Purchase control includes

    • Obtaining the correct materials of defined quality from reliable suppliers and the proper storage of these materials to prevent deterioration of quality. (These are essential elements of uniform quality manufacture).
    • Where necessary, simple checks and periodic random inspections of these supplies will help to achieve the necessary results. Guided by the ISO900x standards, certified test reports from suppliers are often necessary.

    2. Process control of quality can be achieved by

    • Considering all essential areas of the operations. The degree of
    • Attention and the extent of controls applied to each area depends on the size and nature of the production process.
    • As an example, consider that CNC controllers with in-cycle gauging systems provide instant feedback on product quality in an electronic format. This enables operators and/or automated systems to take action before poor quality parts are produced. (Note, the principle of prevention, versus post-inspection).

    3. Control of manufacturing information includes written (and perhaps electronically, via Email, distributed), as well as oral instructions for all employees.

    • It involves a system for reproducing, distributing, displaying, changing and checking the information provided.
    • Poor copies of instructions, drawings, or specifications often result in poor workmanship.
    • Since the incomplete distribution of instructions results in confusion among operators and supervisors, often causing process or operation errors, a factory/ organization-wide electronic information system has a strategic benefit - assuming that it is used properly.

    4. Control of Operators. The careful selection, training, instruction and motivation of employees are essential to control quality.

    • Specification of skill levels, physical and mental characteristics of operators is a necessary prerequisite for selecting and training employees for a quality control related job.
    • Continued attention, assistance, instruction, and motivation after the learning curve has been spanned are also important.
    • Quality circle activities and other participative management concepts are considered to be useful procedures for quality improvement and control via operators.

    5. Control of Process Steps and Process Capability. The establishment of control procedures and techniques at critical process steps on important product, process, operator, or environmental characteristics are essential for the control of quality.

    • This includes preventive and corrective maintenance of equipment and production facilities, as well as automated and manually operated tests and controls.
    • Specification limits and process capability must be based on the analytical assessment of various resources (e.g. machine tools, assembly robots, Co-ordinate Measuring Machines and others).

    6. Control of finished product is an essential step in overall process control and it means some verification, that the finished product or service meets the expected quality by using adequate tests and criteria.

    • Feedback of information from final inspection is also important in maintaining the process.
    • Control charts, sampling plans and automatic test equipment are useful in terms of both recording accurate data and traceability.

    7. Control of measuring instruments and test equipment.

    • Where applicable, calibrations and periodic maintenance of test and process equipment for adequate control are essential.
    • Error of measurement studies are often used to prove-in an instrument and to monitor its accuracy and precision level during use.

    8. Control of corrective action.

    The systematic use of information from each of the

    • Control areas enumerated above is necessary to assure that deficiencies affecting quality are detected and corrected promptly, and that
    • Measures are taken to prevent recurrences.

    Quality Systems for Concurrent Engineering

    It is a well known fact, that in hi-tech industries, such as the automobile, the computing, the aerospace, and other industries, accreditation to the ISO 9001:2008 standard is a major factor in placing and/or receiving orders.

    In order to achieve ISO 9001:2008 accreditation it is necessary to document and maintain the Quality System and to provide evidence of compliance by completing the appropriate records.

    A large amount of work, paperwork, computing and bureaucracy are involved in this activity, and the accuracy of information must be high.

    To reduce the amount of work for accreditation, as well as to integrate and maintain the results, the Quality Information System, should rely on the networking and distributed database / knowledge base infrastructure of the organization.

  • To illustrate what does a typical Quality Information System include and integrate, the most important file types are listed. (Note, that these files could be valuable information to both newcomers as an introduction, as well as to experts as a checklist.)

    • The Quality Manual, describing the quality policy, the organizational structure and the responsibilities of the employees.
    • The Operational Procedures, describing the core working instructions and procedures for quality control purposes. (Note, that measurable outcomes are of utmost interest).

    The Operational Procedures point to the

    • Project Documents, and the
    • Control Files.

    These typically include the following information types:

    The Project Quality Plan. This is a project specific management book, containing records of formal reviews and quality control audits as follows:

    • Client's brief, general program, and phasing
    • Work activity program
    • List of external organizations and communication requirements
    • Project staff and responsibilities
    • Operational procedures
    • Key design input data summary
    • Project submissions/approval register
    • Records of quality system design, reviews, checks and quality control audits
    • Records of any system quality audits
    • Brief change and design amendment register
    • Quality records and archive storage requirements, etc.

    Project Control Files typically include the following:

    • Reports, drawings, drawings register and outgoing/ incoming issue slips
    • Tender documents and record prints
    • Calculations, specifications and schedules
    • Quality audit reports and corrective action reports
    • Project feedback reports
    • Contract documents, and others

    Typical Project Administration Files include the following:

    • Client correspondence
    • Design team correspondence
    • Design team meeting minutes
    • Construction suppliers / subcontractors
    • Main contractors
    • Resident engineer / clerk of works site visit records
    • Instructions given by the architects, and others

    Controlled Reference Documents typically include files of internal and external origin.

    Controlled Reference Documents of Internal Origin typically include the following:

    • Standard specifications
    • Design office memoranda
    • Computer programs
    • Approved visits of consultants, contractors and subcontractors, etc.

    Controlled Reference Documents of External Origin typically include the following:

    • Building regulations
    • National (or equivalent) standards and codes of practice (e.g. ANSI, or BS, or DIN)
    • Recognized national specifications
    • Computer programs, and others

    Typical Uncontrolled Reference Documents include the following:

    • Technical publications, trade literature, journals and magazines, publications of professional bodies, etc.
    • Trade association and advisory bodies' publications.
    • Statutory regulations
    • Clients' standard specifications
    • Project report, site investigation documents and archives
    • Preferred non-mandatory details and guidance notes
    • Press cuttings
    • Company brochures, etc.

    The Elements of Quality Systems and Standards

    The purpose of this summary is to give the scope of the quality standards and to provide a better understanding of their specifications and to assist in their use, either when implementing or evaluating a Total Quality System.

    For the purpose of consistency we emphasize that the word 'requirements', means either

    • Requirements prescribed by the purchaser and agreed by the supplier in a contract for products and services, or the
    • Requirements prescribed by the supplier which are perceived as satisfying a market need.

    The "product, or service" is defined by the quality standards as the output that an organization supplies to a user.

    We discuss now the key elements and areas of responsibilities of quality standards, structured as follows:

    1. Management Responsibility

    • There must be a quality policy, meaning that the supplier of the product or service should develop and define its total policy, business objectives and commitment in a recorded statement, and also that all employees have to be trained throughout the organization.
    • To achieve the policy objectives, one needs responsibility and authority, as part of a structured organization with established levels of control and inter-relationships, covering areas such as: control and maintenance of the quality system, control of corrective actions, control of management reviews, etc.
    • Within the organization, management needs to verify that the product(s) or service(s) are delivered in accordance with the quality objectives. In order to achieve verification (i.e. inspection, testing of products, checking the outputs of services, etc.), design reviews and quality audits have to be performed.
    • The organization has to appoint a representative who is responsible for the quality system. (If this person has other authorities, there should be no conflict of interest between different jobs).
    • The concept of management review within the Quality System includes regular reviews of the documented policies and procedures, as well as the review of internal audits, to ensure that the defined quality system is implemented and followed as required.

    2. Quality System

    • Total Quality System organization. The quality system of a world class design / manufacturing / service enterprise must be part of the overall system, often referred to as the 'digital factory', or 'digital organization' (or even 'digital government' ). In other words the Quality System is part of the Total Management System. (Think of shared, distributed databases, computer networks, report generators, data logging devices, CNC controllers sending quality information to cell controllers, etc.)
    • The Quality Plan identifies those key elements in the product or service necessary to provide fitness for purpose and the means by which they are to be measured.
    • Quality Manual. The extent and detailed content of the desirable Quality Manual is documented in the Standard. The Quality Manual is basically a description of the way the organization conducts its business. Each Quality Manual format is unique to the company, just as the quality system is developed with the products and/or services in mind.

    3. Contract Review

    • In cases where a documented contract has to be established between the supplier and the customer, key steps should be taken to include items such as
    • ensuring that the requirements are clearly understood, agreed and recorded by both parties, that
    • all discussions are documented, and that
    • Both parties go through a defined process to ensure that they have the necessary resources and facilities to conform to all the requirements in the contract.

    4. Design Control

    • Design control means, that the supplier's design function should be capable of taking the customer's needs and translating these, in a systematic and controlled way, into a specification which defines a product or a service.
    • This specification should be such, that the product or service is producable, verifiable and controllable under the proposed production, installation, commissioning or operational conditions.

    5. Document Control

    All quality documentation should be controlled through a formal and defined system that indicates the format of the documentation, how and by whom it is reviewed and approved prior to issue and the method of its review and updating.

    6. Purchasing

    • In the procurement of material or external services, whether for inclusion in the product or services or in support of activities which form part of the supplier's quality system, e.g. calibration services, or specialist inspection or test activities, etc., the supplier should ensure that such material or service is of the quality specified and that conformity can be assured.
    • This activity includes the assessment of subcontractors and the formal definition of purchasing data and dataflow (i.e. clear specification of orders, changes of orders, logistic procedures and partners, their quality assurance documents and procedures, and others).

    7. Purchaser Supplied Product

    • When materials commonly described as 'free issue' are provided by the customer to the supplier, the onus of their conformity to specified requirements is that of the customer.
    • However, the supplier should not knowingly incorporate nonconforming parts into the production or service supplied to the customer.

    8. Product Identification and Traceability

    • Good product quality means traceability. Where appropriate, this may include production processes, stages, inspection and test results, etc.
    • If a problem arises at any stage of production or use, traceability is important so that problems can be analyzed at their root and preventive corrective action can eventually be taken.

    9. Process Control

    Control means careful planning of performance standards and process limits. Features, that closely affect the quality of the product or service can be identified from the quality plan, where this exists.

    Special consideration should be given to the following aspects

    • Accuracy and variability of equipment, including settings and adjustments and their frequent verification, the
    • Skill, capability and knowledge of operators to conform to quality requirements,
    • Special, environmental issues and constraints, such as temperature, radiation, humidity, and others, the
    • Certification records maintained for personnel, process and equipment, as appropriate.

    10. Inspection and Testing

    The Quality plan or documented procedures should include the requirements for inspection and testing and release of product or service. It should cover inspection and testing of:

    • Subcontracted or purchased items or services (i.e. receiving inspection or test),
    • Manufactured items, or services, and
    • Products prior to final release to the customer.

    11. Inspection, Measuring and Test Equipment

    In this section the standard specifies that, for any product or service, the equipment used to check the conformity to specified and end-user requirements during or after the production phase needs control and calibration

    12. Inspection and Test Status

    The standard specifies that, whatever method is chosen, it should be possible to establish quickly and clearly the inspection status of products or services at any stage and, in particular, to identify those which do not conform to specified requirements.

    Furthermore, inspection and test records should identify the inspection authority or individual responsible for verifying the conformance to specification of products, at whatever point in the production process the inspection and testing take place.

    This allows traceability and further analysis.

    13. Control of Non-conforming Product

    As production or service delivery processes may yield suspect or defective items, it is necessary to have methods for preventing further processing, delivery or installation of such items.

    The method of identifying a non-conforming item depends on the nature of the product or the service. In cases such as this, records should be kept and repaired or reworked items reinspected.

    14. Corrective Action

    The Quality System should contain a defined method to eliminate the causes of nonconforming product or service by initiating appropriate corrective action, designed to remove the root of the problem.

    Two different cases are appropriate, as follows:

    • Immediate corrective action should be taken, meaning direct actions taken to resolve the causes of nonconforming products or services
    • A long term corrective action plan should be implemented, and records kept and analyzed on a regular basis relating to the performance of products or services, including manufacturing, customer complaints, etc.

    15. Handling, Storage, Packing and Delivery of Materials

    The handling of materials during the manufacture of a product or the performance of a service should be properly planned, and controls should be set up to ensure that the plans can be monitored.

    The handling and storage of materials should be properly planned to avoid damage due to vibration, shock, corrosion, temperature or any other conditions affecting the quality of the product.

    16. Quality Records

    Quality records represent the objective evidence of the operation of a quality system at all its stages; they therefore have to be:

    • Readily available,
    • Clearly identified as to the product or service applicable,
    • Indexed, and
    • Readily retrievable, and stored properly.

    17. Internal Quality Audits

    In order to ensure that the defined quality system is being operated correctly and effectively, it is necessary to carry out planned and documented checks on the implementation and operation of the system.

    These checks should record the following:

    • Deficiencies found, the
    • Corrective action required, its time scale and the
    • Person responsible for carrying out the corrective action, and
    • Others.

    18. Training

    Specific training to perform assigned tasks, and general training to heighten quality awareness and to mould attitudes of all personnel in an organization is central to the achievement to quality.

    19. Servicing

    This requirement of the standard applies mainly to manufactured products which are designed so that their durability depends on regular maintenance in certain specific areas.

    Such maintenance is often referred to as "after sales service".

    20. Statistical Techniques

    Statistical methods represent powerful tools when used correctly within the quality process.

    They should be selected with care and applied to one or more of the following areas:

    • Market analysis,
    • Customer Requirements Analysis,
    • Product design, reliability specification and durability prediction,
    • Process control/process capability studies,
    • Determination of quality levels and inspection plans,
    • Data analysis, and
    • Others.
