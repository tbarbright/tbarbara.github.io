    Purpose

    The purpose of this chapter is to offer an introduction to globalization and outsourcing with special emphasis on CE and TQM (Total Quality Management with lean six-sigma quality methods and tools).

    The term 'globalization' reflects technological advances, that have made it easier and quicker to complete international trade and financial transactions.

    It refers to an extension beyond national borders of the same market forces that have operated for centuries at all levels of human economic activity, in village markets, in urban industries, and financial centers.

    Globalization is a natural human issue, and therefore should be supported by all, as long as the game is honest, and fair. Unfortunately in some cases it is not...

    Furthermore, we attempt to shed light on some of the reasons why

    • Quality design and TQM standards should be followed in the globalization processes, at all stages,
    • Why the cultural foundation is important to understand when designing and manufacturing products, processs (including service related processes) for a global market, and
    • Illustrate the importance of this subject area with real-world and R&D challenges, and cases.

    What is Globalization?

    Product, process and service system globalization can be defined in many different ways.

    The International Monetary Fund defines globalization as 'a process, that is beneficial, a key to future world economic development, and also inevitable and irreversible'.

    The term sometimes also refers to the movement of people (labor) and knowledge (technology) across international borders. There are also broader cultural, political and environmental dimensions of globalization.

    Others regard globalization with hostility, even fear, believing that it increases inequality within and between nations, threatens employment and living standards, lowers quality of goods and services, and thwarts social progress.

    The term 'globalization' has come into common usage since the 1980s, reflecting technological advances that have made it easier and quicker to complete international trade and financial transactions.

    It refers to an extension beyond national borders of the same market forces that have operated for centuries at all levels of human economic activity, in village markets, in urban industries, and financial centers.

    The International Monetary Fund claims, that '... globalization offers extensive opportunities for truly worldwide development but it is not progressing evenly'.

    It also notes, that some countries are becoming integrated into the global economy more quickly than others. Countries that have been able to integrate are seeing faster growth, and reduced poverty (although the new wealth created because of globalization efforts is more than often spread unevenly).

    Outward-oriented policies, the potential market size brought dynamism and greater prosperity to much of East Asia, transforming it from one of the poorest areas of the world 40 years ago.

    As living standards rose, it became possible to make some progress on democracy and economic issues. Unfortunately due to rapid growth, and other sociopolitical and financial issues the environment and work standards often do not keep up with the advancement of technology.

    This creates a situation, in which competition becomes often unfair, in that the West becomes a less attractive investment game, since it is bound to its democratic systems, its expensive, but relatively good quality health care, its environmental protection, minimum wage, and other labor rights, and social justice laws.

    Typically, this imbalance is overcome by productivity gains, by automation, by progressive innovation, and better quality, nevertheless often the differences are far too high, as well as the attraction of being local to a massive number of people who's earning and spending power is growing, therefore the game is often lost. But there are some changing signs too... An important reshoring trend is triggered by the low energy cost in the USA (due to natural gas sources), IP theft and protection issues overseas (Intellectual Property), increasing labor cost overseas, as well as very rapidly growing automation and productivity gains in the US by means of automation (as mentioned already above). As an example, when you have robots that can perform over 280 operations in less than a second, human labor cannot cope with that... This clearly indicates, that human labor should be increasingly deployed as thinkers, versus robot competitors... and there is nothing wrong about this!

    Globalization is a fundamental human design, therefore should be supported by all, as long as the game is fair... again, the game MUST be fair...

    By contrast to globalization, in the 1970s and 1980s when many countries in Latin America and Africa pursued inward-oriented policies, their economies stagnated or declined, poverty increased and high inflation became the norm.

    In many cases, especially in Africa, adverse external developments made the problems worse. As these regions changed their policies, their incomes have begun to rise.

    The good news is, that besides greed and monopolistic, and feudalistic politics controlling industries and nations in many parts of the world, an important transformation is underway.

    Encouraging this trend by nations helping each other to learn how to create wealth, not reversing it by policies, that make them more and more dependent on monopolies run by a small number of greedy people, is the best course for promoting growth, development and poverty reduction.

    As an example, a recent study performed by New Jersey (USA) science students indicates, that by teaching farmers in Africa how to separate the fertile seeds from the non-fertile ones typically yields a five-fold (!) increase of the harvest, without using any fertilizers, or genetically modified seeds / food, or any other chemicals added to the process.

    Amazingly to achieve this, a family of five or six should spend 2 days of work per year to perform this sorting process to gain 5x growth in output. These are amazing ideas, methods and results, and clearly underline, that the key is to help others, and to help each other to learn how to create new wealth, rather than just giving wealth, that is then typically wasted in the streams of corruption...

    The crises in the emerging markets in the 1990s have made it quite evident that the opportunities of globalization do not come without risks. Such risks can arise due to one, or more of the following:

    • Volatile capital movements,
    • Risks of social,
    • Economic,
    • Environmental degradation often created by the greed of the investors, and developers, and by poverty.

    To summarize this section, this should not be a reason to reverse direction, but for all concerned investors should employ ethical, TQM - driven, long - term sustainable investment policies, encourage and embrace policy changes to build strong economies and a stronger world financial system that will produce more rapid growth and ensure that poverty is reduced, and quality of life is improved everywhere in the world.

    This is because everybody on planet Earth should have the right to free quality life.

    This can only be done, if powerful nations stick together and follow sound environmental protection, labor rights protection, and other democratic / ethical human rights protection policies, that do not allow greedy investors, and local politicians who often support such activities for various financial and other gains, installing factories in third world countries, that have unacceptable pollution levels, or as an example make cars, that have unacceptable emission levels in factory environments, that cause lung cancer, and other long term, often fatal illnesses.

    Does Globalization Increase Poverty and Inequality, and Decrease Quality? Some Important Statistics About Globalization

    According to the International Monetary Fund, during the 20th century, global average per capita income rose strongly (GDP, or Gross Domestic Product, increased almost five-fold), but with considerable variation and imbalance among countries.

    The average income growth might have been remarkable, nevertheless the progress was not evenly dispersed.

    The gap between rich and poor countries, and rich and poor people within countries, have grown.

    The richest quarter of the world’s population saw its per capita GDP increase nearly six-fold during the century, while the poorest quarter experienced less than a three-fold increase.

    The most recent World Economic Outlook studies 42 countries (representing almost 90 percent of world population) for which data are available for the entire 20th century.

    It reaches the conclusion that output per capita has risen appreciably but that the distribution of income among countries has become more unequal than at the beginning of the century.

    Incomes do not tell the whole story. Broader measures of welfare that take account of social conditions show that some poorer countries have made considerable progress. As an example, as a result of the GDP boom, some low-income countries, have impressive social indicators.

    When analyzing international, global trade, statistics created by the International Monetary Fund show, that developing countries have increased their share of world trade, from 19 percent in 1971, to 29 percent in 1999.

    The major regions show great variations.

    As an example, the newly industrialized economies (NIEs) of Asia have done well, in terms of capital growth, often at a huge price of the environment, while Africa as a whole has performed poorly.

    The composition of quality goods and services that countries export is a very important factor in terms of their GDP gains.

    The strongest rise by far has been in the export of manufactured goods.

    The share of primary commodities in world exports, such as food and raw materials, that are often produced by the poorest countries, has declined.

    Globalization has sharply increased private capital flows to developing countries during most of the 1990s. Statistics also shows, that direct foreign investment has become the most important category of capital flows. (Both portfolio investment and bank credit rose but they have been more volatile, falling sharply in the wake of the financial crises of the late 1990s).

    Global Labor Movement, and Labor Cost

    Workers (if allowed...) often move from one country to another partly to find better employment opportunities. (Labour movements in the new, extended Europe is a good example for this trend).

    The numbers involved are still quite small, but as an example, in the period 1965-1990, the proportion of labor forces round the world that was foreign born increased by about one-half.

    There is also potential for skills to be transferred back to the developing countries and for wages in those countries to rise.

    The important issue is, that in case of hi-tech, lean and flexible manufacturing / assembly, labour cost is only 5-7% of the overall cost. In other words it is NOT only the cheap labour cost, that drives globalization, and outsourcing from the West to third-world nations, and other so called 'cheap labour cost markets', but more importantly the opportunities of developing new markets, with wast, well organized and controlled populations... teh other key driver is to be locally involved and controlling the process from day one... (so that the fruits can be picked when they become ripe).

    The Information Exchange Aspect of Globalization

    Information exchange is an integral, often overlooked, aspect of globalization.

    As a result of information exchange, and technologies, such as eTrade, eBusiness, B2B (Business to Business), the Internet, eLearning, and others, direct foreign investment brings not only an expansion of the physical capital stock, but also technical innovation, including the following:

    • Valuable knowledge about modern design and production methods, integrating
    • TQM and TQC processes, methods, skills, and tools,
    • Education and training, that the workforce and their management receives in relationship with the new technology, TQM and TQC processes they have to follow and maintain,
    • Enginering and business management techniques,
    • New knowledge about different export markets, and the
    • Various economic policies, that govern such markets, that they have to master and follow.

    Since the initial cost of the above is typically covered by the investors, the package represents a valuable resource for the developing countries.

    The big issue about information exchange in CE and design, is that despite all legal agreements, copyright laws, and design rights laws, in some countries companies heavily steal ideas, product designs and processes. In other words your great idea you worked on for years, invested everything you had... might make money for somebody else... Is this ethical? Of course not.

    How can you fight this? Do you have the legal muscle to go into battle with a large country's huge enterprise and fight your rights? (Many copy-cats don'y even identify their companies... tha tragic fact is that these products are still imported...)

    What should you do to protect your design and IP (Intellectual Property) rights?

    These are hard pressing questions, and there are no clear answers. We live in an era in which millions of US Dollars worth of illegally copied, fake merchandize reaches the world markets every day... T shirts, video games, DVD videos, medications, cameras, memory chips, cell phones, computer networking servers with extra chips that report back home some interesting traffic (!) and zillions of other products...with famous brandnames, logos, shapes and looks.

    On the top of all this, these fake products often represent good quality, since they were produced on the same machines as the legal, original versions were in the previous shift, by the same workers, in the same factories... Interesting challenge? Try to come up with a feasible strategy on how to protect your globally collaborating development team and product, process, service design know-how.

    Critical Factors, that Improve TQM in the Globalization Process

    Many factors can help to improve quality in the globalization process.

    According to the International Monetary Fund, the experience of the countries that have increased output most rapidly shows the importance of creating conditions that are conducive to long-run per capita income growth.

    The critical conditions, that support TQM improvement in the globalization process include the following:

    • Political stability,
    • Economic stability,
    • Institution building, and structural reform (important for long-term development and financial transfers),
    • Macro-economic stability (important to create the right conditions for investment and saving),
    • Stable and friendly outward oriented policies to promote efficiency, quality of conduct, and trust through increased trade and investment,
    • Structural reform to encourage domestic competition in industry and academia,
    • Strong educational, financial and government institutions, and an effective government to foster good governance,
    • Education, training, and R&D to promote a wide understanding of the benefits and challenges of globalization, and to prepare a nation for producing higher than before quality and productivity results, and
    • External debt management to ensure adequate resources for sustainable development.

    These policies should be focussed on country-owned strategies to reduce poverty by promoting policies that are adequately funded, and include:

    • Health,
    • Education, and
    • Social safety nets, focusing on the poor and uneducated, that will take longer to participate in the new opportunities of fiscal and intellectual wealth creation process, due to the larger gaps they have to be able to jump over.

    How to Introduce Globalization Policies within a Sustainable TQM Framework?

    The introduction and promotion of TQM in the globalization process is by no means an easy task, nevertheless the following key drivers, methods and tools will help to smooth the process:

    • Promote ethical, 'win-win' free trade, meaning, that in certain market segments offer unrestricted market access for all exports from the poorest countries. (Choose markets, products, processes, and service, that you can offer them without loosing valuable, mission critical jobs in your home market). This fair share of benefits and opportunities should help them to move beyond specialization on primary commodities to producing processed goods for export.
    • Encourage the flow of private capital to the lower-income countries, particularly by means of foreign direct investment, with its twin benefits of steady financial flows and technology transfer.
    • Supplement more rapid debt relief with an increased level of new financial support.
    • Analyze their TQM needs and the requirements, and offer technical expertise to find out in what areas could they contribute best, for the benefit of all involved.
    • Analyze the actual TQM methods they understand, find out the core methodologies, the mathematical models, the underlying engineering (and/or other) science foundation they can follow, to improve their product/ process/ service quality and productivity records. DON'T OVERLOOK THE ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT! ... a new factory offering 1000 new jobs in the region should not be the source of causing millions of people to get severly sick!
    • Analyze the TQM technologies involved they can relatively easily implement. (It is no good offering technologies, that are so complex, that they'll never be able to master...)
    • Analyze and review the actual processes they follow and the way the process flow is integrated. This is their 'as is' process model. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method from concept to product. To understand total quality and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us...)
    • Analyze potential failure modes, and alternative solutions in terms of their 'as is' solution. (This will give you clear indicators for potential failure risks, therefore financial, and other possible losses).
    • Analyze the Total Quality Management aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service.
    • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of each process/ solution.
    • Design alternative TQM methods, processes based on what you have experienced/ seen, and learned, that they can master realistically for everybody's benefit.
    • Design an integrated TQM system, based on what you have analyzed, that has remote quality monitoring and networked messaging built-in. (This advanced technology will enable you to monitor TQM processes even 10,000 miles away from the actual process, and most importantly warn you of disturbing trends, so that you can interrupt, control and influence the remote processes BEFORE things go out of hand... (i.e. before parameter values move outside the range limits...)
    • Work in a multi-disciplinary TQM team and exchange ideas with local experts. Value their opinions, comments and suggestions even if initially you might not understand the importance or meaning of such input.
    • Understand the boundaries of TQM, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and developments by working on the cases presented. (Be realistic in terms of what is achieveable... make sure, that everybody realizes, that in order to survive and win, everybody must add quality value... as you will see, in many countries this concept is not always obvious yet...)

    To summarize this section, the most important rule is to offer ethical quality education and training of TQM and TQC principles, methods, tools and technologies, at a level and pace that the receiving party can accept and understand... You have to make sure, that your examples, cases and solutions fit into their culture, else you will alienate them.
