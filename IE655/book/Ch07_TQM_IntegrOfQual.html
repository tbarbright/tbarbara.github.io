Purpose

Why Do We Need to Integrate Quality into Every Hardware, Software, Design, Manufacturing, IT, and Service Activity, Following a Collaborative, Networked Approach?

The world is changing at an increasingly rapid rate. Customers demand quality products, and services on a global basis.

This challenge cannot be dealt with in isolation, therefore quality design, and CE must be networked into every process.

There are many positive and some negative issues about this. Following the world-wide recession waste is being cut down in factories, in the education system, in research and virtually everywhere in our everyday life.

Furthermore, globalization has a major effect on the way international product design, manufacturing and distribution come into play. New, dynamic labor forces in Europe, China, Asia, and India open up new business and investment opportunities, and despite the fact, that instead of negotiating and reasoning better, humans still kill each other to resolve disputes, intelligent, hard working people are moving ahead with plenty of positive energy to establish a better, and sustainable life on planet Earth.

It is now obvious to every logical thinker that adapting quickly to changes in market demand, in product and manufacturing technology and in the social, education and research environment are prerequisites to wealth creation (meaning both intellectual as well as materialistic wealth) and consequently to success in future markets.

We must think and work in new ways. As many rightfully say 'we cannot work harder, we need to be smarter...'.

With the advance of computers we have experienced the exciting opportunities of CAD (Computer Assisted, and then later, Computer Aided Design and soon I_CAD, Intelligent CAD), CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing), Desktop Publishing, etc. and now we are entering into a new era of highly integrated Desktop Design & Manufacturing or in other words Desktop CIM & Concurrent Engineering.

The fundamental reason for this is that talented engineers find it extremely pleasing to work in a team of engineers and customers, to react to market forces and rapidly create new products and new manufacturing solutions to fabricate these new products.

They are harnessing the growing computing power and the ability to share data via computer networks. The important issue is that this technology is available on the top of their desks, networked via the Internet, literally anywhere in the world!

As we have discussed earlier, within the TQM framework, concurrent engineering (CE) represents a structured (or in IT terms object-oriented), logical framework which supports a systematic approach to the integrated, concurrent design of products, and their related processes, including manufacture and support.

This method is intended to ensure that developers consider all elements of the product life cycle from concept through re-engineering, and final disposal, including quality, cost schedule and user requirements.

In contrast to the old, conventional, or sequential product design method, Concurrent Engineering focuses on customer satisfaction, on teamwork as well as on Design for Manufacturing, Design for Assembly, Quality and Total Lifecycle issues.

It is essential to understand that none of the Concurrent Engineering or CIM (Computer Integrated Manufacturing) methods work in practice unless they are applied to all levels of the enterprise, that they are introduced gradually and that they are managed by people who understand and support them.

As Dr. Philip Ashton's Concurrent Engineering PhD research work (at Rolls-Royce Motor Cars Limited, supervised by the author of this book) puts it, 'In the real world there are multiple objects, or perspectives that compete. Through object oriented design and manufacturing we can model the interaction between these objects. To do this, we have to define some very elementary principles or characteristics of objects within object modelling'.

According to Grady Booch's definition, an object is something you can do things to.

An object has state, behavior and identity.

The structure and behavior of similar objects are defined in their common class.

The terms instance and object are interchangeable. An object model is a collection of principles that form the foundation of an object oriented design.

In other words, it is a system modelling, software engineering term emphasizing the principles of abstraction, encapsulation, modularity, hierarchy, typing, concurrency and persistence. (Please feel free to look up these terms in the Glossary, in case you aren't familiar with them).

These principles, and object orientation in general are widely used throughout TQM modeling, as well as in our Case Library series of our eBooks, because of their extreme importance in terms of the productivity and quality gains one can achieve by implementing reusable, tested objects (modules) in product design and in manufacturing, not to forget about IT.

Design for Quality

Design for Quality (DFQ), Assembly (DFA) and Design for Manufacturing (DFM), in the broadest sense, means designing quality and "manufacturability" into the product and the required processes simultaneously.

The method of design for quality and manufacture is based on the recognition that:

1. Design is the first step in product manufacture.

2. Every design decision, if not considered carefully (i.e. if not optimized), can cost unnecessary manufacturing effort and loss.

Note that this "Early Design/Manufacturing Involvement" reduces waste, or put another way, increases the Total Quality of the organisation.

3. The product design must be modular, and should be carefully "tuned" to flexible assembly and manufacturing technologies operated in a lean CIM environment in order to fully realize the achievable quality, productivity, assembly and maintenance improvements.

4. The product and the manufacturing system should be designed with Total Productive Maintenance (TPM) in mind, amongst others, meaning, that the

  • Entire system should be flexible and lean enough to simultaneously produce and partially maintain resources and subsystems, without causing major interruptions to production, and,
  • System should have significant sensory feedback processing capabilities, so that quality changes could be analyzed real-time, predicted, and therefore avoided BEFORE major disasters, and losses occur.

In terms of product design all of the above listed considerations increase customer satisfaction, and in terms of manufacturing and service, reduce the number of accidents, defects, breakdowns, inventories and production losses.

To summarize, DFQ, DFA, DFM mean a level of CAD/CAM integration within a Concurrent Engineering environment, in which product design and manufacturing process are concurrent or parallel versus sequential, non-integrated activities. (Example: sensory feedback controlled robotic manipulator.)

Sources for New, High Quality Product / Process / Service Design Innovation

The Ford Motor Company stresses the following basic Total Quality Excellence (TQE) principles that give the foundation to our discussion on design for quality and on various techniques for improving product quality by preventing defects:

  • Quality is defined by the customer. The customer wants products and services that throughout their lifetime meet his or her needs and expectations at a cost that represents value. (Note, that the customer is now a global person, who might be living in the USA, in China, in Europe, in Japan, in India, or Africa, or elsewhere, sitting in front of his / her Internet computer reviewing product / process / service offers over the web, from multiple, different, competing and globally operating companies).
  • Quality excellence can best be achieved by preventing problems rather than by detecting and correcting them after they occur.
  • All work that is done by company employees, suppliers and dealers is part of a process that creates a product or service for the customer. Each person can influence some part of that process and therefore affect the quality of its output and ultimate customer satisfaction with the products and services offered.
  • Sustained quality excellence requires continuous product and process improvement. This means that regardless of how good the present performance may be, it can become better.
  • People provide the intelligence and generate the actions that are necessary to realize these improvements.
  • Each employee is a customer with a right to expect good quality work from others and an obligation to contribute work of high calibre to those who in turn are his or her customers.

To summarize the quality design philosophy with a future reflects:

  • Customer focus,
  • Continuous process analysis,
  • Innovation,
  • Improvement at all fronts,

driven by able and responsible people, as the key to change.

It is a well known and accepted fact that Total Quality innovation can lead to commercial success, can create wealth in its broadest sense, can increase the market share and can create high growth and sustainable profits.

Firms must introduce new products that are required by customers and change their processes and organizational structures accordingly. But what are the sources for new, better, higher quality product designs / processes, and services, and how could we become more innovative in terms of introducing total quality?

Let us attempt to summarize some fundamental concepts and outline some of the methods, that have a proven track record in the real-world.

New requirements created by the market (i.e. the customers of an old or existing product, or the potential customers of a new product).

Typical examples include external pressures such as functional requirements and/or improvements, safety, fashion, competition, seasonal factors, economy, etc.

Since this method is typically the most successful source for new product innovation, there are many methods that focus on this, including Quality Function Deployment (QFD), or Component oriented Requirements Analysis (CORA), as we prefer to name this method, market research surveys, process modeling (like CIMpgr) applied to functional, and object decomposition of components, and subassemblies, and others, like processes and services.

New technology and the exciting new opportunities that it can create

Think for a moment of the microchip, the mobile (cell) phone, interactive multimedia, or the emerging intelligent sensor technology, full high definition television, 4K and soon 8K video and television, or new materials, such as teflon, or a four axis CNC turning centre and the complex parts one can design for such equipment.

All of these can create new successful products if the designers and manufacturing engineers keep the customers' real requirements in mind and do not get obsessed with technology for the sake of technology itself.

Observe carefully and grasp the opportunity

This group includes situation-related development and innovation opportunities when something goes wrong (e.g. the bulb needs to be changed in the car), or a tool wore out, a part breaks because a product is poorly designed, or when electronic connectors are incompatible, and many others.

Grasp the opportunity when, as a result of a systematic study, analysis and observation, new ideas emerge.

Methods, such as QFD, or CORA, Failure Mode Effect Analysis (FMEA), or PFRA (Process Failure Risk Analysis), Poka-Yoke, quality circle brainstorming activities, functional and process analysis using CIMpgr modeling, simulation, statistical analysis of selected processes, and others, are good opportunities for this type of innovation.

Concurrent Engineering / Collaborative, Multi-Lifecycle Engineering

Collaborative, multi-lifecycle Concurrent Engineering within the wealth of a lean CIM, and adaptive supply chain communications infrastructure represents a new opportunity to innovate, since it integrates design, manufacturing, quality control, shop floor automation, marketing and other processes, based on Internet and intranet networking technologies.

In this respect creating a company culture providing the right data via smart, networked sensors, and the climate for innovation is of extreme importance.

Major design orders from a large organization, a major sponsor, or from government

These innovation opportunities include Ministry of Defence, or DOD (Department of Defense in the USA) type of work, research and product development for tasks to be performed in space (e.g. space suit, various domestic appliances modified for space use), major security, national safety, and other major building contracts, and others.

Step-by-step innovation, and continuous improvement

This group of methods has been very successful in particular in the Pacific Rim countries, notably in Japan, gradually spreading back to Europe and the USA.

Whilst the Stevensons, the Henry Fords, and the Steve Jobs have created their first successful products in the garage out of almost nothing, many companies found great success in gradually improving existing products.

Examples include agricultural machines, the television, the automobile, the video and 35mm camera, digital photo and video cameras (starting with Apple's original 640 x 480 pixel digital still camera), moving into advanced 5, 8 and even 16 Megapixel devices, the VCR, the electric organ, the PC, the telephone, the FAX machine, BMW cars, and many others.

It should be noted, that the CIMpgr object/ component-oriented process modeling methodology, the functional decomposition of objects and their value and gap analysis can be successfully applied to step-by-step innovation and continuous product/process improvement activities.

The great idea: the 'step-function change opportunity'; The Progressive Enterprise

This is a rare commodity. There is no research evidence on how a great idea is created in somebody's brain, however there are clear signs that a relaxing holiday, or some positive or negative excitement, or human emotions, such as love, hate, or even a falling apple can contribute to getting that long awaited great idea, that often looks so incredibly simple, after the event.

The key here, is that the enterprise, that can react real-time to new ideas becomes the progressive enterprise, that can take advantage of the new idea, and take it from concept to market in record time.

Systematic search for improvements

Systematic search for improvements in functionality, quality, for reduced part count and lower cost of an existing or a new product concept. The heart of the methodology is a structured, object - oriented functional analysis using the CIMpgr process modeling method, in collaboration with technology innovation (Esprit CAM example-1, example-2.)

Following this method and structured framework, based on the BOM (Bill of Material) file each component and sub-assembly of the product design is analyzed and valued in terms of function, quality, design for assembly, material selection, cost and other aspects such as ergonomy and aesthetics.

Having done the analysis, the losers become targets for innovation, guided by the already established object-oriented framework.

Recession, or a major shock, or lack of resources forcing change

  • Recession forcing change.
  • Major shock.
  • Lack of resources during a recession can create positive restructuring processes that turn the company and its existing products upside down and inside out.
  • Change of ownership, privatisation.
  • Merger, acquisition, and others, causing turbulance, and cultural change, that often produces innovative thoughts, products and/or services.

To summarize, when thinking about the various innovative ideas, products and services as part of the creative, as well as the feedback control / validation process it is worth considering the following questions:

  • Who is my customer?
  • Is there a market (or other) demand for my idea / proposed product / service?
  • Is it easy to use?
  • Is it uniquely patentable? Who will own the IP (Intellectual Property)?
  • Who will buy my product or service?
  • Why will they buy mine rather then somebody else's product, or idea?
  • Where can they buy it?
  • What do I need to charge for my innovative product / process / service?
  • Who is my competitor? Am I positioning my product correctly?
  • What regulations (safety, government, environmental, pollution, etc.) will my product and/or service be subject to?
  • What cultural, political, religious and other issues will my product and/or service be challenged by?
  • Any other considerations, that might come into play (such as hygiene, emotional issues, nutritional aspects, and others)?

The research method and toolset, supporting product / process, and service - oriented innovation by CE

In order to construct a comprehensive research method enabling creative teams to actively focus on innovation opportunities, a reusable, object / component-oriented structured framework a toolset is needed to integrate the specific ideas, and processes of interest through object modelling.

To that end, the research methods and the toolset should give access and use to the following types of modelling as follows:

  • Analyze the CE/TQM needs and the requirements.
  • Analyze the actual CE/TQM methods presented, find out the core methodologies, the mathematical models, the underlying engineering (and/or other) science foundation.
  • Analyze the CE/TQM technologies involved. (How is science turned into a practical solution/ engineering and/or computing technology? Learn, that science and technology are integrated processes).
  • Analyze and review the actual processes and the way the process flow is integrated. (Follow an object-oriented process analysis method from concept to product. To understand total quality and integration issues is a major challenge for all of us...)
  • Analyze potential failure modes, and alternative solutions.
  • Analyze the CE/Total Quality Management aspects of each process, as well as the end product/ service.
  • Analyze the benefits and the disadvantages of each process/ solution.
  • Design alternative CE/TQM methods, processes based on what you have experienced/ seen, and learned.
  • Design an integrated CE/TQM system, based on what you have analyzed.
  • Work in a multi-disciplinary CE/TQM team and exchange ideas.
  • Understand the boundaries of CE/TQM, as well as the tremendous potential of new ideas and developments by working on the cases presented.

To summarize, we all have to realize, that in order to survive and win, we must add quality value, and continuously innovate.

This is not easy, but for the most part a fascinating and exciting activity, that should be encouraged, nourished, developed, and supported. (In particular young engineers and scientists should be supported all aorund the world to innovate and make everybody's life better!)

Design for Manufacture / Assembly / Quality / Service / Maintenance

There are many internal and external technical, human and legal factors that put increasing pressure on the TQM team (including the design, manufacturing, quality and maintenance engineers, vendors and potential customers) when designing a new product, process, or service.

Due to international competition, and the desire of customers for high quality products at low cost, the design engineer must work significantly closer with all those involved in the creation of a successful product (i.e. the customers, the marketing, manufacturing, quality, service, IT, and maintenance engineers).

Design for manufacturing or assembly (DFM, DFA) is the next, more systematic step in that one needs to carefully analyze each part or sub-assembly for manual or robotized manufacture and assembly, or mass production, and gradually reduce waste accordingly.

Waste, or MUDA, as the Japanese call it, can mean:

  • Products that have unnecessary functions (i.e. functions that don't add value),
  • Many parts that could have been eliminated, or a
  • Large number of assembly directions that need several additional operations and even perhaps more robots,
  • Parts that have to be manufactured to unnecessarily tight tolerances, and
  • Others.

As a general rule for 'designing quality into every aspect of the product / process / service/ and/or IT system', designers and manufacturing engineers should aim to minimize the number of stages and setups through which a part or assembly must go before it becomes a product.

As an example to illustrate this thinking, due to significant developments in CNC systems one-hit machining, or single setup machining is a well proven method and technology.

This technology integrates all important aspects of what this Chapter is discussing, because it

  • Aims to increase quality by designing quality into every product / process / service step involved,
  • Reduce lead-times,
  • Reduce tooling,
  • Reduce setup costs performed by multi-axes complex CNC machines with automated tool and probe changers, offering real-time in-cycle quality feedback and statistical analysis for each part, independently of the batch size (meaning for batch size=1, to several thousands, the same quality procedures are performed on every part).

Single setup machining is typically used in combination of touch trigger probe measuring systems, and/or vision systems, capable of identifying the component's position and orientation, thus reducing expensive fixturing and setup, as well as eliminating quality issues due to poor raw material feed, or part setup mistakes.

The technology illustrated is very attractive, offering an excellent example for designing quality into every system, because although the CNC machines are relatively complex and expensive, typical savings can lead to

  • 50-80% reduction in machining costs,
  • Work in progress (WIP) inventory, not to mention the significant,
  • Reduction in scrap,
  • Enormous increase in productivity,
  • Reduced capital investment (i.e. one machine is required, instead of six, or even eight !)

Please note, that a detailed discussion of these, and other CE methods, including KANRI, KAISEN, MUDA, MURA, MURI, Poka-Yoke, smart-sensors, CMM Measuring Machines, vision systems, sensors, real-time networks, and other related fields, can be found in our Case Library at http://www.cimwareukandusa.com.
