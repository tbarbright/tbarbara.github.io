    Purpose

    The CE process does not end with a product, that leaves a factory and then everybody can forget about it... It should incorporate service aspects too, as part of a multi-lifecycle quality system covering concept to remanufacturing, and even recycling; the entire circle, often more than once.

    As we all know, many products need maintenance, and after sales support. Good CE engineers, designers and quality control engineering managers MUST think about these processes too at the design stage. This way they can create a truly world-class product.

    As an example, many people in the early 1960s in the automobile industry thought that by creating high quality products, customers will not come back and purchase a new model every 3-4 years.

    Others created very high quality cars, and th eresult is that these high quality designers and automakers are still around and prospering, whilst others have sunk... The lesson is, that quality CE is the ONLY realistic, long term future in design.

    The purpose of this Chapter is to offer analytical thinking, and a systems approach on how to develop and maintain a service quality system, that follows quality standards, and in return assures service

    • Conformity,
    • Traceability,
    • Discipline,
    • Keep known and allowed levels of programmed variance,
    • Offers interchangeability,
    • Reduce cost, and amongst many others, and most importantly
    • Improve, and sustain customer satisfaction.

    As we have stated earlier, service quality control means establishing and maintaining specified quality standards of services, and service quality engineering is the establishment and execution of tests to measure service quality and adherence to acceptance criteria specified in the standards.

    We all have, or had good as well as poor quality service experiences in restaurants, hotels, garages, computer service stores, in schools, universities, at airports, at bus stations, at gas (petrol) stations, and elsewhere.

    Let's see what methods there are to improve poor services, and make good ones even better... it is a huge business opportunity!

    How Do the Eight ISO Quality Management Principles Apply to the CE, Design and Aftersales Service Industry in General?

    Based on the latest ISO interpretation, this section introduces the eight quality management principles on which the quality management, including the quality service system standards are based.

    These principles can be used by senior management as a framework to guide their service organizations towards improved performance. (Note, that the Industrial Internet of Things networking and knowledge management framework offers an amazing opportunity to turn the ideas presented in this chapter into major business results.)

    Read these principles again, with customer service quality in mind...

    ISO TQM Standard Principle 1: Customer Focus: Organizations depend on their customers, and therefore should understand their current and future needs, as well as should meet customer requirements, and strive to exceed customer expectations.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Increased revenue and market share can be obtained through flexible and fast responses to market opportunities. (As service examples, consider a web-based airline reservation system, that optimizes time and cost between your needs, and what airlines offer; or a bank, that can approve mortgages, house sales and purchases in record time, because the legal aspects are performed in a JIT fashion, and others.)
    • Increased effectiveness in the use of the organization's resources to enhance customer satisfaction.
    • Improved customer loyalty leading to repeat business
    • Customer needs and expectations are understood better than before. (As a service example, consider that many hotels don't allow children, or dogs. Is there a way around this?)
    • Organizational objectives become better linked to customer needs and expectations, and
    • Customer needs and expectations are better communicated throughout the organization. (Think of a few visual displays in hotels, airports, garages where many questions could be spared by offering clear signs of communication...)
    • Customer satisfaction, as well as the actions taken on the results become measurable.
    • Customer relationships become systematically managed at all levels of the organization.
    • A balanced approach is ensured between satisfying customers and other interested parties (such as owners, employees, suppliers, financiers, local communities and the society as a whole.)

    .

    ISO TQM Standard Principle 2: Leadership: Leaders establish unity of purpose and direction of the organization. They should create and maintain the internal environment in which people can become fully involved in achieving the organization's objectives.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    Under a strong, clear and fair TQM leadership....

    • People will understand, and be motivated towards the organization's goals and objectives (... assuming, that the goals and principles are communicated clearly, and that the resources are made available to implement the desired and realistic goals... as a service example, imagine a hotel where they cut the janitor's budget to half to save expenses...)
    • Activities are evaluated, aligned and implemented in a unified way. (As service examples, consider a hotel, or a garage, where procedures are documented and followed properly...)
    • Miscommunication between levels of the organisation will be minimized. (Lack of directives, procedures, and processes open up the opportunity for miscommunication...)
    • The needs of all interested parties can be considered, including customers, owners, employees, suppliers, financiers, local communities, and the society as a whole. (Not easy, but needs good communication, and of course a true, honest cause... like keeping air and water quality clean, and others).
    • A clear vision of the organization's future can be established. (A good and clear mission statement helps...)
    • Challenging goals and targets can be set.
    • Shared values can be created and sustained, and fairness and ethical models can be created at all levels of the organization.
    • Trust can be established, and fear can be eliminated
    • People can be provided with the required resources, training and freedom to act at a clearly established level of responsibility and accountability. (As a bonus you can use training, and recognition; they always work).
    • Employees' contributions will be inspired, encouraged and recognized.

    .

    ISO TQM Standard Principle 3: Involvement of People: People at all levels are the essence of an organization and their full involvement enables their abilities to be used for the organization's benefit.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Successful organizations need motivated, committed and involved people. (Train by setting good examples. Encourage to employees to seek better solutions. Listen to what they are telling you; they live with their processes every day, they know them better than management...)
    • Innovation and creativity is expanding the organization's objectives.
    • Employees are accountable for their own performances. (Clear definitions, documentation, procedures and processes help!)
    • Employees should be eager to participate in and contribute to continual improvement. (Respect and award them for good ideas.)
    • Employees should understand the importance of their contribution and their roles in the organization.
    • Employees should be able to identify the constraints of their performances.
    • Employees should accept the ownership of problems and it should be their responsibility to solve them
    • Employees should evaluate their performances against their personal goals and objectives. (Set levels, but only rarely rank employees, and even then only on the basis of selected in advance, and well understood critical performance items).
    • Employees should be actively seeking opportunities to enhance their competence, knowledge and experience levels. (Offer them education and training).
    • Employees should freely share knowledge and experience (within confined circles, as business confidentiality allows).
    • Employees should openly discuss problems and issues with each other. (Create a trustworthy, positive atmosphere at the workplace for employees to talk to each other during breaks, and meetings).

    .

    ISO TQM Standard Principle 4: Process Approach: A desired result is achieved more efficiently when activities and related resources are managed as a process.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Lower costs and shorter cycle times can be achieved, through effective and controlled use of resources. (Processes must be well documented and understood. CIMpgr is a great concept and tool.)
    • Improved, consistent and predictable results can be offered to both internal, as well as external customers
    • Focused and prioritized improvement opportunities become reality, since each process step is identified and controlled (in a similar fashion to an object, with it's identifier, attributes, and behavior, when following object-oriented system modeling principles).
    • Activities are defined systematically, therefore the desired result can be obtained realistically. (In CIMpgr Inputs, Outputs, Resources and Controls become very obvious and clear to everyone, at every layer of the process pyramid.)
    • Clear responsibility and accountability can be established for managing key activities in literally any organization. (Responsibilities, and authority are typically not defined clearly in most organizations. This leads to finger pointing, lack of motivation, and poor management / workforce communication.)
    • The capability of key activities can be analyzed and measured. (Identify the key activities and processes. Make them visually clear and understandable to everyone. Imagine a multimedia training program for garage technicians, or for janitors, or for nurses, and many others, who must perform critical services.)
    • The interfaces and communication paths of key activities within, and between the functions (or in other words classes of objects) of the organization can be identified. (Affinity diagrams and CIMpgr models can help here a great deal.)
    • By focusing on factors such as resources, methods, and materials key activities of the organization can be improved.
    • By evaluating process failure risks, consequences and impacts of activities on (internal, as well as external) customers, suppliers and other interested parties becomes possible, and measurable. (Our PFRA method and tools can help here a lot.)

    .

    ISO TQM Principle 5: Systems Approach to Management: Identifying, understanding and managing interrelated processes as a system contributes to the organization's effectiveness and efficiency in achieving its objectives.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Processes that will achieve the desired results will become better integrated and aligned. (Consider using CIMpgr for process modeling; it is a great communication tool).
    • Management and process owners will have the ability to focus their efforts on the key processes. (Affinity diagrams can help here a lot).
    • Since the consistency, effectiveness and the efficiency of the organization will grow, the confidence of interested parties in the organization will grow too.
    • System structuring and tuning will become possible to achieve the organization's objectives in the most effective and efficient way.
    • Understanding the interdependencies between the processes of the system will yield good results. (An integrated approach to process modeling, requirements and risk analysis within a TQM framework works extremely well).
    • Structured (and object/ component-oriented) modeling approaches that harmonize and integrate processes will become reality.
    • The resistance created by cross-functional barriers will be reduced, providing a better understanding of the roles and responsibilities necessary for achieving common objectives. (Make everybody a contributing team member, then reward them well).
    • Organizational capabilities, and the establishment of resource constraints prior to action will be better understood by all involved (and mostly by all those who have created the models).
    • Targeting and defining how specific activities within a system should operate become reality. (Clear, measurable targets are essential).
    • Continually improving the system through measurement and evaluation becomes possible, due to the analytical and quantifiable approach of the process models. (CIMpgr can create wonders).

    .

    ISO TQM Principle 6: Continual Improvement: The continual improvement of the organization's overall performance should be a permanent objective of the organization.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Performance advantage through improved organizational capabilities. (Well documented procedures should be continuously improved, and communication lines should be distributed. New ideas should be encouraged to 'think outside the box'. In most service industries the fatal mistake is that management does not understand the way the typical hotel guest, or airline passenger think, or behaves, just to raise a few examples. They don't know what information and services they need, how they orient themselves, because they have been in the business for too many years, separated from the customers, already forgotten what it is all about... the key here is to find a few new customers who can open managers' eyes and ears and explain them what should be improved by walking through the process steps, step-by-step, analyzing every process, including the process's condition space.)
    • Alignment of improvement activities at all levels to an organization's strategic intent.
    • Flexibility to react quickly to opportunities. (As a service example, consider the introduction of new materials in a dental office to avoid mercury poisoning.)
    • A consistent organization-wide approach to the continual improvement of the organization's performance. (Change must come from the top, and change management should cover the entire business; assuming it is a change for the better.)
    • Employees should be offered training in the methods and tools of continual improvement. (Short lunch break training sessions are very valuable.)
    • Continual improvement of products, processes and systems should be an objective for every individual in the organization.
    • Employees and management should establish goals to guide, and measure to track continual improvement.
    • Management should recognize and acknowledge improvements. (Everybody likes respect and recognition; when it is due, give it to them!)

    ISO TQM Principle 7: Factual Approach to Decision Making: Effective decisions are based on the analysis of data and information. (Information is data about data).

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • Informed decisions (can only be made when the data is reliable and accurate; in service industries, because of the human input, often data is not accurate, not factual, but filtered through emotions.)
    • An increased ability to demonstrate the effectiveness of past decisions through reference to factual records. (Records are often inaccurate, and therefore cannot be used as benchmarks. Quality records are essential.)
    • A factual approach increases the ability to review, challenge and change opinions and decisions.
    • Management must ensure, that data and information is sufficiently accurate and reliable (and that there is no hidden agenda; such as collecting evidence for future 'games' and other types of misuse, although collecting evidence is often essential to develop a case, before an employee gets fired. Not everybody is an 'angel' in the team...)
    • Data should be made accessible to those who need it (within a security framework); data protection laws must be studied!)
    • Data and information should be analyzed, based on validated, robust methods. (Statistical tools help here.)
    • Decisions and actions should be based on factual data and information analysis, balanced with experience and intuition.

    .

    ISO TQM Principle 8: Mutually Beneficial Supplier Relationships: Even if an organization and its suppliers are interdependent, a mutually beneficial relationship enhances their ability to create value.

    Key Drivers and Achievable Gains Include the Following:

    • There is increased ability to create value for both parties, meaning the employer, as well as the employees.
    • The flexibility and speed of joint responses to changing market or customer needs and expectations becomes a possibility. (A lean organization, adopting JIT principles can always react to market changes, or disasters better.)
    • Optimization of costs and resources is possible. (By analyzing the resource side of CIMpgr models huge savings can be achieved.)
    • Relationships can be established, that balance short-term gains with long-term considerations. (Affinity Diagrams document such relations well.)
    • Pooling of expertise and resources with partners can be beneficial.
    • Identifying and selecting key suppliers is possible.
    • Clear and open communication is required.
    • Sharing information and future plans becomes a business reality. (Management must implement an 'open door' policy, a friendly and 'ready to listen' atmosphere, and should inform employees of some of the non-confidential future plans. Note, that some employees and managers are not 'angels', in that they join a company for a year, or so, to spy on future development plans, customer service tricks and secrets, and others.)
    • Establishing joint development and improvement activities becomes a possibility.
    • Inspiring, encouraging and recognizing improvements and achievements by suppliers is beneficial to all parties.
    • Recognizing opportunities for improvements is beneficial to all parties.

    Automotive Service Quality Case

    As a result of high-quality integrated design and manufacturing / assembly processes the amount of warranty repair work performed at car dealerships are reduced, driving the need to offset the losses by becoming more competitive in the service aftermarket.

    After the warranty period, when customers have more choices for automotive service, surveys show that convenience - even before service quality -is the most significant factor in a customer's decision about where to get their car serviced.

    As surveys, and several experiences indicate, managing the customer service experience becomes the competitive difference between the customer and the service operation.

    Despite this obvious opportunity, dealerships often have limited resources, as well as an arrogant attitude to scheduling customer service visits as they try to schedule requests and resources for service.

    The result, is that customers' time is wasted, both when trying to make appointments, as well as when waiting for their cars in the service, as often ignored by service providers, because they have to wait on-hold or, even worse, get continuous busy signals while service advisors find themselves tied to their telephones.

    Based on this introduction, discuss the following TQM service issues:

    • Automotive service opportunities: Should we let customers schedule their own service appointments via the web, email, or an automated phone system-anytime, unassisted? Is the knowledgeable human operator important in this case? Discuss and explain why?
    • Service managers are key to customer satisfaction: The Service Manager is the most important person when it comes to sales and customer retention. If customers like them, the service business grows, else rapidly declines... How would you change into a likeable service manager? How would you improve your current, not that popular Service Manager? What are the key attributes, tricks and science here? How far should you go, and accept, or give up?
    • Service offered by the dealerships, versus by independent garages: USA FEDERAL LAWS PROHIBIT NEW CAR DEALERS FROM IMPLYING OR DENYING WARRANTY SERVICE BECAUSE ROUTINE SCHEDULED MAINTENANCE WAS PERFORMED BY AN INDEPENDENT REPAIR SHOP. How many of the customers know about this law and how many have been manipulated back to the dealers? Many of us have experienced this situation, but what can we do? What should services do?
    • Competitive pricing of automotive services: Today, the majority of garage-service owners are complaining about a decrease in profit margins and cash flow. How would you determine automotive service prices? What is fair, and what is over-priced?
    • Effective management of service employees: Effectively managing people is not an easy task. What are the do's and don'ts, in what situations, and why?
    • Post - service customer reviews: Effective data communication is essential. Garages are often rated by their customers, nevertheless nobody likes to fill in forms, or answer questions on the phone for 10 minutes, not even for 5 minutes... What is the best customer feedback solution? How would you make your customer interested in helping to improve the service (...where is the carrot?)
    • How to cope with high turnover: You have a good technician, you reward him by offering more free training. He decides to leave after a few years; you loose your investment. Your customers are unhappy, because they are used to his smile and quality work. What is a reasonable solution (without returning to feudalism?)
    • How to cope with sabotage: You need to increase your business. An easy-sounding route is to let your technicians change parts, that are fine. If these are warranty claims, the garage gains, the customer is happy, and the factory looses... but the garage always gains, because you charge for labour... is this a feasible path? Is this ethical, and good business, that can be sustained? How would you fight back as a manager sitting in the factory?
