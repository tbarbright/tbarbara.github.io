    Purpose

    The purpose of this chapter is to explain how Design for Manufacturing (DFM), Assembly (DFA), Quality (DFQ), and design for other objective functions (DFx), embedded into Deming's 14 Points of Management Rules and Quality Concepts, help our concurrent engineering / PLM mission.

    It is important to realize, that DFM, and DFA, and DFQ, etc. are not the only methods and tools available to the CE / PLM team, nevertheless they are very important, since they offer opportunities to simplify product designs, reduce the number of parts, eliminate assembly errors, and many other wasteful operations and opportunities.

    First, let us introduce Deming's rules, then turn our attention towards DFM, DFA, DFQ, and DFx / PLM in general.

    Deming's 14 Rules of Management and Quality Concepts, and How These Rules Effect Our Concurrent Engineering Mission

    According to one of Dr. W. Edward Deming's students: "He is the man who taught the Japanese how to produce goods of high quality at low cost. He has introduced the concept that higher quality means lower cost, an idea foreign to most managers". Since it is well known, that design, manufacturing/assembly and service integration quality made Japanese products and services truly outstanding, this is an important comment in our concurrent engineering discussions.

    In order to understand how Deming has helped design for quality manufacturing, assembly, service and other objective functions, first we explain, that following the classical school, the idea of a good manager is the one who:

    • Sets up a system, such as a product design system,
    • Directs the work through subordinates,
    • Sets a standard of performance for his employees by making crisp and unambiguous assignments.
    • Sets goals and production targets,
    • Rates people as objectively as he / she can.
    • Identifies poor performers and either educates them or replaces them. By doing so he / she believes that he /she creates the most efficient system possible.

    Deming's way is different in the sense, that he believes that the purpose of the organization is not to make profit for survival, but to provide the best service or goods at the lowest cost.

    He believes that not only the manager but everyone must contribute to improve the system. After all, the machine operator knows his machine best, the truck driver his routes of delivery, and the maintenance technicians many products.

    In short, Deming's method means that the manager will have a natural basis for building a team and will not have introduced adversarial relations.

    Deming's way is not just attention to quality control. It is also a management philosophy for achieving lower cost and higher quality.

    As Deming puts it: 'There will be no room for managers who do not know how to work with their people to produce high quality goods at low cost. High reliability cannot be secured without worker co-operation.

    Complex systems cannot be understood without statistics. In the competitive world of the future, companies which have not mastered these ideas will simply disappear, and there will be no excuses'.

    To summarize, Deming's fourteen paraphrased rules of management are as follows:

    1. Create consistency and continuity of purpose towards the improvement of products and services with the aim of becoming competitive, staying competitive, and providing jobs. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of the design process of product families. Think of using parametric design methods, well tested modules, components from reliable suppliers, and object oriented thinking throughout th eentire design system.)

    2. Refuse to allow commonly accepted levels of delay for mistakes, defective material, or defective workmanship. Adopt the new philosophy.

    Accept that we are in a new economic age. Act accordingly otherwise you'll disappear !

    Western management must awaken to the challenge, must learn their responsibilities, and take on leadership for change. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of well tested supply chains and the importance of early vendor collaboration.)

    3. Eliminate the need for, and dependence upon inspection to achieve quality by building quality into the product in the first place. Design for quality, manufacturability, assembly, maintenance, etc. Design quality into the product, process and/or service. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of team-oriented problem solving; teams, that have customers, suppliers, quality, marketing, manufacturing, assembly, etc. functions represented. Let them get involved with th edesign at an early stage.)

    4. Reduce the number of suppliers and end the practice of awarding business on the basis of the price tag.

    Buy on statistical evidence, not price. Instead minimize the total cost, including the analysis of quality.

    Move towards a single, or few suppliers, and develop a long-term relationship of loyalty and trust. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of supply chains that you can trust.)

    5. Search continually for problems in the system and seek ways to improve it in order to decrease cost. Improve constantly and forever the system of production and service, to improve quality and productivity, and thus constantly decrease cost. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of continuous quality improvement opportunities; this should include human performance evaluations and tuition support for further development. Human capital is the most important capital any company has.)

    6. Institute modern methods of training and education on the job. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of including the big picture into training courses, so that everybody understands at least the basics of the company's mission, the way designers try to work with everyone to create good products, and others.)

    7. Focus on leadership and supervision to help people to do a better job. Provide the tools and techniques for people to have pride in their workmanship. Create an appropriate infrastructure to enable people to concentrate on their jobs. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of proper CAD/CAM systems, simulation tools, and others that help the CE/PLM team to reduce waste and costly changes.)

    8. Eliminate fear. Encourage open, honest, constructive communication between the workforce, the management, the designers, and everybody who plays a key role in the success of the enterprise. Make sure, that everyone may work effectively for the company.

    9. Break down barriers between departments. Encourage problem solving through teamwork, and prompt information exchange between research, design, sales, and production to foresee problems and solve them effectively. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of organizing group meetings between designers and manufacturing, assembly, quality, maintenance engineers and technicians, so that they exchange ideas, and discuss challenges. Make sure, that people know each other and trust each other too.)

    10. Eliminate the use of numerical goals, slogans and posters for the workforce. They soon become absurd and meaningless. (Use funny cartoons instead if you are keen on posters !) Note, that design quality and innovation cannot be measured by numbers easily... there is a lot more too it, than fighting for numbers...

    11. Eliminate work standards and quotas on the factory floor. Substitute leadership. Eliminate management by numbers and numerical goals. Use statistical methods to improve and understand quality and productivity and eliminate all standards prescribing numerical quotas.

    12. Remove barriers that rob the hourly workers, managers, engineers, and others of their right to pride of workmanship. The responsibility of supervisors must be changed from sheer numbers to quality. (In terms of CE/PLM, let people talk freely and discuss issues as they occur.)

    13. Institute a vigorous program of regular education and re-training to keep people abreast of new developments in various areas of science and technology. (In terms of CE/PLM, think of setting up regular design assessment meeting, during which, as an example, maintenance technicians could suggest design changes; a huge opportunity in most companies.)

    Explain and teach in a balanced way the broad issues, the corporate culture as well as the technical details, as appropriate to various levels of the workforce.

    14. Clearly define and demonstrate management's permanent commitment to quality and productivity, and assure everyone, that the transformation is everybody's job.

    Design for Manufacturing, Assembly, Quality, Maintenance

    There are many internal and external technical, human and legal factors that put increasing pressure on the Concurrent Engineering team (including the design, manufacturing, quality and maintenance engineers, vendors and potential customers) when designing a new product. Due to international competition, and the desire of customers for high quality products at low cost, the design engineer must work significantly closer with all who are involved in the creation of a successful product (i.e. the customers, the marketing, manufacturing, quality and maintenance engineers).

    Design for manufacturing or assembly (DFM, DFA) is the next, more systematic step in that one needs to carefully analyze each part or sub-assembly for manual or robotized manufacture and assembly, or mass production, and gradually reduce waste accordingly.

    Waste, or MUDA, as the Japanese call it, can mean the following:

    • Products that have unnecessary functions (i.e. functions that don't add value), or
    • Many parts that could have been eliminated, or
    • A large number of assembly directions (orientations) that need several additional operations and even perhaps more robots, or
    • Parts, that have to be manufactured to unnecessarily tight tolerances,
    • Parts, that are manufactured using expensive materials,
    • Parts, that are designed and manufactured use toxic materials, that are difficult, expensive, or even impossible to reuse / recycle, and others.

    In this section we provide sets of principles and rules, that will help the CE/PLM Team to take the best possible decisions.

    Introduction to Design for Manufacture and Single Setup Machining

    As a general DFM rule designers and manufacturing engineers should aim to minimize the number of stages and setups through which a part or assembly must go through before it becomes a product.

    Due to significant developments in CNC (Computer Numerical Control) machines and systems one-hit machining, or single setup machining is a well proven method and technology.

    It aims to reduce lead-times, tooling and setup costs performed by multi-axes complex CNC machines with tool and probe changers.

    Single setup machining is typically used in combination of touch trigger probe measuring systems capable of identifying the component's position and orientation, thus reducing expensive fixturing and setup.

    The technology is very attractive, because although the CNC machines are relatively complex, typical savings can lead to 50-80% reduction in machining and work in progress (WIP) and a significant reduction in scrap.

    Design for Manufacturing Strategy

    When planning for manual or programmable, flexible manufacturing and robotized assembly one should consider many conflicting management and technical factors, such as:

    • Market and design strategy for the product as well as the processes involved. Note, that the product strategy should normally be global and the process strategy is usually local
    • Clearly understand all processes involved from market analysis, customer requirements to design, manufacture/assembly, test, packaging and maintenance. If some processes are not clear one should develop the necessary prototypes, models, computer simulation, experiments, etc. to minimise risk
    • Analyze the manufacturing and assembly functions, operations  and assembly sequences simultaneously at the design stage preferably using advanced modelling tools and an experienced Concurrent Engineering team to examine the assembly and sub-assembly definitions of the product and find the best organisation and production methods
    • Apply the most appropriate, rather than the latest technology for each process or operation
    • Take advantage of the product specification, design, manufacturing and assembly, testing, modelling, simulation, carbon footprint load, toxicity level, and cost analysis tools that are available to synthesise a system's design, functionality, reuse potential and behavior.

    Well known tools include the Boothroyd and Dewhurst DFM/DFA methodology and software tools, assembly line simulation tools such as SimFactory, BYG/GRASP, axiomatic approaches such as Suh's principles of design, mould flow simulation tools, such as MOULDFLOW, cost analysis tools, such as Activity Based Costing (ABC) and many others.

    Introduction to Design for Manufacture, Assembly, Quality, Green Design for the Environment, and Maintenance Rules

    In order to exploit the concept of Concurrent Engineering / Green PLM to the fullest extent, the products to be manufactured and assembled must be suited for the selected method and processes.

    Before designing a product for manual or automated manufacture with good quality and ease of assembly and maintenance in mind, the Concurrent Engineering team should consider the following basic rules for component, product and manufacturing/assembly system design:

    • Follow a modular design and product structure, using standard components if possible
    • Design for minimum number of parts
    • Design parts/products to be multi-functional and for multi-use
    • Select the appropriate materials and
    • Design parts for ease of fabrication
    • Follow the layered assembly principles and consider the following aspects in particular: parts handling and feeding, parts orientation, parts identification, parts positioning, ease of part mating, compliance, processing surfaces and allowable tolerances, joining methods, assembly directions, fasteners and labels
    • Use the appropriate DFM/DFA assessment tools, including sustainable green aspects, and economic justification methods,  and then program the best affordable sustainable green quality, and lowest cost into the entire process
    • Consider carefully part and finished product testing and
    • Packaging, again here too, use sustainable green methods and technologies, that can be reused / recycled,
    • Poduct maintenance, after sales customer care, and
    • Reuse / recycling / remanufacturing options.

    Sustainable Green Design Trends, Rules and Concepts

    In product and process design, sustainable green means waste reduction, and optimization, therefore advocates lean six-sigma concepts and methods.

    In terms of green sustainability we consider an eco-friendly state, or process that can be maintained over time, for a very long time.

    Also, the ability of an ecosystem to maintain ecological design and manufacturing processes and functions, biological diversity, and productivity over time.

    Furthermore, sustainable green design engineering encompasses the concept of meeting present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

    In other words, the characteristic of green, sustainable advanced quality design, manufacturing, service, etc. is being able to coexist with another system indefinitely, without either system being damaged.

    The sustainable green concept also emphasizes, that the creation of wealth within the community considers the wellbeing of both the human as well as natural environments, and is focused on the more complex processes of development rather than on simple growth or accumulation only.

    Advanced, sustainable green design and manufacturing activities are used in many areas, including product and process design, control, fabrication, quality assurance, quality control, test, assembly, disassembly, remanufacturing / recycling, and others.

    Greening of products and processes started out as a compliance issue. Due to measurable and visible green-house effect and other positive political changes, greening is now a socio-economic tidal wave in almost every country in the world.

    Modular Design and Product Structure, Using Standard Components

    Modular design and product structure offer several advantages in that:

    • Products can be configured easier and a family of products can be created or derived from one all-encompassing design (in particular when using parametric technologies within a powerful PLM architecture)
    • New product families can be created by incorporating existing and new modules, or new functions using existing modules
    • All downstream design and manufacturing processing costs can be reduced
    • Some modules can be built in-house and some externally. As the product is modular, this decision can be taken at an early stage of development, reducing overall product development cost
    • Engineering changes can be implemented more easily and at a lower cost, as modular parts of the product can be changed independently
    • More product options are possible and can be created in a significantly shorter time (note, that there are always some lead-time reduction opportunities; you just have to explore all aspects of the process).
    • For the product as well as for each module the design risks are reduced because testing can be done in a more systematic fashion well before assembly takes place, which enables problems to be isolated.
    • Dissassembly, field service and maintenance is easier and less expensive.

    The basic rules that should be considered in this respect include the following:

    1. Create different functional variants from basic modules.

    2. Satisfy common functional requirements with standardised modules, allowing the required scale of economy.

    3. The more flexible the production system, the easier it is to implement changes in product design (i.e. features, functions, programmed bahavior, look and feel, ergonomics, and others).

    4. Isolate technologies that are likely to change fast. (Use software, that can be upgraded easily. As an example think of how simple it is to upgrade an Apple iPhone, or an iPod... I always wonder, why couldn't we upgrade our digital camera software, or software in our automobiles, or in dive computers over the Internet too... designers claim, that it would be unsafe to do so... not really; this is just old fashioned market control... it will come / it is here...)

    5. Isolate functional requirements and/or solutions that are likely to change faster then the rest of the product. (By modular design, such components can be interchanged, updated, upgraded, developed further... This is an excellent example for modern thinking: USA designed Red Digital Cameras: http://www.red.com/epic_scarlet and in PDF. Note, that this camera is not just years ahead in terms of resolution and quality, but also breaks completely new grounds in terms of digital image capture and processing...)

    6. Standardise electronic and mechanical interfaces, mating surfaces, etc. to maximise interchangeability. (This is an excellent example for modern thinking: USA designed Red Digital Cameras (note, that URLs often change unfortunately): http://www.red.com/epic_scarlet and in PDF).

    7. Store well tested and designed modules in a solid model product design database for ease of access by other members of the Concurrent Engineering team. (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3, Example-4)

    8. Models of a family or a product line should be designed to be manufactured, assembled and tested using standardized (i.e. design for six-sigma optimized), preferably flexible manufacturing and assembly processes.

    9. Never bury important components or sub-assemblies, because they will be difficult to maintain and test.

    Design for Minimum Number of Parts

    In an ideal world a product should be manufactured from one single component and material using one process... but of course this is typically not possible. (Just imagine an automobile, and you'll understand the complexity of implementing this great idea...)

    However, reducing the number of parts in any product design is often possible. These are some of the visible benefits:

    • Inventory holding costs of parts and sub-assemblies (e.g. fasteners).
    • Work-in-progress inventory and material handling costs.
    • Manufacturing and assembly costs (usually assembly costs increase with the number of parts in an assembly, no matter how small or insignificant they appear to be).
    • Maintenance costs, the
    • Cost of testing, quality control,
    • Cost of reuse, recycling and remanufacturing, and others.

    In order to design for minimum number of parts one should analyze the functional and aesthetic requirements of the product as a whole, as well as each component by using methods such as DFA, and CIMpgr and then try to eliminate candidates that the analysis identifies either because of unnecessary functionality, or because of missing functionality, or for some other engineering or economic reason.

    The basic rules one should consider in this respect include the following:

    1. Eliminate extra parts by either deleting them, or, if this is not possible, incorporating their functions into multifunctional parts (e.g. by following a net shape manufacturing approach, rather than assembly).

    2. Parts must be separate, or isolated from other parts, otherwise their assembly and/or disassembly will not be possible.

    3. Non-existent parts cost nothing to make, handle, orient or store.

    Design Parts for Ease of Fabrication and Follow the Layered Assembly Principles

    The prime concern in advanced, or flexible, robotized assembly is to provide unobstructed vertical access for all parts and sub-assemblies. This can be achieved by avoiding lifting and rotating the assembly by following the layered, vertical access, or stacked assembly principles.

    Following this concept, typically the largest and most rigid and stable component serves as the assembly base, so it is placed first into the fixture, or it acts as a fixture itself, followed by subassemblies and parts layer after layer.

    Parts handling, feeding and orientation

    There are many different aspects that should be considered in this group, including the following:

    • Are the parts flexible or rigid?  Flexible parts, such as cables, wires, belts are more difficult to handle and assemble than rigid parts because the position and orientation of the part varies during handling. As a general rule, all parts that are to be assembled by means of a robot should be rigid and stiff. Furthermore, orientation of the part should be maintained from the point of manufacture to the point of final assembly if at all possible. Think of the following example: a switch doesn't need to be wired to the board, it could be mounted on the board itself with continuity circuitry to provide the electrical connections. If the cable cannot be avoided for some reason, the plug, or cable end must be in a known position (in robotic terms: "location") and orientation for the robot or assembly head to pick it up. Belts should be replaced by direct drive shafts or gears if possible. Parts can be linked together and kept in a known orientation by means of a shaped plastic tray, or part of a web, or tube, or some other device, like a magazine.
    • Small parts feeding. The most common method employs vibratory bowl feeders, elevating feeders, barrel feeders, parts magazines, strips, etc. (Figure 5.9) These devices can ensure small part feeding as well as orienting. Since the bowl vibrates, it causes small parts to slide and tumble along a specially designed track that spirals upward inside the wall of the bowl. Meanwhile the parts are randomly selected and gradually oriented. They are eventually knocked off the track by a narrow, shaped section of the track, or by strike-off plates, compressed air, or some other method. The parts are then presented in a known position and orientation to the robot, or the gripper of the assembly head. Independently of which feeder is used, tangling, jamming and shingling can be eliminated. If springs are fed, closed-loop springs with a wire diameter greater than the spacing between coils should be used. As a set of general rules, consider the following: sharp edges should be avoided to prevent parts of catching each other or the slides they have to pass through. Tangling can be avoided by part design. To increase the stability of the part, it should have the lowest centre of gravity. The bases of the parts should be flat, preventing them from riding on each other and the mating surfaces of the parts should be as large as possible and perpendicular to the direction of travel.
    • Parts designed specifically for robotized or automated grasping should be compatible with the kind of gripper jaws used. Typically they should incorporate the appropriate slots, tabs or holes to aid grasping. Parts with relatively large and flat top surfaces are ideal for vacuum or magnetic pickup.

    Symmetrical parts

    The more symmetrical the part, the easier it is to handle, position and orient. Furthermore, symmetrical parts increase assembly reliability and quality, in particular if the processes are automated, because they reduce the possibility of mating parts in the wrong orientation. If symmetry is not possible, the designer should overemphasise asymmetry by incorporating a holdable flat or shaped surface, a slot or a hole, a pin or a chamfer.

    Ease of part positioning, mating and compliance

    Part surfaces brought together during an assembly process can be divided into two different groups, these are:

    1. Surfaces that mate, hence are critical and have a function, e.g. support another functional surface, transfer torque, or force, etc., and

    2. Surfaces that do not mate, i.e. are not important from a functional point of view, in other words are "free surfaces", but still cause concern for reasons of product aesthetics or safety.

    When designing for ease of part mating (i.e. bringing parts together in an assembly process) the Concurrent Engineering team must concentrate on simplifying components and their functional surfaces without compromising their functional behaviour or the required aesthetic appearance of the product.

    Compliance

    Compliance in Design for Assembly (DFA) means accommodating uncertainty of dimensional tolerances.

    Since every mechanical component is manufactured within a given tolerance, defined by design and the machinery used to manufacture it, parts and robot grippers or other tools can jam.

    Part misalignment and tolerance stack up occurs when components are produced by different methods, (for example turning, stamping, casting and injection moulding, etc.) and when the designers are not familiar with the quality of machinery the manufacturing engineers work with. Hence compliance must be designed into the product as well as the production processes.

    One of the best ways of avoiding jamming and other types of compliance problems is to design compliance, (e.g. generous chamfers on both mating parts so that the parts fit together even when slightly misaligned).

    This can be achieved by chamfers and various shapes of lead-ins.

    Fasteners

    If at all possible, fasteners (screws, nuts and bolts, clips and rivets) should be eliminated and parts joined by designing them so that they can be snapped or interlocked together. Avoiding the need for fasteners does not mean that components cannot be taken apart.

    On the contrary, parts that snap together may be disassembled easily if the design allows the snap to be pushed back

    If fasteners cannot be avoided, then one should analyse the kind, variety, material, head-shape and overall geometry of the fasteners to be used in a product.

    Obviously the larger the variety the higher the cost. Also if the geometry is not right, automated feeders and screwdrivers cannot be used.

    Important issues to consider include:

    • The length-to-diameter ratio of the screw (to prevent the screw from tumbling in the tube feeding an automated screwdriver) should be 1.5 to 1 and must allow access clearance between the screw head and the wall of the tube wall.
    • The drive geometry / configuration of the screw head. These include Phillips, hex, Allen, square recess and slotted. The latter is the least desirable shape since it is the most difficult to engage with the screwdriver bit.
    • The orientation of the screw - generally vertical versus angular orientation is preferred.

    Design for Quality (DFQ) Principles and Examples

    Major automotive companies stress the following basic Total Quality Excellence (TQE) principles that give the foundation to our discussion on design for quality and on various techniques for improving product quality by preventing defects:

    • Quality is defined by the customer. The customer wants products and services that throughout their lifetime meet his or her needs and expectations at a cost that represents value.
    • Quality excellence can best be achieved by preventing problems rather than by detecting and correcting them after they occur.
    • All work that is done by company employees, suppliers and dealers is part of a process that creates a product or service for the customer. Each person can influence some part of that process and therefore affect the quality of its output and ultimate customer satisfaction with the products and services offered.
    • Sustained quality excellence requires continuous product and process improvement. This means that regardless of how good the present performance may be, it can become better.
    • People provide the intelligence and generate the actions that are necessary to realise these improvements.
    • Each employee is a customer with a right to expect good quality work from others and an obligation to contribute work of high calibre to those who in turn are his or her customers.

    To summarize Ford's philosophy, it reflects customer focus, continuous process analysis and improvement on all fronts, people as the key to change and delivery and problem prevention by good design and good practices in general.

    In order to develop our generic methods for designing and manufacturing with total quality in mind, let us discuss some of the most important methods and techniques relating to this topic area:

    • Design for quality principles and the main steps of quality control, or KANRI.
    • Improvement techniques, often referred to as KAISEN.
    • Design for manufacturing and improving product quality by preventing defects (Poka-Yoke).
    • Total Quality Management (TQM) techniques and Quality Circles.

    It must be noted, that the techniques and management methods to be discussed in this section can be successfully applied to a wide range of technical and non-technical areas.

    Quality Control, steps of control, or KANRI

    Quality Control is the effort of an entire enterprise to develop, design, manufacture, inspect, market and service products that will satisfy the customers at the time of purchase and give them satisfaction for a long time after purchase. The word "quality" applies not only to products but also services, such as design, sales and maintenance.

    In order to perform Quality Control (QC) functions effectively throughout all aspects and phases of the enterprise activities all personnel including executives, managers, engineers, administrators, foreman and workers and operators need to participate and collaborate. As the Japanese put it: "The operator's only concern should be quality, not productivity. The foreman on the other hand must deal with productivity as well as quality!"

    Steps of control, or in Japanese KANRI means the continuous check of operations and processes for the sake of complying with standards and well tested instructions. KANRI (originally taught by Deming to the Japanese during his very fist visits to Japan) includes four steps, these are:

    1. Plan. Set the criteria of control, the variables and the objectives

    2. Do. Ensure that the operation is carried out according to the rules and standards

    3. Check. Check/study the condition and the results of an operation

    4. Action. Take actions based on the result of the previous step

    Let us discuss each of these steps in some more detail.

    It is worthwhile remembering that the structured specification and modelling techniques, such as DFD, IDEFo and CIMpgr, are all based on processes and activities, the main target areas of KANRI. For example, the process definitions in a CIMpgr diagram could be tested for accuracy and correctness using the Plan, Do, Check and Action steps.

    Plan! As with most things the first step always should be planning and setting the objectives. Plan means: What changes might be desirable? What data are available? Are new observations needed? How are observations to be used? In terms of KANRI this means the following:

    • Set the criteria of control, set the variables and the objectives  (e.g. quality, cost, delivery time, safety, quantity). Set rules that define the criteria accurately (e.g. define acceptable quality, define when is a component defective, or reworkable, define delivery times). Set the criteria what quantity means, or what safety means
    • Define the methods required to achieve the set criteria of control. Typical examples include the following:
      • - Specify materials and components
      • - Determine methods of measurement, inspection and test
      • - Determine methods for testing components and/or activities, and/or processes during the processes (e.g. in-cycle gauging, etc.) as well as at the end of the processes (e.g. testing a part on a Co-ordinate Measuring Machine)
      • - Specify machines, equipment, instruments, jigs, fixtures, tools, etc.
      • - Specify activities and processes, operations and their conditions

    As a general rule be "problem conscious" and follow clearly defined rules. Observe and analyse the "as is" system, i.e. the current status of the operations or activities. Collect data accurately. Learn to listen. Collect ideas for improvement. Analyse the factors affecting the process or activity.

    Do! Within this step the Concurrent Engineering team needs to ensure that an operation or activity is carried out according to the rules and standards. In terms of KANRI this means the following:

    • Train the appropriate methods and standards to perform the operation or activity properly
    • Perform the operation or activity. Share the problems with everyone (Remember, there are no problems, only opportunities for improvement)
    • Collect data on the operation or activity according to the performance criteria set
    • Check! or Study! Check means: Study the results. What did we learn? What went wrong? What went right? What can be predicted? and How? In terms of KANRI this means checking and evaluating the conditions and the results of the operation or activity as follows:
    • Check whether or not the operation or activity was carried out according to the standards and rules set earlier
    • Check specified parameters and their values and compare the results against standards and rules set and expected values. (Use statistical methods and experimental design - Taguchi method).

    Act! or Action! This is the fourth step and is closing the first iteration of the loop. Note, that usually more than one iteration is required. This means: take actions based on the result of the previous step, i.e. adopt the change or abandon it (then repeat the Plan - Do - Check - Act cycle with accumulated knowledge). In terms of KANRI this can be interpreted as follows:

    • Take remedial measures if an operation is deviating from the previously set standards and rules
    • Analyse, identify and eliminate the causes of abnormal results. Take actions faithfully and promptly to avoid the recurrence of the same cause in the future. Note, that accurate records and statistical analysis of collected data can often help to prevent faults because they can show the level of abnormality
    • Take actions to improve the entire process or activity as defined by the four KANRI steps of control. Establish a feedback-control system and prevent faults by continuously monitoring and/or controlling sensitive parameters

     As one can see the difficult aspects of this four step analysis include setting the objectives and variables to control and then to actually capture the data promptly and take action as necessary. Since control parameters are often outside the range of a team's responsibility one should make sure that both inside and outside suppliers are aware of the importance of the value of the particular parameter. (Note, that this is one of those areas in which ISO9001 accreditation can be of extreme value to a corporation.)

    The other very important issue here is to make sure that the required data is collected accurately and on time. It is no good finding out faults a few days later when the products have already left the factory or design office.

    Gradual Product Design and Process Improvement Techniques, or KAISEN

    There is no doubt that our Japanese colleagues are the masters of gradual improvement and we should collectively learn from each other. As they define it in Japan 'Improvement is an attempt to breakthrough from the present, and control is an effort to prevent slip-off from an improved stage. These two complement each other as two wheels of the same cart to create a better workshop.'

    The key aspects of gradual and systematic improvement are as follows:

    1. Be problem conscious. There is no shortage of problems in any office, or factory, or system. Accept problems as opportunities for improvements. Not just the managers, but all members of the organisation should be problem conscious. Start in your own area and gradually expand cutting down on waste.

    2. Act as a team. The wisdom of all should be brought together to solve a problem and act effectively. Awareness and sensitivity to what is happening is crucial. Joint lunches, Quality Circles (discussed in more detail later), team meetings, etc. are crucial in terms of developing trust.

    3. Identify problems. The Japanese call it MUDA (meaning waste), MURA (irregularity) and MURI (strain). The idea of course is to eliminate MUDA in terms of people, machines, materials and methods

    Due to the importance of MUDA let us discuss the basic principles in some detail concentrating in particular on the teamwork aspects of Concurrent Engineering and the way the Japanese identify problems and act upon them.

    MUDA

    MUDA means waste. It means many different types of waste, including: unbalanced workload, long waiting times and uncoordinated action plans between departments, as well as waste in terms of, "is the right man doing the right job?"

    MURA

    MURA means irregularity, or differences and variability caused by men.

    Are individual differences wider than normal? Is co-ordination between workers causing unnecessary irregularities?

    Is the workload evenly distributed? Are the peaks preventing workers and managers from performing their jobs properly?

    Is the workload and the type of work balanced in terms of skills, responsibilities and authority? Is proper training provided?

    MURI

    MURI means stress and strain. It reflects on problem areas such as: Is there a shortage of manpower? Is there an opportunity to simplify the operation unloading strain of the operators?

    Is there an opportunity to reduce organisational strain in the design and manufacturing office by organising work and activities better? Is manpower and staffing acceptable?

    Is there a physical strain (e.g. posture, environmental pollution and noise)?

    Design for Manufacturing and Improving Product Quality by Preventing Defects (Poka-Yoke)

    Poka-Yoke is a technique for avoiding simple human error and aiming for zero defects at all levels of work. (Poka-Yoke means "mistake-proofing" or "fail-safing".)

    Although the concept existed for a long time, it was a Japanese manufacturing engineer, Shiego Shingo who developed the idea into a practical methodology.

    The fundamental concept of Poka-Yoke is to respect the intelligence, knowledge and experience of machine operators and assembly line workers, designers and manufacturing engineers trying to eliminate the possibility of failure, or defects through design and sound practices.

    Poka-Yoke underlines the fact that a good design engineer cannot sit behind closed doors any more, because he, or she needs to know a lot about processes and manufacturing methods too.

    Poka-Yoka is emphasising very much the same goals as JIT, (Just-in-time methods), in that 'it is not acceptable to produce even small number of defective components, or products because defects are wasteful' (see our discussion on MUDA again). Poka-Yoke claims that errors can be avoided, although people and machines can make mistakes.

    The fundamental concept is that any kind of mistake is made by people and that people make fewer mistakes if they are supported by proper training and by a manufacturing/assembly system which is designed on the basis that errors can always be prevented by sound design methods and experienced people.

    Note, that in this sense sampling inspection is by definition not adequate since it allows a percentage of defects however small that defect is. Imagine, a customer getting the odd defective pacemaker, even if it is the only defective product out of a million. Obviously this is a major problem! Poka-Yoka emphasises that one needs to inspect products 100% as they are created, manufactured, assembled, etc. using simple in-process techniques that work in practice.

    Poka-Yoke is a technique that can be applied to many different areas of design, manufacture and even services. These are:

    • Source inspection. Avoid defective parts or components entering the next process.
    • 100% inspection. Avoid defective parts by continuously inspecting all processes.
    • If an error is detected stop the process immediately ("If there is a good reason for it, you may stop the line...").

    All of the above groups focus on human related errors. For example omitted processes, processing errors, errors setting up the machines or workpieces, missing part or using the wrong part, adjustment errors, improperly designed and/or prepared tools and fixtures and others.

    These can be grouped as follows:

    Errors due to a misunderstanding, e.g. mistakes made, because we jump to the wrong conclusions, before we understand the situation or problem fully. Possible cure: Training, standardisation of procedures, checking the problem in advance

    Errors made due to absentmindedness. These are mistakes without knowing that they have happened. Possible cure: Discipline, training, good organisation of time and workplace, checklists, error detection and alarms, limit switches and counters at the assembly line

    Forgetfulness. These are errors mostly due to the lack of concentration. Unfortunately they can cause major disasters. Possible cure: Alerting the operator in advance, checking at various intervals, discipline and training

    Errors made in identification, e.g. a label is misread, or two similar parts are mixed up. Possible cure: Vigilance, discipline, training, fail-safe design, error detection and alarms

    Errors due to tiredness or slowness. These are errors caused by actions slowed down due to tiredness or an in-ability to make quick decisions. Possible cure:  Work organisation and load analysis, discipline, training, work standardisation

    Errors due to a lack of standards. If there are no reference values for measured data, or if there are no benchmarked solutions/procedures, errors can occur. Possible cure: Standardisation, accurate instructions, discipline and training in measuring the appropriate data

    Panic and surprise errors, e.g. machine malfunctions, line stoppages, computer break downs, etc. without prior warning. Possible cure: Preventive maintenance, training, safeguards, fail-safe design, well rehearsed procedures

    Errors due to the lack of experience or training, e.g. a new operator is not familiar with the settings, etc. Possible cure: Training, checklists, error detection and alarms, limit switches and counters

    Deliberate errors. Unfortunately some people in some circumstances make deliberate errors either by ignoring regulations and safety guidelines or by other means and can cause serious damage to other operators or to machines.

    Possible cure: Fundamental education and training, circumstance analysis by the appropriate experts, or as a last resort removal of the operator, or technician, or engineer, or manager

    To summarize, mistakes can happen for many reasons, but in almost all cases they can be prevented if we make the necessary time and effort to prevent them. We illustrate the above Poka-Yoke principles with practical examples. Try to apply them in your own organisation.

    Concurrent Engineering team management techniques and the role of the Quality Circles

    In order to achieve good quality output that satisfies both the inside as well as the outside customers the Concurrent Engineering team must be managed well and must look after its own quality of work. One way of dealing with this is through Quality Circles.

    Through Quality Circle activities people get together and openly talk to each other in a pleasant and relaxed atmosphere and try to discuss and solve daily or longer term problems. As the Japanese put it one of the most important objectives of such activities is "to respect humanity and build a happy and bright workshop".

    Quality Circle activities attempt to promote quality control in the workshop and at all levels of the enterprise, including the Concurrent Engineering offices and workshops. Quality Control itself is the effort of an entire enterprise to develop, design, manufacture, inspect, market and service products that will satisfy the customers at the time of purchase and give them satisfaction for a long time after purchase. The word "quality" applies to not only products but also services, such as sales and maintenance.

    The most important success factors of Quality Circle activities, with particular interest to Concurrent Engineering include the following:

    • Self development. Note the amount of industrial and product design, hardware, software and manufacturing process/quality related knowledge a good Concurrent Engineer working in a CIM team must keep up with.
    • Voluntary activities are crucial.
    • Group activities are beneficial to develop respect and team spirit.
    • Participation by everyone is important.
    • Application of quality control techniques and statistical methods, in particular for trend analysis, are vital, and the acceptance of QC activities as a form of feedback control is vital.
    • Mutual development and creativity (e.g. brainstorming and team problem solving) is essential.
    • Quality consciousness, problem consciousness, and a positively critical attitude is healthy and motivating.

    These aspects are essential at all stages of the Quality Circle development process. Note, that it should be a self-development process, not a stage, or forced activity.

    To summarize, with design for manufacture, assembly, quality and maintenance, we are dealing with a very complex research and development problem which includes design, manufacturing, organisational and human aspects too.

    In order to be able to deal with the complexity, we often need to change the attitudes and the practices of the accountants, the marketing, design, manufacturing, quality control and maintenance engineers, as well as the customers in order to make sure that there is a better understanding between these disciplines resulting in better products to everybody's benefit.
