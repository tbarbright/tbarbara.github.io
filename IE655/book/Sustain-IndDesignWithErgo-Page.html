Introduction and Some Definitions

Industrial design can be defined as a combination of applied art and applied science, whereby the aesthetics and usability of products may be improved.

Ergonomics is the science of designing the job, equipment, and workplace to fit the worker, or in general terms the user. Proper ergonomic design is necessary to increase user-satisfaction, as well as to prevent repetitive strain injuries, which can develop over time and can lead to long-term disability.

In the case of very successful, high-quality product designs there is a strong relationship between industrial design and ergonomics (or Human Factors Engineering, for our purposes meaning the same).

By applying the principles and methods of human factors, consumer research, industrial design, and others, this profession is dedicated to developing consumer products that are useful, usable, safe, and desirable in the marketplace.

There is some discussion of whether the term 'human factors', or 'human factors engineering' differs from 'ergonomics' – whether one relies more heavily on psychology or physiology, or if one is more theoretical or practical than the other...

Since at the time of writing, the two terms are usually considered synonyms, in this text they'll be used as synonyms.

The origin of the term ergonomics is Greek. Ergo means 'work' and 'nomos' means law. It is interesting to mention, that in Canada they use the term 'ergonomics' more, a French term, whereas in the Anglo-saxon world they use the term human factors more (an English term).

Note, that for our purposes we'll use both terms, meaning the same definition.

Ergonomics is a very important subject area. There are several new developments, such as Honda's revolutionary (and still prototype) assistive walking and climbind devices, as well as others.

There are many examples in the real world that illustrate how well or how badly things can go wrong when, amongst others, the design team does not consider the importance of integrating industrial design and ergonomics concepts and principles into their designs (www.baddesigns.com, copyright © Michael J. Darnell 1996-2006).

Unfortunately, in most universities around the world, industrial design with ergonomics as part of design, is not taught to mechanical, electronic, industrial and many other engineers. For some reason many academics believe, that industrial design belongs to the subject area of architecture... not at all. As an excellent example, Apple Inc. has clearly demonstrated to the world how important the integration of industrial design and ergonomics is, without forgetting high quality and reliability too.

Furthermore successful product designers must follow sustainable green design solutions. In product and process design, sustainable green means waste reduction and optimization, and therefore advocates lean six-sigma concepts and methods.

In terms of green sustainability we consider an eco-friendly state, or process that can be maintained over time, for a very long time.

Also, consider the ability of an ecosystem to maintain ecological design and manufacturing processes and functions, biological diversity, and productivity over time.

To achieve high quality industrial design with ergonomics in mind, all above principles must be followed in integration. As many examples illustrate this, it is not easy to achieve this goal.

Some Definitions of Industrial Design

Industrial design can be defined as a combination of applied art and applied science, whereby the aesthetics and usability of products may be improved.

As an example, consider a modern automobile. Industrial designers, automobile stylists, hardware and software engineers, marketing and engineering managers must work together to achieve the desired level of integration between art, applied science, aesthetics, usability, safety, sustainable quality and cost of a new automobile, amongst others.

Also, industrial design as a discipline can be defined as a blend of functionality, fashion, applied art, architecture and engineering with an attempt to develop physical (and/or virtual, i.e. computer generated simulation, or virtual reality) solutions to meet particular needs.

Note, that simulation, CAD (Computer Aided Design), material selection, the analysis of physical properties, the analysis of behavior in different conditions, dynamic load analysis, stress analysis using Finite Element methods, heat and vibration analysis, virtual, simulated crash tests of automobiles and aircraft, and many others are essential analytical methods and tools not just for the hard core design engineers, but also for the ergonomically focused industrial designers.

Industrial design is an applied art whereby the aesthetics and usability of products may be improved for marketability and production. The role of an industrial designer is to create and execute design solutions towards problems of engineering, marketing, brand development and sales.

Industrial design focuses on visual features of shape, configuration, pattern or ornament applied to a manufactured article.

Protecting an industrial design is extremely important. Industrial design registration provides some protection against imitation and unauthorized use of a design. Design rights related laws are similar to copyright laws. (Canadian Intellectual Property Office, www.tbs-sct.gc.ca/cioscripts/gloss/gloss-alpha_e.asp).

Unfortunately in our global world, entire factories and even nations' economies are built on counterfeiting famous designs, or copying them, making minor modifications, and marketing them as alternative products, sold at half price or even less.

These nations claim, that they obey international design rights, copyright laws and IP (Intellectual Property rights and laws), but in reality these laws are not enforced, thus counterfeiting prospers as big business, financially supporting those who supposed to enforce the law, not to do so...

The problem is, that whilst popular and fashionable products become less expensive like this, their quality is typically much poorer. This leads to brandname degradation and value loss; something that is bad for all, even for those who counterfeit!

Also, this process destroys innovation because hard working, talented innovators cannot survive in this distorted and corrupt system...

One should be very clear though, that these counterfeiting nations are after innovation and talent... yes, they want the innovators' brains and talent. This is because innovation is one of the most valuable 'products' mankind can ever produce... more valuable than oil, gold, dimonds, or even platinum...

This is one key reason why industrial designs must be protected for everybody's benefit; both the inventors', the designers', the manufacturers', as well as the customers'...

A good industrial design optimizes the function, value, and appearance of a product or system for the benefit of the human user. It includes features such as shape, configuration, pattern, or ornamentation, including any combination of these features (esearch.carleton.ca/htr/defs.php).

The purpose of sustainable industrial design with ergonomics is to ensure that goods satisfy the demands of human users, including fashion, style, function, materials, quality, safety, sustainable green engineering criteria and cost.

Industrial design became a fully fledged professional activity in the early 20th century through the efforts of the pioneering US industrial designers who worked with the large-scale manufacturers of the new mass-produced technological goods – General Electric, Ford Motor Company, Eastman Kodak, Gestetner, and others. From the USA the profession moved across the Atlantic in the years after 1945.

It is easy to see the enormous change our world has gone through due to industrial design with ergonomics, and others, when we analyze old automobile designs, old kitchen tool and domestic appliance designs, bicycle designs, ski designs, and others.

In Europe, France, Germany, Austria and Italy made special contributions to the evolution of industrial design after 1945, the end of WWII, and by the 1980s all the main industrialized countries had their own industrial design professions, including Japan and some other Asian countries where market economies were under development.

It is also interesting to notice, that in countries where free market economies were illegal and depressed both industrial design and ergonomics were depressed too as fundamental principles.

This is because in these countries basic functionality was the accepted norm, since there was a shortage of almost everything all the time, and there was no competition... the state of course always knew how much was needed of anything and the customer base had basically no choice...

Since the 1980s in particular, the important trend to notice in the free market economies, is that not only mobile phones, computers, televisions, automobiles, buildings, furniture, clothing, washing machines, and others, but also industrial machinery, such as robots, machine tools, material handling systems, and other industrial equipment is hugely influenced by both industrial design, as well as ergonomic principles.

This is because in a modern, open and free society we must focus not just on how time is spent at home, but also at the workplace as well as in public areas.

The more enjoyable, safer, sustainable, better quality experience we can create in these domains, the more fun the workers will have, the more innovative and productive they'll become, and as a result everybody wins.

Some Definitions of Ergonomics, or Human Factors Engineering, with Some Examples

There are several useful definitions for Ergonomics, or Human Factors Engineering. These include the following:

Ergonomics is a branch of engineering science in which biological science is used to study the relation between workers and their environments (wordnetweb.princeton.edu/perl/webwn).

Human factors engineering attempts to discover and apply information about human behaviors, abilities, limitations and other characteristics to the design of tools, machines, systems, tasks, jobs, procedures, and environments for productive, safe, comfortable, and effective human use.

As good examples, consider the importance how humans interact with simple hand tools, and power tools such as electric screwdrivers, drills, circular saws, jigsaws, and others.

Also, think of how drivers and passengers interact with the interior of an automobile, such as the automobile's dashboard, steering wheel, temperature controls, radio and entertainment controls, GPS controls and others.

As other examples, study how we interact with products and objects / things, as we walk through a city, as we use the transportation systems, the stores, buildings and public space available.

Then think of our personal space at home how we interact with and use our furniture, our domestic appliances, computers, the mouse, the keyboard, trackpads and many others...

As you analyze the above outlined experiences you'll find excellent examples demonstrating how important sustainable industrial design with ergonomics is in every aspect of our lives.

The Key Objectives of Ergonomics

The key objectives of human factors engineering / ergonomics is to address the following interrelated areas:

  • Enhance both the effectiveness as well as the efficiency levels at which processes are completed (e.g. ease of use, reduced operator errors, increased human as well as machine productivity)
  • Enhance certain desirable human values (e.g. improved safety / reduced risk, reduced fatigue and stress, increased job satisfaction, better quality of life)

More, on What Ergonomics Is...

Ergonomics is the science of designing the job, equipment, and workplace to fit the worker. Proper ergonomic design is necessary to prevent repetitive strain injuries, which can develop over time and can lead to long-term disability (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergonomics).

Ergonomics is the science of the design of equipment, especially so as to reduce operator fatigue, discomfort and injury (en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ergonomics).

Ergonomics is the study of the 'fit' between human operators and their workplace, which can be used to design working environments that maximise user efficiency (www.tuition.com.hk/psychology/e.htm).

The scientific study and analysis of the human, the machine, and/or working environment interface and an investigation of those elements in the system that affect optimum human performance on a given task or set of tasks (www.dit.ie/services/healthsafety/glossaryofterms/),

Ergonomics is a discipline that involves fitting the job to the worker and not the worker to the job. It is the science of adapting workstations, tools, equipment and job practices to be compatible with the individual worker and thus reduce the risk of injury due to risk factors (www.workriteergo.com/ergonomics/glossary.asp).

Ergonomics is an application of scientific information concerning humans, such as behavioural and biological information, to the design of objects, systems and environment to ensure their safe, efficient and convenient use by people; (Adapted from the definition used by the British Ergonomics Society www.iso.org/sites/ConsumersStandards/en/5-glossary-terms.html).

Ergonomics is the science that deals with the interaction between people, their work place and the environment. It also considers the physiology of workers in the design of tools, equipment, and the work methods needed to conduct their activity safely (www.sfsa.org/sfsa/glossary/deftrmee.php).

The scientific foundation, both in terms of data and methodology, for a user-centred approach to design. Knowledge and methods that aim to develop product, equipment, furniture and environment to fit the user's capabilities and to promote user safety (herkules.oulu.fi/isbn9514259378/html/g240.html).

The applied science involving the factors and interaction of the workplace environment on its workers. Although it is most often associated with automation in the workplace, this science covers the cause and effect of any workplace environment (www.j6insurance.com/glossary.asp).

Ergonomics is the science of obtaining a correct match between the human body, work-related tasks, and work tools (www.muschealth.com/gs/glossary.aspx).

As a summary, we should not forget, that ergonomics should be an integral part of every design that interacts with humans, including children.

The toy business is a multi billion US Dollar business just in the USA. Even more so in the world. Most importantly, toys can be dangerous to children as well as to the environment (e.g. toys that are choked upon by small children because of their size, shape and form factor, or lead, or cadmium in the paint used on toys, that are toxic, and others).

This is why the science of obtaining a correct match and balance between the human body, adult or child, work, or play-related tasks is extremely important for every designer.

Ergonomics is a Scientific Approach to Problem Solving

In order to fully apperciate human factors engineering, one needs to study, amongst others, the following:

1. The anatomical and mechanical structure of the human body

  • Anthropometry: the measurement of human body
  • Human Biomechanics: the characteristics of the body in mechanical terms
  • Human skeletal system analysis

2. How the body works

  • Respiratory system
  • Circulatory system
  • Metabolic system

3. Assessment of physical work capacity

4. Energy expenditure & energy requirement

5. How does the mind work?

  • Nervous system, information processor
  • Artificial Neural Network (ANN)
  • Response time

6. How the five senses work? (These are: seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching and haptics devices)

7. How does the body interact with the environment? (Reaction to heat, cold environment, polluted air, high altitude, underwater, vibration, microgravity, acceleration, diet, and others).

8. Body rhythms, work schedule, the effects of diet and alcohol, daily rhythms of work, leasure and sleep, multiple shift work (in particular night shifts and sleeping habits), lighting and sound / noise conditions at work, and many others.

As a science subject, ergonomics / human factors engineering represents a systematic approach to the organization and research of relevant information about human capabilities, limitations, characteristics, behaviors, and motivation in relationship to the design of products, objects, or in other words, things, as well as the processes involved with the use of such products and things in different environments.

Ergonomics follows scientific investigation methods to discover relevant information about humans and their responses to products, processes, objects, or things, within different environments.

The result of such scientific investigation methods provides the basis for design recommendations, design rules, reduced risk procedures, simulation and CAD/CAM analysis methods, for trying to predict possible effects of various design alternatives.

Ergonomics also offers ways to evaluate products / processes, things we design to ensure that they satisfy their intended objectives.

Ergonomics as a Profession

Ergonomics as a profession is an important committment of the following:

  • Recognition of individual differences in human capabilities and limitations
  • Reliance on scientific method and procedures that acknowledge that products, processes, environments (including natural habitats), and people do not exist in isolation
  • Appreciation for the unique design implications (dealing with user interface)
  • Conviction that design of things, procedures or methods influence health and safety
  • Emphasis on empirical data and evaluation in the design process
  • A systematic application of relevant information about humans capabilities, limitations, characteristics, behaviors, and motivation
  • Procedures people use and the environment in which they use them
  • Others.

As an example, The Augmented Cognition Technical (Ergonomics) Group is concerned with fostering the development and application of

  • Real-time physiological and neurophysiological sensing technologies that can ascertain a human’s cognitive state while interacting with computing-based systems
  • Data classification and integration architectures that enable closed-loop system applications; mitigation (adaptive) strategies that enable efficient and effective system adaptation based on a user’s dynamically changing cognitive state
  • Individually tailored training systems
  • Roadmaps for future directions concerning augmented cognition science and technology and guidelines of use for the technology and the user information that may be garnered from it

As another example, The Environmental Design Technical (Ergonomics) Group is concerned with the relationship between human behavior and the designed environment.

Common areas of research and interest include ergonomic and macroergonomic aspects of design within the home, office, and industrial settings.

An overall objective of this group is to foster and encourage the integration of ergonomics principles into the design of different environments.

As a summary, note, that many of these fascinating R&D areas listed above are outside the scope of this publication, nevertheless we should all be aware of their importance.

Ergonomics is a wide and complex field of science. It incorporates various types of engineering, psychology, medical sciences, medicine, physiology, life sciences, education, industrial design, project management, business administration, computer science, information technology / information systems design, statistics, physics, and many others, and designers should study these further (www.hfes.org).

Some Definitions of Aesthetics in Industrial Design

As discussed above, industrial design is a combination of applied art and applied science, whereby the aesthetics and usability of mass-produced products may be improved for marketability and production (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_design).

Aesthetics (also spelled æsthetics, or esthetics) is a branch of philosophy dealing with the nature of beauty, art, and taste, and with the creation and appreciation of beauty.

Often aesthetics is defined as a "critical reflection on art, culture and nature."

Aesthetics studies new ways of seeing and of perceiving the world.

Aesthetic design is concerned with form, colour, style and the compatibility of an object with its visual environment.

Form includes shape, proportion, balance, texture and finish. Note, that these sattributes should be used as part of the designers' rule set.

Often many of these rules are embedded into a CAD/CAM software system, and even in the way manufacturing processes are chosen... as an example consider a CNC machine's output quality versus traditional methods. (CNC machines can produce accurate geometry as well as high quality surface finish... and they can perform this within very tight tolerances and variation; something that manual methods cannot produce.)

Color, in many cases (e.g. the color of an automobile, or computer, or cell phone cover, or iPad, or even a toy) is an emotional area and it is often chosen based on intuitive and artistic feelings, rather than hard core engineering principles.

In other cases, where safety is an issue, color is used to warn, to reeassure, to guide human operators.

It is true to state, that judgments of aesthetic value rely on our ability to discriminate at a sensory level.

Aesthetics examines our affective domain response to an object or phenomenon. Immanuel Kant, writing in 1790, observes that "Everyone has his own (sense of) taste". The case of "beauty" is different from mere "agreeableness" because, "If he proclaims something to be beautiful, then he requires the same liking from others; he then judges not just for himself but for everyone, and speaks of beauty as if it were a property of things."

Judgments of aesthetic value seem often to involve many other kinds of issues as well.

Responses such as disgust show that sensory detection is linked in instinctual ways to facial expressions, and even behaviors like the gag reflex.

Yet disgust can often be a learned or cultural issue too; as Darwin pointed out, seeing a stripe of soup in a man's beard is disgusting even though neither soup nor beards are themselves disgusting.

Not just humans, but also machines, furniture, kitchen tools, transportation equipment and others can look disgusting... this is because often they cannot be cleaned properly... and this is a design fault. Designers should consider the entire lifecycle of a product and think and plan for maintenance too.

Aesthetic judgments may be linked to emotions or, like emotions, partially embodied in our physical reactions.

Likewise, aesthetic judgments may be culturally conditioned to some extent. As an example, Victorians in Britain often saw African sculpture as ugly, but just a few decades later, Edwardian audiences saw the same sculptures as being beautiful and full of life.

Beauty, and evaluations of beauty may well be linked to desirability, perhaps even to sexual desirability. Thus, judgments of aesthetic value can become linked to judgments of economic, political, or moral value.

As an example, an automobile can be beautiful partly because it is desirable as a status symbol, or because it signifies for us over-consumption and offends our political or moral values.

In the literature, a third major topic in the study of aesthetic judgments is how they are unified across art forms. Can we call a person, a building, a song, a dance, an automobile, a painting, a dive resort, or a mathematical proof beautiful?

What characteristics / attributes do they share which gives them that status?

It is clear, that each art form has its own language for the judgement of aesthetics, and that the borderlines are not sharply divided, since they are different experessions of humanity, our feelings, emotions, needs and desires.

Some Definitions of Usability in Industrial Design

As discussed above, industrial design is a combination of applied art and applied science, whereby the aesthetics and usability of products may be improved for marketability and production (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_design).

In design, usability is the study of the ease with which people can employ a particular tool or other human-made physical or virtual object (including software objects) in order to achieve a particular goal.

This can include endeavors as varied as hardware and software, related to computers, automobiles, watches, consumer electronics, communication, knowledge transfer objects (such as a cookbook, a document or online help, or this eBook), and mechanical objects such as a kitchen tool, a hand tool, sport equipment, door handles, and many others.

Usability includes the methods of measuring usability and the study of the principles behind an object's perceived efficiency or elegance.

In human-computer interaction and computer science, usability studies the elegance and clarity with which the interaction with data and information, a computer program, or a web site is designed.

Usability differs from user satisfaction insofar as usability also embraces usefulness.

With usability in mind, the focus is to make a product, process or thing:

  • More efficient to use (i.e. more efficient here means, that it takes less time to accomplish a particular task; consider backing up data on a mainframe computer's tape drive, versus on a moderrn laptop's external hard disk... what a huge difference in efficiency!)
  • Easier to learn (i.e. a process can be learned with less energy, aggrevation, and frustration. As examples consider the user interfaces of different web sites and different browsers, or DOS versus an Apple Mac interface...)
  • More satisfying to use (i.e. better fitness for purpose as defined by different users. As an example, consider a modern digital camera interface versus and early generation digital camera interface usability struggles...)

The huge success of graphical computer and mobile device interfaces demonstrate that usability is becoming increasingly popular and widely recognized as companies see the benefits of researching and developing their products with user-oriented instead of technology-oriented methods that ignore usability concerns.

By understanding and researching the interaction between product and user, the usability expert can also provide insight that is unattainable by traditional company-oriented market research.

For example, after observing and interviewing users, the usability expert may identify needed functionality or design flaws that were not anticipated.

A method called "contextual inquiry" does this in the naturally occurring context of the users own environment. In the user-centered design paradigm, the product is designed with its intended users in mind at all times. In the user-driven or participatory design paradigm, some of the users become actual or de facto members of the design team.

The term user friendly is often used as a synonym for usable, though it may also refer to accessibility. Usability is also used to describe the quality of user experience across websites, software, products and environments.

ISO defines usability as "the extent to which a product can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals with effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction in a specified context of use."

Usability is a qualitative attribute that assesses how easy user interfaces are to use. The word "usability" also refers to methods for improving ease-of-use during the design process.

Usability consultant Jakob Nielsen and computer science professor Ben Shneiderman have written (separately) about a framework of system acceptability, where usability is a part of "usefulness" and is composed of. They consider the folowing attributes:

  • Learnability: How easy is it for users to accomplish basic tasks the first time they encounter the design?
  • Efficiency: Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they perform tasks?
  • Memorability: When users return to the design after a period of not using it, how easily can they re establish proficiency?
  • Errors: How many errors do users make, how severe are these errors, and how easily can they recover from the errors? (One should note here, that errors are also made by software engineers... such errors and bugs often lead to user errors that otherwise could have been avoided...)
  • Satisfaction: How pleasant is it to use the design?

Usability is often associated with the functionalities of the product controlled by law. For example an automobile lacking a reverse gear, or a brake, or a fog light is considered unusable according to the laws in most countries.

When evaluating user interfaces for usability, the definition can be as simple as "the perception of a target user of the effectiveness (fit for purpose) and efficiency (work or time required to use) of the Interface".

It is important to distinguish between usability testing and usability engineering.

Usability testing is the measurement of ease of use of a product or software. In contrast, usability engineering (UE) is the research and design process that ensures a product with good usability.

Usability is an example of a non-functional requirement. As with other non-functional requirements, usability cannot be directly measured but must be quantified by means of indirect measures or attributes such as, for example, the number of reported problems with ease-of-use of a system. This is not a simple task, since users and their experiences can be very different.

Essential Usability Considerations

In order to help the design community to create usable systems, let us list some of the essential questions designers (i.e. industrial designers and software engineers with ergonomics in mind) should be clear about before working on a design.

(As you read this section, also consider what we state in our CORA - Component Oriented Requirements Analysis, discussions in Chapter 8.)

  • What do users want or need to do with the machine / tool / software / website they interact with?
  • Who are the users, what do they know, what do they not know, should they learn what they do not know, and what can they learn?
  • What is the general background / educational standing / experience level of the users?
  • What is the environment in which the user is working with the machine?
  • What should the operators do and what should be left to the machine to do? How is this interaction and job sharing / control sharing activity communicated by the machine to the user /operator?
  • Can users easily accomplish their intended tasks? For example, can users accomplish intended tasks at their intended speed? Think of human operators working with robots together... maybe robots can work faster, but should the operators be forced to increase their speed? And if so what are the consequences in either way?
  • How much training do users need? What documentation or other supporting materials are available to help users? Can users find the solutions they seek in these resources?
  • What and how many errors do users / operators make as they interact with the product / machine? Can the user recover from errors?
  • What do users have to do to recover from errors? Does the product help users / operators to recover from errors? For example, does the control software of a CNC milling machine, or a robot present comprehensible, informative, non-threatening error messages that trained operators can easily understand?
  • Are there provisions for meeting the special needs of users with disabilities / accessibility challenges?
  • Are there substantial differences between the cognitive approaches of various users that will affect the design or can a one size fits all approach be used? Should there be a context sensitive and dynamically adjusting interface design, that presents only relevant information for the human decision maker?
  • Should the advanced user / operator be presented with shortcuts, that can create risky situations if there is a human error? (As an example, think of editing a robot control program whilst the robot is executing the program... this is often technically possible, but is this safe practice?)
  • Does product packaging influence usability? Is it important to create the product package design together with the product design?

As you read this section, also consider what other questions of usability industrial designers with ergonomics should raise before working out a design in more detail.

Some Definitions of Fashion and Art in the Context of Industrial Design with Ergonomics

Fashion is often defined as the characteristic or habitual practice, or the latest and most admired style in clothes and cosmetics and behavior, or consumer goods (especially clothing) in the current mode (wordnetweb.princeton.edu/perl/webwn)

Fashion is the style and custom of products, such as clothes, automobiles, computers, mobile devices, architectural and furniture creations, music and entertainment productions and many others, prevalent at a given time. In its most common usage however, "fashion" describes the popular clothing style.

Many fashions are popular in many cultures at any given time. (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fashion).

In terms of global design aspects it is important to note, that if one product is fashionable in one culture, it does not mean that it is also fashionable in another culture. (Note, that this has historical, political, cultural and often even religious reasons.)

The important conclusion is, that fashion is time, culture, health and safety, hygiene and technology related, just to name a few, and therefore is to be considered when dealing with the subject area of sustainable green industrial design with ergonomics.

Art can be defined as the products of human creativity; works of art collectively; "an art exhibition"; "a fine collection of art".

Also the creation of beautiful or significant things; "art does not need to be innovative to be good"; "I was never any good at art"; "he said that architecture is the art of wasting space beautifully".

A superior skill that you can learn by study and practice and observation; "the art of conversation"; "it's quite an art".

Artwork: photographs or other visual representations in a printed publication; "the publisher was responsible for all the artwork in the book" (ref.:wordnetweb.princeton.edu/perl/webwn).

Art is the process or product of deliberately arranging elements in a way to affect the senses or emotions. It encompasses a diverse range of human activities, creations, and modes of expression, including music, literature, film, sculpture, and paintings (ref. en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art).

Art can be defined as a human endeavor thought to be aesthetic and have meaning beyond simple description.

Includes music, dance, sculpture, painting, drawing, food preparation, architecture, hair styling, stitchery, weaving, poetry, writing, woodworking, engineering, computer interfaces and hardware designs, automotive shape and geometry (e.g. "... a Bentley is a sculpture on wheels...'), and many others (Ranky ed. ref.: oregonstate.edu/instruct/anth370/gloss.html).

What Skills Should Industrial Designers Have?

Industrial designers, together with other engineers, managers and market research experts perform several tasks, including the following:

  • Discuss designs, concepts, ideas and prototypes with engineering, ergonomics, marketing, manufacturing, sales and other experts, including potential customers and vendors, to establish and evaluate design concepts for manufactured products.
  • Customer requirements analysis, risk analysis, process modeling, simulation, statistical analysis, and thers. (Note our related analytical tools in this eBook.)
  • Coordinate the look, feel and function of product lines for multiple product lifecycles.
  • Design graphic material, including 3D simulations for illustration, or marketing, or advertising and promotions.
  • Create working models, both in the physical as well as in the virtual domain.
  • Direct and coordinate the fabrication of rapid prototypes, additive manufacturing models or samples.
  • Create drawings and specification sheets from sketches and conceptual designs.
  • Modify and refine designs, using working models, as well as simulation models to conform with customer specifications, production limitations, or changes in design trends.
  • Understand and follow sustainability rules when selecting materials and processes for the designs.
  • Understand the basic manufacturing and assembly capabilities needed to manufacture the product lines.
  • Prepare sketches of ideas, detailed drawings, illustrations, artwork, and/or blueprints, using drafting instruments, paints and brushes, as well as CAD tools, animation tools and computer simulation methods and tools.
  • Present designs and reports to customers or design review boards, or committees for approval, and discuss need for modification.
  • Read publications, attend exhibitions, study competing products and design styles and motifs to obtain new perspectives and generate design concepts.
  • Advise corporations on issues involving corporate image projects or problems.
  • Evaluate feasibility of design ideas, based on factors such as appearance, safety, function, serviceability, budget, production costs/methods, and market characteristics.
  • Design and model the packaging and presentation of products.
  • Travel and understand cultures and user behavior in different user communities around the world.
  • Attend international trade shows and understand major new trends in design.
  • Develop outstanding communications skills to extract customer needs and desires.
  • Understand how simple to complex projects can be put together and lead towards successful designs.
  • Others

As can be seen from this list, industrial designers have a very exciting profession and a bright future as humanity, technology and nature blends together to create better quality life.

Examples to Illustrate Usability in Web-site Design

As an interactive exercise, test and evaluate the usability of the shown web- pages. Consider the following: Learnability: What is the key message and how easy is it for users to accomplish basic tasks the first time they encounter the design? Efficiency: Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they perform tasks? Memorability: When users return to the design after a period of not using it, how easily can they re establish proficiency? Errors: How many errors do users make, how severe are these errors, and how easily can they recover from the errors? Satisfaction: How pleasant is it to use the design? Layout and Color Scheme: What would you change in the layout? How dod you like the color scheme?
