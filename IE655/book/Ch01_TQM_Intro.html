What is TQM (Total Quality Management)?

Total Quality Management is an approach to business involvement led by the theme of quality. Quality means fitness for use, therefore quality MUST be a major focus in Concurrent Engineering.

It involves the continual satisfaction of customer requirements at lowest cost by harnessing the efforts of everybody in the company.

Quality assurance means sustaining a system which prevents defects. This includes quality control and quality design engineering.

Quality control means establishing and maintaining specified quality standards of products. (As a technology example review the CMM, Co-ordinate Measuring Machine testing an engine block).

Quality engineering is the establishment and execution of tests to measure product quality and adherence to acceptance criteria.

As often defined quality is the overall level of product /process / service excellence.

It is also quite obvious that without similarly "excellent people, equipment and software" one cannot provide the desired level of quality at the predefined cost.

According to Jaguar Motor Cars, "Quality is making money out of satisfying customers. Quality comes first".

John F. Akers, former CEO of IBM, defines quality as follows: "Quality is everyone's job. Quality is essential merely to stay in the race".

Others claim that: "Quality means putting the customers first every time".

Dr. Akimasa Kurimoto, Yamazaki Machinery states that: "The success or failure of the new business strategies is, in general, attributed to the capability of the company to create its culture in ways that would make the new business strategy work. Quality is a fundamental management philosophy, causing cultural change of mind and attitude. Total quality management is the prime corporate business strategy to facilitate the best blend of management and working practice".

Lucas Engineering & Systems defines Total Quality as follows: "The term Total Quality of Performance relates not just to the technical quality of products but rather to the quality of performance of every function in an organization. This is in recognition of the fact that the quality and cost of a product depends upon its design, the lead times and the reliability of the processes operated by many contributory departments (including design, manufacturing, and administrative), the choice of materials and the effectiveness of supporting structures".

Mike Robson, international quality expert, claims that: "We all want quality and excellence. Excellence is a journey, not a destination. There is no magic to quality".

The conclusion is that every design, manufacturing, IT, service, and other enterprise needs a total quality system which is:

  • Understood by everyone
  • Clearly communicated to everyone
  • Followed by everyone
  • Powerful enough to guide, measure, and quantify to be able to control, and maintain.

The Traditional Versus the TQM Approach

The main challenges when applying traditional quality management philosophy include the following:

  • Traditional quality management focuses on correcting mistakes after they have been made, rather than preventing them in the first place.
  • This philosophy allows mistakes to be made. It actually builds them into every aspect of the system, typically costing around 20% of the turnover of a corporation. (This is a key management problem !)
  • Accepts, that quality has to be sacrificed as the volume and the productivity goes up.
  • As viewed by accountants, it is an expensive add on item of the assembly lines, manufacturing systems, service systems, in broader terms the value chain (... obviously this is ALL WRONG, because this kind of thinking and management actually destroys the business !)

On the contrary, modern quality thinking claims the following:

  • Because Total Quality Management involves every person, product , process and service design aspect and machine / resource of the organization
  • Requires a total commitment, therefore it is not a "quick test and fix" approach

It is a preventive system designed into every aspect of the World Class Design, Manufacturing, IT and Service enterprise, including product design, manufacture, management, service, and others. (In accounting terms costing somewhat less than conventional quality systems, i.e. typically around 10% of the turnover).

To summarize, the methods taught in this 3D eBook publication are relevant not just to TQM and TQC as applied to design, manufacture, test of products, but to a large variety of services too, because they reduce development costs, improve working capital management, and increase customer satisfaction by offering powerful methods and software tools.

Rules for Brainstorming in CE / TQM Teams

Brainstorming in an active team, or as part of an Internet chat-group, is an excellent opportunity to come up with ideas that the TQM team might be able to employ.

Since you'll be probably working in a small real, or virtual team over the web, make sure that everyone in the team understands the basics of brainstorming. These are the rules you should follow:

  1. Clarify the objectives. (In this case focus on CE, TQM and TQC).
  2. Identify all the tasks you have to accomplish as individuals as well as a team, at the beginning of the session.
  3. Put the agreed tasks into a logical order.
  4. Estimate the time and additional resources you will need (e.g. web searching and research time, or meeting time) to accomplish the job.
  5. Allocate responsibility.
  6. Review the entire plan before you start and adjust as necessary.

Having accomplished the above plan, when brainstorming, focus on the following rules:

  1. Keep a relaxed atmosphere by choosing a comfortable, undisturbed environment (like a nice country club, or brach resort, or more realistically a pleasent, and undisturbed office).
  2. Choose a team leader who has some experience in how a session will operate, and who doesn't want to dominate the discussions.
  3. Organize the appropriate team of 3 to 5 or perhaps up-to 7 people. (If you have too few, or too many the intensity of the discussion will suffer!)
  4. Summarize the problem / challenge definition again (see the list above).
  5. Generate as many ideas as possible, even if many of them are totally off-beat, or even sound as crazy at the time. (Free wheeling is encouraged! Do NOT discuss or evaluate any of the ideas in detail at this stage because it will divert the focus of the team).
  6. Give everyone equal opportunity to contribute to the new idea generation process. (Let them warm up, relax and roll...)
  7. Write down every idea... even if it sounds crazy. (You might use them later).
  8. Allow time for ideas to incubate. Brainstorm in sessions of 30 minutes, or an hour with perhaps a few days between them if the team still has some steam...

The Purpose of Concurrent / Simultaneous Engineering (CE)

The purpose of CE methods is to cut product and/or service process development lead time, to reduce waste, to reduce the number of costly product design changes, to improve quality, and to create a sustainable lean and green workflow from concept to integrated product/process design, manufacture, assembly, maintenance, reuse / recycle, and after-sales support, based on analytically established customer requirements, and well-tested process models.

The diagram below clearly illustrates the essence of CE. It is every company's aim to create products, processes and services that are required by customers, that are innovative, represent high quality, and can be introduced to the market at a competitive price. The diagram below clearly illustrates the extremely high cost of product design changes. This waste is typical without deploying CE methods. In this publication we'll learn methods, tools and technologies, that will lead us to innovate, and minimize the number, and therefore the cost of changes during the entire lifecycle of a product. This is the heart of any sustainable greening process and this is the fundamental goal of every creative and quality-focused engineer. Product Life-cycle Cost Reductions with Green CE / PLM

As illustrated below, it is important to understand, that Concurrent Engineering, combined with Product Lifecycle Management (PLM), Total Quality Management, Sustainable Green Focus and Project Management offers major product life-cycle cost reductions.

As you study the graph below, consider the following:

In engineering systems the term 'life-cycle cost' means the sum of all the costs, both recurring as well as non-recurring, related to product, structure, system, process, IT, and/or service during its life span. (A typical life-cycle is illustrated below.)

As you can see from this graph, life-cycles typically start by identifying a customer need, a requirement, a want, a desire, and/or a sound business opportunity, and end with product/process retirement, conversion, re-engineering, recycling and eventual disposal activities. (Note, that in the case of a modern, environmentally friendly, concurrently engineered product/process design system, 85-90% of products are eventually re-used and recycled.)

Since by understanding the underlying drivers and processes of concurrent engineering, the opportunities for saving valuable product/process design and development cost and time are huge. (Note, that the aim is to be close to 100% re-use and re-cycling, and as we will discuss this challenge in this publication, there are already good examples for achieving this goal...)

Besides others, the graph below underlines the fact, that concurrent engineering economic studies, process-by-process cost and environmental (green) analysis, and gain assessments are essential part of the big picture, because they help to analyze and compare alternatives in virtual digital environments. This is costing a lot less than real-world experimentation on the shop floor when the product/process is already in full swing production and the commited costs are already high.

We can furthermore see, that the greatest savings occur at the acquisition phase, in other words around the basic idea to detailed design transition phase. This is why a factory with a future will have to understand and master concurrent engineering and NPI&I (New Product & Process Innovation & Introduction) methods and apply them at all stages of the business to be able to successfully compete.

It is also important to recognize, that as shown below, the commited cummulative life-cycle cost curve increases rapidly during the basic product/process idea to detailed design transition phase. In general, typically 80% of life-cycle costs are 'locked in' at the end of this phase and at the beginning of the 'release design to manufacturing' phase by the decisions made during the requirements analysis, preliminary and detailed design phases. As illustrated by the cummulative, actual life-cycle cost curve, only about 20% of actual costs occur during the acquisition phase, in other words around the basic idea to detailed design transition phase, with about 80% being occured at the product construction, operation phase.

As a conclusion, as shown below, the concurrently engineered NPI&I activity must understand the potential for product / process life-cycle savings in all processes, with their requirements and risks involved, their time and cost demands, and then simulate these issues in advanced virtual digital design and manufacturing (Green PLM) environments, and take decisions as early as possible to avoid dead-end paths and costly changes. (Note, that this statement is true for every product/process, IT, and service product development activity.)

The Dimensions of Design Quality in the CE Context

Dimensions of quality refers to the fact, that the quality of the design of a product, or process can be evaluated in different ways, including the aproaches listed below. This is important for designers, because they must satisfy their customers. (Please review the examples offered and try to identify the discussed dimensions of design quality in each given case):

  • Brand name quality, meaning: What is the assumed / expected quality, or in other words: What is the reputation of the company, and its products and services? (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3, Example-4, Example-5)
  • Features, meaning: What features / functions does the product / process have? How do these compare to other, similar (i.e. competing) products and services?, and others, (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Performance, meaning: Does it perform certain expected functions? Does it perform functions better, than others? How well does it perform a function?, and others, (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Reliability, meaning: How often does the product, or process fail? How badly does it fail, if it fails? What is the frequency of need to repair?, and others, (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Durability, meaning: How long does the product, or process function error free? (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Serviceability, meaning: How easy is it to repair the product / process? How easy is it to perform routine maintenance to avoid major breakdowns?, and others. (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Aesthetics, meaning: How cool /beautiful / jazzy / fancy / attractive does it look and feel (...and even smell, in case of the leather seats of a new automobile, or motorbike, or new furniture) ? How does ergonomic design help?, and others. (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Excitement quality, meaning: How exciting is the product / process? In other words, what is the adrenalin gain when sitting in a fast accelerating sports car, or when riding a fancy motorbike, or others? (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Safety quality, meaning: How safe is the product / process? How does ergonomic design help to increase safety?, and others. (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3)
  • Conformance to eco-standards, sustainable green products, processes and service systems, meaning: Does the product conform to national / international green design, and greenhouse emission standards? What is the carbon footprint of the product, process, or service? How can this be reduced to zero? How can we design sustainable lean and green features into products, processes and service systems? Did the designers / architects chose bio-degradable materials? Is anything toxic about the product or process, that requires special care when in use, or when recycling? Does the product conform to national / international environmental standards? Does it conform to sustainable green eco-standards? Am I going to have difficulties with it if I take it abroad? Is it going to be compatible on an international basis (e.g. cell phones...)?, and others. (Example-1, Example-2, Example-3, Example-4)

Challenges on How to Improve a Product, a Process, a System, a Service, or an Organization, by Employing CE and TQM Methods and Solutions

There are many methods and solutions to innovate and develop new products and processes, to cut down waste and to improve an organization, a design office, a manufacturing enterprise, a product, a service, or a process.

In our integrated world, where everything relates to everything, one major discipline that helps to improve product, process, and service quality is Total Quality Management, the focus of this eBook.

The purpose of this entire eBook is to help you to learn ways to improve products, processes and systems, by using TQM methods, and tools.

There is no doubt that modern Total Quality Management sounds impressive, but it is also expensive, because it aims at perfection in every aspect of the organization.

On the contrary, many large and small companies claim that total quality is a business strategy. It is "a matter of life or death".

According to Harold Geneen, of ITT, USA, "Quality is not only free, it's our most profitable product line".

But how can this be true ?

As a simple and quick justification process, think for a moment of the fact that by following the traditional quality management philosophy, the fewer the faults, the higher the cost, whereas by applying a total quality approach, the fewer the faults, the lower the cost.

    In other words, modern Total Quality Management means:

    • Meeting the agreed requirements of the customer now, rather than in the future, in a precise, measurable manner.
    • According to Philip Crosby, traditionally companies measure quality by indices, which only the Quality Department understands. However if quality can be measured in financial terms, such as e.g. $1.42 million, the message can be conveyed more effectively).
    • The customer to be satisfied is both internal and external, and is part of the internal, and external supply chain. This way everybody in the organization becomes a "service provider" and wants to get the job done right the first time.
    • Zero defects must be the aim for every human, machine and service provider, in everything we / they do. It is something we can gradually achieve, not instantly. It is a journey, not a fixed distance, or fixed time destination.
    • TQM methods and the culture of quality has to be introduced gradually and managed by people who understand and support the principles, know how to deal with co-workers, and know the tools.

    It is essential to understand that none of the TQM methods work in practice unless they are applied to all levels of the (preferably digital) enterprise, including the internal, as well as external customers and supply chains.

    TQM has its best chance if implemented in a modern, digital factory, in which every major process is monitored, based on analytical, quantitative and computational methods... (For more on this topic please visit our on-line, web-based article).

    To summarize, TQM methods and tools address the entire enterprise, including humans and machines, the business systems, product / process and service system design, process planning, manufacturing planning, the shop floor, packaging, maintenance, after sales service, and others, for several life-cycles, via the internal, as well as the external supply chains. The able, hard working and diligent engineer with an integrated systems - oriented vision will be able to understand market drivers and create successful products, processes and service, supporting the products and processes.
