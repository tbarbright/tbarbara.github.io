    Purpose

    As introduced earlier, CE Design Team Networking, with TQM and TQC feedback-loops is essential, because they enable to cut product and/or service process waste by understanding processes better, via sensory - feedback processing networks, like the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT).

    Furthermore, TQM and TQC represent essential methods and technologies, to improve quality, and to create a lean, networked, collaborative feedback controlled workflow from concept to integrated product / process design, manufacture, assembly, maintenance and after-sales support, based on analytically established customer requirements, and networked logistic processes.

    When we design a network for TQM and TQC applications we simulate first. The goal of most TQM / TQC network simulation scenarios is to evaluate some aspect of a system’s key characteristics, their behavior or performance, and to quantify, typically in terms of statistics, the results, and then use the results for making better quality decisions. This requires a simulation environment with software tools that provide insight into a model’s dynamic operation.

    The purpose of this chapter is to offer an:

    • Introduction and overview of computer networking for TQM and TQC,
    • Explain some critical networking methods applicable to TQM / TQC,
    • Network simulation solutions, that improve quality,
    • Explain some network planning methods, that follow quality standards, and to
    • Illustrate the importance of this subject area with real-world and R&D challenges, and cases.

    Why Computer Networking for CE/PLM/TQM and TQC ?

    Computer Networking, as an enabling methodology and technology for TQM and TQC systems, with a mission to capture reliable data (usually via sensors) needs:

    • Sound methods, and technologies,
    • New management style,
    • Practices that are based on co-operation, respect and trust.

    This is fundamental because networking for TQM and TQC means sharing and exchanging quality information at all levels of the digital enterprise, including real-time shop-floor processes, as well as management offices; (as they say 'from the shop to the top', and then 'from the top to the shop'; preferably real-time.)

    In order to achieve the maximum benefits of quality lean manufacturing systems operated in digital factories, one of the most important rules to follow is to establish distributed, intranet networking between able people and machines, including the workstations, cells and manufacturing lines, within a line balanced client/server architecture.

    This ‘meaningful exchange of quality information’ should happen between individual intranet-web-enabled machines, devices, workstations, and robot controllers and their sensor-controlled auxiliary devices.

    Furthermore, cells need to communicate with:

    • Each other,
    • Cell/ line control computer,
    • Digital factory production control systems,
    • Quality control systems, assuring that quality is checked at process level (i.e. in-process, and in-cycle),
    • Programming,
    • Product / process design,
    • Manufacturing systems engineering offices, in order to gain valuable feedback from the shop floor.

    In this Chapter we introduce some of the most important network planning and engineering management principles and rules, and offer simulation examples, that lean manufacturing cell and system designers, and digital factory networkers keen on obtaining quality data in real-time, should keep in mind when preparing for TQM accreditation. (Note, that our methods are generic, analytical and open-source computational, therefore can be applied to a wide-range of application challenges.)

    Our network requirements analysis and planning methodology is based on well established and tested engineering, engineering management and computing principles, as well as on some new, object / component-oriented process modeling and simulation algorithms, and methods, and uses our well established CORA (Component-oriented Requirements Analysis) tool.

    As an example of a study, please review an open-source CORA network requirements model.

    The core concept is that we always evaluate network customer requirements, various available and feasible new solutions, and then, and only then, design a system, that integrates well with the accurately analyzed / specified environment.

    In terms of CE, PLM, TQM and TQC manufacturing networks in digital factories, where total quality can be monitored and controlled, the ‘customer’ can be several, including:

    • An individual human operator, or manager in front of a multimedia workstation, a machine, device, or robot, with its own controller, real-time networked with its sensor-controlled auxiliary devices, and/or tools, safety devices, part orientation and setup tables and devices, pallet changers, conveyors, and others,
    • A group, or cell of networked devices, interacting with each other, as well as with their line management computer, that is integrated with the factory LANs (local area networks) for integrated production control, management, quality monitoring, machine tool and robot programming, and other purposes, as well as a
    • Line of such devices and machines, organized into larger systems.

    Why Product Lifecycle Management?

    In a recent presentation at ProEngineer in MA, USA, Product Lifecycle Management: What’s Real Now, Michael Burkett, Jennifer Kemmeter, and Kevin O'Marah, explained:

    'An examination of today’s Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) products is reminiscent of the parable of the blind men and the elephant, where each perceived something different depending on which part of the elephant they touched'. 

    Indeed, the seven offerings that AMR Research examined as part of its deep dive process were more comparatively diverse than those of any other such product examination ever taken. 

    The following are our most important findings:

    A single Product Data Management (PDM) foundation is essential, but vendor functional similarity makes selection tricky—industry know-how breaks the deadlock.

    Other PLM applications are all over the map and largely immature—a best-of-breed strategy will work well for most users.

    We do not yet see the critical functional mass in PLM offerings for any market that would dictate a single-vendor strategy.  We do not believe this will change until the earliest single vendor opportunities coming in Aerospace and Defense (A&D) and Consumer Packaged Goods (CPG).

    A key reason for vendor and product diversity is the hope that a PLM strategy will link heretofore dissimilar and often warring factions: manufacturing, engineering and design, purchasing, and (increasingly) sales and marketing. 

    It should be no surprise that vendors with Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP), PDM, Computer-Aided Design (CAD), and services pedigrees have such differing products and approaches. 

    Some key findings are as follows:

    • Technology differences are less about integration than flexibility.
    • Flexibility is a matter of workflow, data architecture, and scalability.
    • A single PDM foundation is essential, but vendor functional similarity makes selection trickyóindustry know-how breaks the deadlock.

    Typical user stories include the following:

    • A contract manufacturer reports that CAD models and Bill of Materials (BOM) files are in disagreement 90% of the time.
    • A company reports that material usage across products can only be approximately known, and then only after a week of special query programming.
    • A business electronics manufacturer reports that disconnects between sales and engineering product information frequently lead to product being sold that cannot be built.

    It is clear that some approach to consolidation and control of product information is essential as users seek more collaboration in their New Product Development and Introduction (NPDI) processes.  The great majority of user references contacted for this study, 85%, had started their PLM strategies with PDM implementations, most seeking product data control and faster engineering change management. 

    Many are increasingly looking to PLM as the path to better and faster product innovation, but essentially all agree that PLM needs to be built on PDM.

    When asked about cost and risk for PDM implementations, users reported that data conversion was the biggest problem. 

    A large minority of users, especially in complex discrete engineering industries, has spent over 18 months reconciling product information to populate a central PDM environment.  PDM is increasingly viewed as the system of record for all product information.  Users trying to increase design reuse to reduce part redundancy want to be able to query a single repository. 

    This repository need not (and probably often cannot) be physically centralized, but users are working to make it logically unified. 

    Most vendors offer, and many users employ, a layered or buffered approach to PDM.  This is intended to allow two levels of product information control and access.' 
    A Rigorous Computer Networking Method for CE Teams

    The rigorous design team networking method by which one can achieve the benefits of lean design, manufacturing / assembly, service and IT, and even lean de-manufacturing, and disassembly, is as follows:

    • CE/PLM/TQM /TQC networking requirements analysis. During this analysis the typical questions we have to ask are as follows: What does the customer want? (Note, that a customer here can be an individual CNC machine, a device, a robot, a robot cell, and others, that want to monitor, test, evaluate, assure various parameters to be within tolerance limits. When performing this step-by-step process analysis, one should always try to satisfy this need at the highest quality and minimum cost. Further questions that should be answered here include the following: How could the processes be simplified (for all key real-time, mission critical, as well as off-line processes)? What are the engineering solutions for satisfying customer needs? How does our competition do? What level of technology (i.e. engineering solutions can the plant accept? (Note, that there is always a cultural issue, that should not be overlooked?)
    • Object oriented CE/PLM/TQM / TQC system analysis. (This is the 'as is' system scenario, meaning that we need to analyze, understand and model the current TQM system, if in place, environment well.)
    • Object oriented TQM system design. (This is the 'to be' TQM system scenario that needs to be designed. Note, that simulation is a valuable support tool at this stage too. As an example, simulation can be used to predict breakdowns, and their effects on product, process, cost, quality, and others.)
    • Optional small scale test phase implementation feedback loop of the most important aspects and processes (This could mean testing critical operations/ processes on one machine, or device, or cell, before designing the entire line, or system.)
    • CE/TQM / TQC system design refinement feedback loop. (Based on feedback information one should refine and improve the processes, and the design of the system. Note, that simulation is a valuable support tool at this stage too.)
    • Full CE/TQM test phase implementation feedback loop to all key aspects and processes
    • CE/TQM / TQC networked system design completion
    • Implementation and system integration by the team of engineers, managers and operators
    • Final documentation. Prepare the electronic documentation on the way preferably using interactive multimedia and on-line Internet technology. This will help to satisfy customers as well as save significant funds if the documentation has to be updated. Consider the fact, that this document will be viewed/ read by a variety of engineers, managers, operators around the world, and will be used as a quality guideline, or benchmark. Furthermore, don’t ignore the fact that the better, clearer, and more educational your documentation is, the less downtime and technical support expenses you’ll face
    • Subject to contract, continuous documentation maintenance and support, system administration support and optional formal education at all levels to all involved. (Again, your interactive multimedia Internet based documentation could be the same as your distance learning resource material. You will save a lot.)
    • The identification of future R&D and other collaborative/ sales opportunities. This reflects all the useful feedback that your audience/ customers will provide. (A good solution for such collaborative feedback is an Internet-based chat group that you can set up to provide a secure environment for all to exchange good ideas on how to improve quality design, manufacturing, service, management and IT aspects, that you can use to improve your business processes, knowledge, and documentation)

    A critical factor of our networking method for TQM and TQC is vendor evaluation. Both the potential customer as well as the marketing engineer needs to pick the best solutions providers, else the risks might be very high... (We have warned you...)

    Simulation of Computer Networking for CE/TQM / TQC, Using the OPNET IT-Guru Network Simulation Package

    Simulation, as a method, like most system analysis and design methods, involves systems and models of them.

    A system for our CE/PLM/TQM and TQC network simulation purposes can be defined as a facility of processes, either actual, or planned, or virtual, with all important automation and human resources, computer communication systems, sensors, tools and materials, and fail-safe systems.

    As an example, think of a real - world network of welding, or assembly robots, composed of physical sites that can send, route, or receive information, between a network of distributed robot controller PCs, server workstations and various routers and switches. In terms of networking, these sites are referred to as nodes.

    In our study, we employ a simulation software package, called IT-Guru, by OPNET Technologies, Inc., USA. This modern, object-oriented simulation system uses communication node objects to represent the physical sites of our networked robots.

    Real-world nodes are connected by and communicate across links, represented in IT-Guru by communication link objects. Links carry information over distances and can represent items such as electrical, or fiber optic cables, or even wireless communication links between networked devices.

    As communication systems of distributed networked robots become more complex, it is often necessary to view groups of nodes within a network as a single entity. Such a grouping of nodes, along with the links connecting them, is called a sub-network (Ref. IT-Guru, OPNET Technologies)

    As a practical example for such an architecture imagine, that the modern robot controller PC is not just controlling the arm’s movements, but often smart sensor equipped welding guns, or active sensory controlled assembly heads, a variety of vision and other non-contact and contact sensors, safety devices, communication between the controller and the rest of the network, and many others.

    Therefore, a robot in such a modern, networked system has both an inside-distributed network, run by the real-time side of the robot controller, and the higher level robot controller PC, being integrated into a distributed network, such as the robot line.

    The term simulation applies to a wide range of methods and applications designed to mimic the behavior of real world, or imagined, virtual world systems.

    Simulation for our networked TQM and TQC engineering purposes is usually performed on a computer with powerful software, nevertheless ‘mental simulation’, typically preceding any computerized work is a valuable and very powerful engineering problem solving method too, because it enables the system analysts and engineers to think of real-world, or imagined (i.e. virtual) experiences and reason over which parameters and values are truly important, and which are ignorable.

    In terms of supporting this stage of reasoning with an analytical, quantitative and computational approach, we recommend our CORA, Component-oriented Requirements Analysis, and our PFRA, Process Failure Risk Analysis software tools; (Both methods are described in more detail in other Chapters in this eBook publication, as well as in other eBook publications in our Case Library; please look up: http://www.cimwareukandusa.com).

    Following our integrated quality approach to networking, we should select our vendors, based on our requirements analysis results.

    As an example, when selecting the robot and system integration vendors, one should be prepared to be able to answer the following questions:

    • Does the solutions provider have successful, long term experience with the methods and technologies you require in the particular (e.g. automotive) industry?
    • Did you check the references in detail? Did you visit them, seen the systems and talked to the people who actually use it every day?
    • Does the solutions provider offer a scalable, flexible, lean, reconfigurable, modular solution? (You never know what the future brings... you have to be lean, agile and stay flexible.)
    • Does the solutions provider support open standards? ... and links to intranets/ Internet/ the client - server networking architecture?
    • How difficult is to 'plug-and-play' new welding/ assembly robots/ devices into such networks?
    • What data can be communicated via such networks and for what purposes? (Could include real-time sensor feedback, smart sensor integration, production control, quality control, remote monitoring of robots, etc.) Did you check this out in detail or just read the robot's user and service manuals...?
    • Can the solutions provider offer expertise all along the project from requirements analysis, through system analysis, system design, integration, validation, implementation and maintenance/ service/ upgrade?
    • What if something goes wrong? ... or during the project major changes occur (e.g. scale down, or scale up, or focus shift)? How would your solutions provider react to that?
    • Does the solutions provider understand management of change? Can they integrate the new robot/ machine/ process into your existing design/ manufacturing/ assembly/ management/ quality assurance culture?
    • Do they help engineers, technicians, operators and maintenance staff to learn about the new system/ process?
    • Do they have on-line. and/or multimedia based learning tools and resources? (Note, that educated operators, engineers and managers make the difference! The training expense associated with the use of advanced technologies may be holding back small manufacturers from using hi-tech solutions).
    • Do they offer you access to some of the development tools (e.g. software) that you might need to customize your system?
    • Is your solutions provider prepared to help you to develop new, more advanced welding/ assembly robot and automation strategies and systems that will create wealth for you? (Do they understand this and support you all the way, or ...?)
    • Can the solutions provider evaluate the economic benefits you'll gain? Are these calculations realistic? Did you ask an expert / your team to check the best and worst scenarios?

    Anything else, that we or you have forgotten? Ask your team, and your vendors... continuously debate the above issues... brainstorming is essential to get the best results in this case.

    Network Feasibility Study

    When designing a feasibility study for networked devices and systems, one should explore different strategies in the context of the overall organizational and communications requirements.

    One should consider the potential for growth from a typical small system to various large-scale networked systems. Therefore the suggested simplest object - oriented process modeling methodology, as explained in the illustrated Data Flow Diagram, is as follows:

    • Consider the processes, or activities first (draw the ellipses first...)
    • Think of the typical data sources, and data sinks (draw the boxes...) and think of typical
    • Data stores (draw the files...),
    • Establish the data flows, and
    • Rethink, refine and discuss the diagrams. Edit, discuss again and finalize them as part of a team exercise.

    When developing a network feasibility study, the team must consider, discuss and design the following:

    • What type of network is needed to satisfy the type of information that will be transferred between different processes?
    • What is the rate of data flow, what are the
    • Transmission speed requirements, and what are the storage requirements?
    • What are the staff skill levels and is there a strong support towards the selected network?

    By evaluating different options, one should establish a strategy for an organization-wide communications network and determine the actions required as a result of this strategy.

    The strategy should be validated using specialist support and consultants if necessary. The cost savings have to be determined regarding the planned network versus the traditional paper-based plant data communication system.

    Network Planning Strategy (Including Internet/intranet Networked Server Sites)

    Staff involved in the networking project has to be educated in the scope of the selected network(s) and the related connectivity issues, meaning that:

    • The networking requirements have to be decided.
    • The capabilities of different hardware suppliers should be evaluated.
    • Existing suppliers and their support for the selected network must be evaluated.
    • The readiness of existing plant and systems for network compatibility must be evaluated.
    • Back-up, safety and security has to be considered. The potential risk of failing processes, exposure to downtime if activities are focused on a single communications network must be discussed.

    Costs have to be determined for:

    • Cable,
    • Interface devices,
    • Head-ends,
    • Modems, or others,
    • Network design and installation,
    • Application software development,
    • Sensor, cell and line integration,
    • Maintenance, training, and others.

    The availability of technicians to cover the service must be reviewed. External services with a view to supplementing existing staff, if necessary, must be reviewed.

    • Employees/ experts that could become candidates to implement the system must be short listed and evaluated.
    • The reliability of the service must be evaluated.
    • A detailed investment appraisal must be conducted.
    • A time-phased implementation plan must be developed.

    An organization-wide engineering network (perhaps including the Internet) awareness campaign must be designed and delivered before the robot cell/ line network is in place.

    CE/TQM / TQC Network Migration Strategy

    The engineering network design team often has to deal with a variety of different networks, and related protocols, and technology.

    These are the steps to follow, when a network migration strategy must be developed for TQM / TQC networking:

    • Define the ultimate goal for the network architecture and the key intermediate milestones.
    • Determine the required and desired user services and functions and the related application program interfaces.
    • Determine the architecture of the existing networks.
    • Determine the organizational responsibilities for network management.
    • Define the logical network design(s).
    • Choose migration priorities by performing a cost benefit analysis.
    • Establish the migration policies and approach.
    • Define the migration time and events schedule.
    • Apply generic policies to develop strategies for specific environments.
    • Prototype migration strategy ideas.
    • Promote migration policies to users, vendors and company equipment purchasers.
    • Execute and monitor the network migration strategy plan.

    Within a sub-network or group of systems requiring connectivity, the strategist / consultant/ team must thoroughly understand each vendor's plan to upgrade equipment. Vendor approaches may differ and time should be allowed to make adjustments to the general network migration strategy.

    Network Implementation Strategy (Including Internet/ intranet Networking Server Sites)

    The key points to consider are as follows:

    • A steering committee must be set up, conveying management a clear goal.
    • Task groups have to be set up.
    • The project manager should decide on a starter kit for a pilot evaluation.
    • The pilot implementation should be used to replace some existing cables and interfaces. (Wireless networks could be considered).
    • Existing equipment must be upgraded to the selected network specification.
    • Staff training/ recruitment is necessary for covering network management and operators for actually running the network (e.g. the robot line’s Internet server / client network).
    • User interface design/ understanding and support is required.
    • A post-implementation review must be carried out to ensure that the network system is running efficiently.

    Server Balancing Simulation for CE/PLM/ TQM / TQC with OPNET’s IT-Guru

    Based on the above described requirements analysis, network planning, and server balancing reasoning, for our purposes, we have decided to follow a simple, nevertheless powerful network architecture, in which we have

    • Three servers (called server_1, server_2 and server_3 in our models), that are linked to our lean manufacturing / assembly lines, as well as to our digital factory TQM / TQC information management system,
    • Linked to a load balancer (called load_balancer), to assure robust and fail-safe operation 24 hours a day, seven days a week,
    • Linked via a server family gateway (called server_fam_gateway in our simulation model) to the networked manufacturing / assembly / robot line, run over an Internet/intranet protocol,
    • Client gateway to the individual machine / robot cell controllers, (in networking terms, called ‘clients’), as well as linking to management computers for remote data analysis, programming, quality control, and other purposes via the company intranet/ Internet link.

    Following our organization-wide engineering network architecture, including the Internet, our network is integrated with the machine controller CNCs, robot cell/ line network via sub-networks.

    Before running this simulation model, and discussing the results, we introduce some basic network design and simulation methods, principles and terminology following the IT-Guru notation (as in IT-Guru, by OPNET Technologies, USA.)

    Sub-network Modeling

    In terms of the way IT-Guru handles sub-networks, a sub-network contains other network objects and abstracts them into a single object. A sub-network can encompass a set of nodes and links to represent a physical grouping of objects (this can be a local area network of CNC machine, or robot PC controllers) or it can contain other sub-networks (for example including the material handling system control of the line).

    Sub-networks within other sub-networks form the hierarchy of the network model. This hierarchy can then be extended as required to model the structure of the network.

    A sub-network is considered the parent of the objects inside of it, and the objects are the children of the sub-network. The highest level sub-network in the network hierarchy does not have a parent, therefore it is the top sub-network, or global sub-network.

    Sub-networks can be created and interconnected within this top level, or within other sub-networks.

    Sub-networks provide a powerful mechanism for manipulating complex networks by breaking down the system's complexity through abstraction.

    As an example, a large network of hundreds of CNC devices and robots with many components can be segmented based on the proximity, connectivity, or other architectural considerations of its elements. (For example, sub-network objects can represent networked robot cells, or lines, or design / quality control / robot programming office LANs (local area networks) within an automotive factory).

    Following this model, within each robot line, sub-network objects can represent each robot cell or design, or programming department sub-network. In IT-Guru, other than the objects it contains, the primary attributes of a sub-network are its geographical position and size. However, these attributes can be ignored when a sub-network is used strictly to abstract other network level objects or when sub-networks exist independently from each other.

    Attributes

    IT-Guru allows the networking engineer to manipulate attributes in many ways, including the following:

    • setting attribute values for a single object or a group of objects,
    • promoting attributes to specify their values when a simulation is run,
    • creating new attributes,
    • defining attribute properties,
    • saving attribute properties for reuse with other attributes.

    Three types of attributes may comprise an object. These types can be as follows:

    • built-in attributes,
    • model attributes, and
    • extended attributes.

    Built-in attributes provide basic information about an object. They specify information such as the object ’s name, location in the work space, and graphic representation.

    Built-in attributes are characteristic of each object type.

    When an object is created in IT-Guru, the program automatically specifies initial values for these attributes. (These values can naturally be edited, depending on what devices, or objects, and protocol stacks the network designers want to simulate).

    Model attributes add information to a node or link object. They determine how the object behaves in a network model by specifying protocols, data rates, and similar information.

    Model attributes automatically become part of any object using that model. The model developer defines model attributes and sets initial values for them.

    Extended attributes are optional model attributes that the model developing engineer can add to an object, thus further customizing its behavior in a network model. Support for extended attributes must be provided in a model by the model developer.

    In IT-Guru, all attributes share a set of basic properties. Attribute properties define the attribute, specifying information such as its name, data type, and allowed values. These properties are generally fixed, but can be given different values in some cases (IT-Guru, OPNET Technologies, 2002).

    Communication Nodes

    Communication nodes exist within sub-networks and represent network devices that transmit and receive information. Node behavior is determined by its node model.

    The node model specifies the node’s internal structure and can be specified via its model attribute node object, that is said to be an instance of its node model.

    Distinct instances of the pieces of equipment (such as a robot controller, called ‘client’ in our model) are independent of each other in a real network, although they may have identical capabilities.

    A network model can contain any number of communication nodes, using the same, or different node models.

    Communication Links and Channels

    Links in IT-Guru allow nodes to communicate with each other using structured messages called ‘packets’. When a packet is given to a transmitter in a source node, it is conveyed over a link to a receiver in a destination node.

    A communication channel can be thought of as a pipe, where packets are placed in one end by a transmitter channel and retrieved at the other end by a receiver channel. If a link has multiple communication channels, it can be thought of as a bundle of pipes, each one conveying packets from the source node to the destination node (IT-Guru, OPNET Technologies, USA).

    IT-Guru supports two types of links: point-to-point, and bus. Each link type provides a fundamentally different types of connectivity. Point-to-point links connect a single source node to a single destination node, whereas bus links connect each node of a fixed set to all others in the particular set.

    Packet Formats

    Packets carry information and can be sent between transmitters and receivers.

    In our example, packets can carry robot programs, when uploaded from the design/ programming office servers to the robot lines, and then to the individual CNCs, or robots, or parts of them, if there is a need for an update, or edit, or quality control, production control, maintenance and other data.

    As an example, packets can include mission critical, ‘panic’ related real-time TQC data between the robot controller PCs and the line servers.

    Packets are data structures consisting of storage areas called fields and can either be formatted, or unformatted. Formatted packets have fields designed according to a packet format , which specifies the packets’ field names, data types, sizes, and default values. Formatted packets can be read by corresponding communication protocols only. Unformatted packets have no pre-defined fields (IT-Guru, OPNET Technologies). In IT-Guru, packet formats are pre-defined and typically named according to the model in which they are intended to be used.

    Evaluation of the Network Simulation Results

    The goal of most TQM / TQC simulation scenarios is to evaluate some aspect of a system’s key characteristics, their behavior or performance, and to quantify, typically in terms of statistics, the results, and then use the results for decisions.

    This requires a simulation environment with software tools that provide insight into a model’s dynamic operation.

    Based on IT-Guru’s in-depth analysis, the network engineering analyst can collect object, scenario-wide and global statistics as follows:

    • Object statistics are collected from individual objects. They allow the network engineering analyst to evaluate the performance of specific network nodes or links (a single hub’s Ethernet delay, or a server balancing change, as in our example).
    • Scenario-wide object statistics are collected from all relevant objects in a network (for example, Ethernet delay for every node). They allow the network engineering analyst to easily monitor the performance of all objects of a specific type.
    • Global statistics are collected from the entire network. They represent results that apply to the network as a whole (such as global end-to-end delay) and let the designers and management analyze aspects of the network’s overall performance.

    More specifically, IT-Guru offers the following types of statistics, when analyzing networks:

    • Queue Size,
    • Available Space,
    • Overflow Occurrences,
    • Delay
    • Inter-arrival Times,
    • Packet Sizes,
    • Throughput,
    • Utilization,
    • Error Rates,
    • Collisions, and
    • Application-Specific Statistics Defined By A Model Developer

    Because there are many possible statistics to collect during a network simulation study, the data files would quickly grow past practical use if the simulation program recorded them all.

    Therefore, the analyst must specifically select the statistics that is valuable for the particular study to collect before running a simulation (Ref. IT-Guru, OPNET Technologies).

    As in our simplified example, based on the plotted graphs and screens, management can easily evaluate the need of the balanced server architecture, and even investigate ‘what if’ scenarios further, without committing themselves to major upfront investments.

    As a final thought in this Chapter, consider the power and the opportunities of setting up sensory feedbck-controlled TQM / TQC networks in various remote factories, that you can then optimize (and hopefully keep within predefined variation) via neetworked data analysis.
