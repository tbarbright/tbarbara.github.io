    An Introduction to Kaizen Methods

    The Japanese term Kaizen highlights a commitment, a culture, a set of methods for the entire enterprise to focus on activities that are low in terms of adding value, therefore considered to be waste.

    Kaizen (and JIT) attacks the sources of waste, and then individuals and groups take responsibility for problems to solve. During this process, such quality teams become empowered to make personal efforts to minimize the following:

    • Inventory,
    • Defective finished products (zero defects),
    • Inefficient work methods,
    • Unnecessary movements and transfers of material,
    • Process, and
    • Operational mistakes that undermine quality product / service.

    To achieve such goals, those who formally control and lead the business must demonstrate belief in the value of skills, and encourage the commitment of everyone working in the operation.

    The success of Kaizen assumes the mutual support between internal groups.

    Modern management, particularly evident in Japan, and in the USA, and some parts of Europe, Asia, China, Hong Kong, and elsewhere, accept multi-skilled, committed employees who respond flexibly and with know-how to resolve local, operational challenges and fluctuations, as an industry norm.

    An Introduction to Kanban Methods

    Kanban is an automated directional method and device that gives information concerning

    • What to produce,
    • When to produce,
    • In what quantity,
    • By what means (i.e. resources), and
    • How to transport it.

    The term Kanban emerged from the standard operations list.

    It came from the practice whereby foremen in separate workplaces would write their group's work content on slips of paper, and post them, along with other foremen's, to show what was going on.

    The quantity to be produced, the time, methods, procedures and amount to be transported, where it is to be transported, the place to store it, the means of transportation and the type of containers to be used can all be known at a glance through the Kanban.

    The Kanban system was designed to:

    • Apply standard operations at any time,
    • Give directions based on the actual conditions existing in the workplace,
    • Prevent addition of any unnecessary work for those engaged in start-up operations, and
    • Prevent a deluge of paper which cannot serve as future source material.

    The other function of Kanban is to move information with the actual material, component, or sub-assembly.

    If this condition can be maintained one can achieve the following benefits:

    • No overproduction will occur,
    • Priority in production becomes obvious (when the Kanban for one item piles up, that is the item that must be produced first), and
    • Control of actual material becomes easier.

    The Rules of Kanban

    The Kanban rules offer tools to manage quality at the workplace effectively.

    We now summarize and explain the preconditions to be established and the six basic rules of Kanban:

    Rule 1: Do not pass on defective parts to the subsequent process, meaning that if a defective part is discovered measures must be taken immediately to prevent its recurrence.

    The process that has produced the defective part must immediately discover it and report it to the cell controller, which then takes further decisions.

    To avoid the piling up of defective components, corrective actions must be taken immediately by the machines themselves (if they are capable of doing so) by the workforce, and the management. (Example cell layout.)

    Rule 2: Draw parts and material at the time they are needed and in the required quantity.

    This is a basic JIT (just-in-time) rule, meaning that no loss is to be generated by overproduction.

    Rule 3: Produce only as many as required by the subsequent process.

    This rule relates to the second one, with the extension that it is predicated on the condition that the process itself must restrict inventory to the absolute minimum.

    In other words this rule also implies to produce the exact amount in the order required.

    Rule 4: Maintain equipment and workforce in such a way that parts can be produced at the time they are needed and in the quantity needed. Equalize production.

    Rule 5: Kanban is a means to fine tuning. For the workforce Kanban becomes the single source of information for manufacturing, material handling and transportation.

    Rule 6: Stabilize and rationalize the process.

    Defective workparts exist only because there is

    • Not sufficient standardization, in terms of agreed and set procedures, and processes, within pre-programmed and cost effective control variable values, and
    • Rationalization of the workplace.

    According to Kanban, waste, unevenness and unreasonableness create defective parts. In other words if the tools are right and the organization (including skilled people, and quality machinery) is sound, the probability of producing faulty parts and waste is usually very low.

    At Toyota they say that "a manufacturer's profit can be found in the way he makes things". It reflects the philosophy of attaining cost reduction by the elimination of wasteful operations.

    Waste Reduction at Toyota (and in Other Progressive Companies)

    There are many types of waste. At Toyota, they divide waste into the following seven categories:

    1. Waste arising from overproduction

    2. Waste arising from time on hand: waiting

    3. Waste arising from transporting

    4. Waste arising from processing itself

    5. Waste arising from unnecessary stock on hand

    6. Waste arising from unnecessary motion

    7. Waste arising from producing defective parts, or products, or services

    "Think Kanban" Rules

    In the Toyota factories where Kanban was invented the basic TQM thinking is as follows (Toyota prototype automobile image-1 and image-2):

    • Balance the production plan
    • Make each lot size (or in other words, the batch size) as small as possible
    • Produce only what is needed, when needed and in the quantity needed. (This is to ensure, that there will be no waste arising from neither overproduction, nor from excess capacity).
    • Make sure that everyone can understand where the problem is. If we know where the problem is, it is relatively easy to solve
    • Clarify the purpose behind the task of problem solving. Probe into the true cause and offer a proper rather than a temporary solution
    • Even if there is only one defective item, provide a corrective measure.
    • Even if the defect occurs only once every thousand times, ascertain the facts. With these facts, the core of the problem can be found and steps can be taken to prevent recurrence of defects. This type of defect is harder to find than those that occur more frequently
    • Move from work improvement to equipment improvement.
    • Toyota insists on carrying out the work improvement phase of improvement activities thoroughly before moving on to the equipment improvement phase.
    • Differentiate between man-hours and number of workers, and between labour saving and people saving. (For example, when automation devices are installed, there may be a labour saving of 0.9 worker. But if the process still requires 0.1 worker, the money spent does not result in reducing the number of workers. This is often erroneously looked upon as labour saving).
    • Regard an improvement activity completed only when the result sought in the initial goal is obtained. If the result cannot be obtained it is often that the work has been done without much thought being given to it. Confirm the result of implementation at the workplace, adjust all those parts inadequately done and confirm the result again. By repeating this process, a good result can be obtained through improvement

    Toyota, BMW MINI in Oxford England, and other progressive enterprises consider the workplace as an organic entity, that dynamically develops and changes.

    The hands and legs have not entrusted their brains to the management division. Therefore, engineering must not act as if it were the commander-in-chief of the workplace.

    On the contrary, the autonomy of the work-place must be emphasized and respected and placed into the hands of the experienced and ethical workers; because they live with the processes every day!

    The engineering division gives support to the workplace and provides services in those areas that need them. It makes certain that the responsibility is not scattered and that the information provided is neither excessive nor deficient

    Lead Time Reduction Rules

    At Toyota, they define lead time as the time elapsed from the time we start processing materials into products to the time we receive payment for them.

    Generally speaking, lead time is the sum of the time required for processing and the time the product is in storage.

    Zero Inventory Rule

    According to Toyota, and other progressive companies, for any manufacturing company the most desirable condition is to reduce inventory.

    Of course, it is practically impossible to have zero inventory, but is often set as a goal.

    One shouldn't forget that in order to achieve this in one factory, possibly the suppliers have to keep a reasonable inventory, or alternatively all of them have to work following lean manufacturing and JIT principles, empowered by dynamically adjustable supply-chain management systems.

    Zero inventory is an aim; it is not easy to achieve in practice.

    The rational level of inventory is furthermore influenced by seasonal effects, as well as by technology, security, and other factors, such as politics, oil supplies and industrial actions in international ports, and others.

    To conclude this thought and principle, what a company can and should do is to accept the "zero inventory" as a challenge and try to reduce the inventory as much as feasible for the particular business.

    How Do Kanban and Just-In-Time Rules Integrate?

    Toyota defines JIT as follows: "Let the process have what is needed, when needed and in the quantity needed".

    Automobiles, for example consist of thousands of parts. The number of processes required to manufacture a car are high. To harness all of these different processes and to engage them into a just-in-time production system is a very difficult task.

    To reach this goal in reality, production plans have to be dynamically changed and one should rely on flexible cells and systems integrated into CIM (Computer Integrated Manufacturing).

    Factors causing changes in production plans are numerous, including changing market conditions and varied factors affecting the manufacturer. When these factors are at work, and if a problem arises in the preceding process, the next process may find a shortage of parts and other items. Whether we like it or not, some subsequent processes may have to stop production or be forced to change plans.

    To summarize, to build quality into the product and into the processes involved, each worker must be responsible for each of the processes he performs, and must ensure the quality of each. Inspection must be brought within the process, and in order to send only good products to the subsequent process, defectives must be picked up on the spot.

    Reasonably sized (i.e. small) cellular manufacturing systems, controlled by able, motivated and responsible humans and computers, as well as by principles and methods such as Kanban and JIT, help to create such a manufacturing enterprise.

    An Introduction to JIT (Just-in-Time) Methods

    The central objective of a Total Quality policy is the elimination of waste by the minimization of manufacturing variance. In other words, the central feature of Total Quality Management and Control, CIM (Computer Integrated Manufacturing), and lean production methods, is the elimination of waste in order to increase competitiveness, quality and productivity.

    CE, CIM, JIT and Total Quality Management

    Whether one considers CE, CIM (Computer Integrated Manufacturing), Total Quality or JIT first as a solution depends on where the main problems and opportunities are. The truth is that they all have to fit together well with each other, as well as with integrated product and process design (or, Concurrent Engineering), as well as administration and management.

    If one can see the chance of making a dramatic reduction in lead time, the first step will probably be to adopt a small CIM program within which JIT principles could be applied and then followed up with Total Quality Management, already building on the CIM (or networked, flexible, lean, digital factory) infrastructure.

    In other words CIM, JIT and Total Quality cannot be viewed in isolation.

    If the main opportunity for manufacturing improvement is through cutting the total cost of quality in the business, or if it is important to give the customers an improved perception of the quality of the business and products, then one should definitely go first for CIM, JIT and Total Quality.

    As a general rule, as one implements Total Quality, the need for large inventories will disappear, and opportunities will open up for implementing a JIT philosophy. It is no accident that the Japanese started from the Total Quality ideas of Feigenbaum and Deming and went on to develop the concept of JIT and FMS/CIM (Flexible Manufacturing Cells and Systems, and digital factories).

    According to Professor J. Parnaby (Lucas Engineering & Systems):

    "JIT methodology means the development of materials flow procedures wherein materials are supplied exactly at the time they are required to the point at which they are processed and have value added; it does not depend upon the purchase of high technology equipment.

    There may, of course, be areas where the production engineering of process changeovers require attention or where information technology in the form of personal or microcomputers can be applied to support the process.

    However, JIT is primarily a consequence of improved methodologies, not the application of high technology, and it starts from the viewpoint that everything actively constitutes a process capable of improvement to give better customer service and lower cost".

    As PA International defines JIT, as an approach to scheduling which back schedules from the due date for an order, so that each operation takes place "just in time" to meet that date.

    It is a production philosophy which aims at the elimination of all processes which fail to add value. Batch sizes are minimized and WIP (work in progress) inventory is reduced radically.

    In developing the JIT approach, there are two important messages to bear in mind:

    1. It is fundamental and when completed there can be no other comparable improvement since it completely tailors a manufacturing strategy to the needs of a market and produces mixed products in exactly the order required.

    2. It requires a total systems approach in which many aspects of the business have to be changed.

    It is not just a question of applying a particular technique but instead requires simultaneous changes based upon sound analysis and TQM management.

    All of the methods for monitoring and controlling factory processes and procedures, including day-to-day financial control,

    The whole organizational structure, i.e. reducing the large number of fragmented specialist departments and replacing these with integrated multidisciplinary departments capable of taking much more of an overall business viewpoint.

    JIT Rules in Practice

    According to Professor H. Wildemann, just-in-time production rules were introduced in Germany for four reasons. These are:

    1. Inventory in the logistic chain represents stored capacities.

    It is more profitable to store capacities in capital assets than liquid assets. This requires regrouping individual items in the balance sheet. The goal is to aim for short throughput times and high flexibility.

    2. Inventories hide mistakes.

    Optimizing a single function, such as purchasing, production, or sales, requires inventories. Only these safety stock inventories enable smooth production and prompt delivery to be achieved, and they compensate for unbalanced capacities, insufficient flexibility, scrap, and lack of delivery accuracy. If inventories are reduced, sources of error become apparent and there is immediate pressure to remove them.

    This causes a permanent rationalization of the production process.

    3. Throughput time is an important indicator of manufacturing effectiveness.

    Short processing and supply times reduce the forecast period and improve flexibility changes in demand.

    In many enterprises, the planning certainty of delivery dates is relatively low.

    Some orders are completed before and some after the planned delivery date. The strategy of the production planning department is to reduce the dispersion of finishing dates by constant interventions in the production process.

    It is an established fact that this strategy has shown great success in many areas. Drastic reduction in throughput time is only possible by extensively changing the structure of production, i.e. through a selective decentralization of manufacturing functions into cellular systems and focusing on stronger product orientation.

    This change in structure leads to a manufacturing segmentation that achieves flow optimization in certain segments and controls them so well that shop floor activities are transparent.

    4. There is no manufacturing structure with lower costs than continuous flow production.

    This principle is applied to industrial production of individual and job-lot manufacturing by calculating daily lot sizes.

    It is connected to the introduction of organizational rules that maintain a continuous flow according to the following rule: produce every day only partial quantities of an order. Therefore, the continuous change of production represents a particular problem.

    Strategies to minimize the set-up times as well as investments in flexible production techniques are attempts to synchronize different capacity units.

    JIT and the Dynamically Adjustable Supply Chain

    As a conclusion of what we have stated above, JIT production requires the application of the following basic ideas to the whole supply chain, including:

    • Job structures, meaning, that job descriptions and the scope of the responsibilities must be simple and offered with built-in flexibility (due to the established trust, this is feasible).
    • Training programs have to be consistant and continuous with the requirements.
    • Process flow routes and process capabilities in manufacturing and in office processes must be modeled to reduce waste.
    • Procedures for changing over machines and processes have to me modeled and analyzed to reduce waste.
    • Communication systems (i.e. intranets and the web) should be propagated by all levels of staff.
    • Interfacing management procedures between customers and suppliers is essential.
    • Procedures for goal setting, project management to achieve targets and continuous improvement across departmental boundaries, following a total quality style is essential.
    • Capital expenditure authorization procedures must be simplified and decisions must be taken at the level, where the most accurate information is available to take responsible decisions.
    • Support for quality diagnosis and control should be implemented.
    • Material flow control disciplines, e.g. containerized flow with very small batch size, or batch size one is essential.
    • Factory scheduling and computer aided planning systems, integrated with dynamic simulation to adjust to real-time changes is essential.
    • Support procedures for quality diagnostics and control is essential, preferably implemented via intranets within the factory.
    • Relationships between internal departments in the organization to achieve a distributed customer-contractor style is very important.

    Multidisciplinary Teams

    The development of JIT methods is a multidisciplinary team process and totally visible general management leadership is essential. It relies on new team approaches to factory operation with some clear specific aims.

    Clear definition of the business ratio targets to be achieved for competitiveness, e.g. for stock turnover ratio, sales per employee, ratio of staff to direct workers, lead times, percent reduction of product cost and percentage return on sales and capital

    Simplification of all process flow routes whether for information in offices, paperwork associated with order processing, in the design function or for material flows in manufacturing

    The creation of a logical simple cellular structure with clearly defined leadership, based upon natural people and machinery groupings around information or material flows following process flow chart analysis, careful calculation of lead-times and meticulous analysis of component and product characteristics

    An Introduction to Lean Production Methods within the TQM Context

    Cellular, and Lean Flexible Manufacturing

    Cellular and lean manufacturing, assembly, and production in general, represents implementation reality for establishing a JIT method, following Kanban principles.

    The fundamental thinking behind this is that every leading institution and small and large manufacturing company in the world is dealing with some aspects of flexible manufacturing and automation. This is because they have found that designing and making goods and products "to order" rather than "for stock", is the key issue in cutting manufacturing costs and lead time and eventually stay in business in the future.

    Flexible Manufacturing Cells and Systems (FMCs and FMSs) in this sense are offering the JIT and lean fabrication facilities and technologies with sophisticated quality control feedback loops.

    FMS may be defined as a reprogrammable manufacturing system in its broadest sense, dealing with high level distributed data processing and automated material flow, using highly flexible, computer controlled (and in some cases manually operated) material and information processors within an integrated, multi-layered feedback control architecture.

    The benefits of FMS within a CIM system can be summarized as follows:

    1. Increased productivity (often by a factor of 2 to 3.5).

    2. Decreased production costs (often by 50%).

    3. Decreased inventory and work-in-progress (WIP) to a level lower than ever before.

    4. 100% inspection, thus increasing the quality of the product to a pre-programmed level.

    5. Batch size one, thus offering flexibility and quality at a level never dreamed of before.

    6. Decreased repetitive ("assembly line type"), often hazardous, physical work, increasing the need for intelligent, human work.

    To summarize, "FMSs are computers with hands" enabling the implementation of the Total Quality / CIM / JIT concepts on the shop floor by providing an accurate and high quality feedback controlled manufacturing facility.

    This logical and simple lean, reconfigurable and agile cellular manufacturing enables the following to be achieved:

    Note, that in a similar fashion to the above, flexible, lean, and JIT measures can be, and should be specified for non-manufacturing cells too, including, design offices, for goods received processes, for processes dealing with customer order paperwork, and many others.

    • The operation of simpler production control systems based on customer-supplier relationships between cells and clear ownership of each cell by a cell team. Input-Output analysis then provides performance measures.
    • The step by step controlled implementation of Kanban cell by cell, with temporary insulating buffers, as a materials flow control discipline followed by elimination of traditional production control and progress chasing departments.
    • The definition of cell and operator accountability for quality.
    • The creation of new, simpler, low overhead organizational structures with fewer layers.
    • The introduction of new flexible job structures with multi-skilled staff and with indirect control activities being carried out by direct staff on-line.
    • The elimination of traditional work-orders, detailed work-in-progress monitoring and several paperwork procedures associated with the traditional financial control function.
    • Continuous, 100% quality control. (Example technologies include contact and non-contact sensing systems with computer control: Renishaw (UK) example-1, example-2, example-3, example-4, example-5, example-6, example-7.)
    • The installation of team-based and cell-focussed continuous improvement procedures supported by structured training programmes and well as institutionalized project management procedures for involving all levels of personnel to ensure ownership and commitment.

    The implementation of focussed and relevant measures of performance at manufacturing cell level directly supporting the achievement of business ratio targets for creating the pressures for competitiveness, meaning

    • Daily cell stock-turnover ratio,
    • Daily cell representative lead-times,
    • Daily cell percentage achievement of mixed output plan, and
    • Daily cell quality achievement measured in parts per million.

    Note, that in a similar fashion to the above, flexible, lean, and JIT measures can be, and should be specified for non-manufacturing cells too, including, design offices, for goods received processes, for processes dealing with customer order paperwork, and many others.

    Just-in-Time Design and CE Drivers

    Based on PTC's findings (Parametric Technologies Inc., MA, USA) because 48% of new designs are modifications of existing designs, the speed of developing new products is greatly enhanced by the ability to reuse previous product configurations—Gartner

    To survive in today’s intensely competitive environment, companies must respond instantly to rapidly changing customer and market requirements. With an efficient configuration management process, smart companies now have the agility they need to quickly define and produce new products that exceed customer expectations.

    By far, the most complicated, yet crucial area of manufacturing is configuration management. “CM” is not only complex but also difficult to define, since it encompasses many facets of product development. There are varying opinions on how to best describe ‘config management.’

    But most understand it as the collective activities associated with determining and documenting the content of manufactured products over time as they are developed, sold, modified and retired from service. No matter how you define ‘CM,’ it is the key to controlling product configurations. Who Needs It?

    The need for configuration management is driven by the need to build products. Just as every manufacturer must build products, every manufacturer must manage the configurations of their products in some form.

    The difference is: how effective and efficient is the process?

    Companies who do it well, and utilize technology to optimize configuration management processes can improve the efficiency of their product development process to achieve results such as:

    • Significant reductions in time-to-market,
    • Lower product costs, and
    • Higher product quality.

    With today’s globally dispersed product development teams, the need for effective configuration management has never been more acute.

    Today, product development is a highly collaborative process, requiring input from many organizations: design, engineering, manufacturing, sales, marketing, and services.

    Without effective configuration management, it’s impossible to provide all these stakeholders with a clear understanding of the product configuration concept so that they can contribute their part to the product design.

    Consequently, everyone involved with product design and manufacturing isn’t operating with all the information they need to get their jobs done. Yet, this lack of information is just the tip of the iceberg.

    JIT Operation Zones

    There are two main zones of JIT operation which can be developed either sequentially or in parallel:

    1. Within the factory, i.e. where material and information is delivered from one machine to the next, or from one cell to its customer in the correct order of variety and at exactly the time it is needed with the very minimum of buffer stores.

    2. Between the factory and its outside environment, meaning:

    • Between the factory and its suppliers - this requires the suppliers to redesign their factories and interface systems as in 1 above; and
    • Between the factory and its customers - here customers pull-off the mix of products they require as they require them. This pulling-off process creates the Kanban card circulation back inside the factory following the sell-daily make-daily concept.

    The fact that a company feels that it is likely to be a difficult and long term process to get its suppliers operating JIT is no excuse for not developing internal Zone 1 JIT procedures, since the benefits of internal JIT procedures are very great in their own right.

    Results Achievable with JIT

    To summarize, the achievable results are very impressive, and include the following (reported by several technical directors of factories operating under JIT principles in the USA, Japan, as well as in Europe):

    1. All levels of management trained in manufacturing systems engineering and a manufacturing systems engineering development function is installed.

    2. Complex production flow systems are simplified and a new factory architecture is set up comprising cellular production units and one service unit. Product range rationalized and simplified to reduce obsolete or non-contributing varieties.

    3. Average lead-times for manufacture reduced from 5 days to 35 minutes.

    4. No-value-added and wasteful activities reduced by 60%.

    5. Supplier development system introduced and 30% reduction in complexity achieved.

    6. Process changeover times reduced to a maximum of 5 minutes.

    7. U-shaped Kanban cells with job rotation flexibility introduced to replace a traditional linear push system with specialized jobs.

    8. Piecework removed and productivity (standard hours / direct operators hours) increased by 35%.

    9. Personnel reduced by 350.

    10. New cellular business-within-a-business organizational structure with fewer layers and flexible jobs.

    11. Quality circles for continuing operational improvement.

    12. Overall stocks reduced by £3.5m. In-cell WIP reduced by 99%.

    13. Kanban container batch size of one unit.

    14. All managers and operating staff trained on a Kanban trainer.

    15. Floor space required reduced by 30%.

    16. Much improved product quality.

    17. Overall stock turnover ratio increased from 7 times to 21 times.

    These figures speak for themselves; in our view, no more persuasion is necessary on behalf of JIT.

    Note, that in a similar fashion to the above, flexible, lean, and JIT measures can be, and should be specified for non-manufacturing cells too, including, design offices, for goods received processes, for processes dealing with customer order paperwork, and many others.
