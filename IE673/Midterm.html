1989 Jeep Wrangler folding windshield:

The Jeep Wrangler is famous for its folding windshield but early models exhibit an alarmingly high rate of failure. As most owners rarely if ever fold the windshield down the hinge eventually becomes frozen in place. Part of the reason that most owners never fold the windshield is due to the design which requires the removal of eight different bolts including those which hold the sun visors. Plus, once the windshield is down the driver is left with the front roll bars sticking out which generates a lot of wind noise and requires a lot of extra effort to remove.

As a result of this frozen hinge, should the owner then decide to fold the windshield down for either maintenance or to change their driving experience, the hinge will fold over on itself rather than move on its axis as intended. To the owner it may appear that the windshield is folding as intended as the damage they are causing is not obvious. It is only after the windshield is returned to its upright position that the damage has become clear but by then it is too late to prevent and the hinge needs to be replaced.

This could have been detected during the design phase by Chrysler. The company had two seemingly conflicting requirements that the windshield fold down and the front roll bar extend forward to the windshield for improved safety. It seems that nobody did proper review of these two features and so a system was designed which ultimately missed one of the requirements.

Chrysler could have designed the windshield with quick release fittings that wouldn’t require so many tools to remove. The sun visors, a carryover from the CJ-7 which the Wrangler replaced, should have been redesigned to swing away from the roll bar and not get stuck. This could encourage owners to fold their windshield more which would prevent the hinge from freezing up. Additionally they could have used thicker plates for the hinges to prevent them from folding if the hinge were to get stuck.

In my company we are careful to analyze requirements carefully for conflicts and solicit feedback from our customers in cases where features aren’t being used. In the event an error like this makes it out we make sure to correct it in later models and offer options for owners of older models to upgrade the required parts.

Broken Body Mounting Bolt:

Years of corrosion have frozen this bolt in place. When removal was attempted the head sheared off of the bolt. This presented a costly delay as methods of broken bolt removal needed to be researched. Compounding this issue is the fact that the bolt was designed in such a way that the nut which holds the bolt is inside of the body rail and therefore inaccessible. In some cases this nut breaks loose from the sheetmetal holding it in place which causes it to spin uselessly when removal is attempted. The only way to proceed is to cut a hole in the body rail and extract the broken bolt.

This is a failure to consider the maintenance of this item during design. An important part of the product lifecycle is considering the maintenance and disposal of a product once it has reached the market. In this case it appears the manufacturer failed to consider what would happen if these bolts became corroded and needed to be removed. It is very important to consider the failure conditions that could arise in the product. When designing an item my company makes sure to consult all stakeholders, including those who perform maintenance on our products.

Damaged Solar Array:

Solar power is a sustainable green method of power generation but requires careful planning to properly implement. One important consideration is the location for the solar panels. This solar array at Stevens Institute of Technology was installed outside of one of the engineering buildings with the intention of providing power to the classrooms and research labs inside. Unfortunately the location chosen is just beyond the outfield fence of the college baseball field. This has resulted in a number of home run baseballs being hit into the solar array causing severe damage and reducing the effectiveness of the installation. Installing any sort of protective shielding to prevent baseballs from entering the array would also block the sun, thus further reducing the effectiveness of the array. One solution proposed by the university was to fine students who hit baseballs into the solar panels but this was met with a backlash as the baseball field predates the solar array and to fine students for hitting home runs would effectively punish athletic achievement.

The placement of this array shows that the designers failed to consider environmental factors when designing their system. The decision to fine students to accidentally damage the panels shows a further lack of understanding and an attempt to shift blame for the problem away from the decision makers.

My company always makes sure to consider the environment in which our products are used. In case of errors in judgement we look for solutions to the problem rather than blame the user.

Garbage Truck Crash:

On July 3rd of 2019 a Union City garbage truck suffered a terrible accident that left 12 people injured and shut down both NJ495 and the Lincoln Tunnel just hours before the traffic rush to a long holiday weekend. The truck smashed through several cars and then went through a guardrail on an overpass overlooking the highway. The accident caused major delays and backed up traffic all throughout manhattan as drivers were forced to divert to the George Washington Bridge and the Holland Tunnel.

Although an investigation is still underway as to the actual cause of the accident it is clear that there were several quality failures here. If there was an issue with the truck’s brakes it could highlight a failure to properly maintain the truck. Additionally, the driver stated in an interview that he swerved to avoid as many cars as he could and thought that the guardrail of the overpass would stop the truck if he hit it. This highlights a possible lack of training for drivers in emergency situations as he unable to safely bring the vehicle to a stop and mistakenly believed that the guardrail would stop the truck. It also highlights a design flaw of the overpass if it failed to prevent a vehicle from careening over the side.

My company strives to provide emergency training to our operators to prevent accidents. Education is an important part of quality. Additionally we must work to ensure that all of our safety systems, such as guard rails and safety devices, are functioning properly in case of an emergency.

737 Max

Aircraft manufacturer Boeing has been making headlines for the last few months over the global grounding of its newest 737 aircraft, the 737 Max. Two separate crashes, one in Indonesia (Lion Air 610) and one in Ethiopia, (Ethiopian Airlines 302) brought intense scrutiny on the plane which was grounded by regulators in Europe and China. The United States did not ground the aircraft until much later after pressure by the international community. Investigators focused on the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) which was a ew system which would automatically push the Bose of the aircraft down without pilot input or any alert to the crew. Several pilots had complained about the system in the past and the lack of documentation on this feature.

The MCAS system was necessary due to the larger engines uses on the Max which would cause the aircraft to pitch upwards. The MCAS was intended to give the aircraft similar handling to older 737 aircraft which would avoid the need to get a new type certification. This shortcut was to keep Boeing competitive with Airbus which had just introduced a new aircraft to compete with the 737. Boeing opted not to retrain pilots, a decision which proved fatal when flaws in the MCAS system reading data from faulty sensors caused two aircraft to pitch down into the ground. This situation would have been recoverable had pilots received the proper training. This highlights an education issue on the part of the pilots and a poor use of technology issue for Boeing.

In my company we make sure to provide proper training and notify customers of all new features, no matter how small we believe they are.

Enter Here Sign

This is the drive-through entrance for a restaurant in Conroe Texas. Typically a restaurant with a drive-through will include a sign indicating where customers should begin queueing to place their order. These signs should be clearly marked to aid the flow of traffic and prevent accidents. This sign is confusingly designed to resemble the familiar international sign for no entry with the text “Enter Here.” This causes people to believe that the drive through is closed or inaccessible which could lead to confusion as people attempt to drive the other way around. It seems that the designers of this drive-through were unaware of the preexisting connotations of the sign shape that they chose. This could highlight a lack of education or awareness on international standards in road signs which therefore causes them to not be followed. This also shows a lack of cultural understanding as most people in the area would associate the red circular sign with a line through it as a Do Not Enter sign. The most obvious solution to this problem would be to change the shape or color of the sign so that customers can more easily find the entrance.

In my company we make sure to adhere to all international standards and cultural expectations. It is important for any product to be designed in such a way that it makes sense when compared to existing standards and expectations.

Building Stress Cracks

These images were taken from around the Fredrick E. Giesecke Engineering Research Building. This building is a state of the art research facility which houses multiple laboratories and offices. The building was finished in May of 2015. Over the last few years multiple stress fractures have appeared in various locations around the building. According to the building manager, when the building was designed, settling of the ground was not accounted for in the engineering and structural plans. This has resulted in visible cracks in many locations throughout the inside and outside of the structure. Cracks in the outer wall has also resulted in hot air from the outdoors breaching the offices at the rear of the building reducing the effectiveness of the building’s HVAC system. As a result of this damage the university has had to redesign and rebuild parts of this building which is only four years old. The failure to follow proper engineering processes has resulted in an expensive set of problems for this brand new building. Additionally what should have been a world class state of the art facility has become something of an embarrassing joke among students and faculty.

In my company we always make sure that proper engineering reviews are conducted by unbiased parties. We also make sure that our builders follow best practices and are comfortable raising issues with the design before they become an expensive problem.

Broken Door

This is the entrance door to Fredrick E. Giesecke Engineering Research Building. The building features a double door main entrance which is accessed by a card swipe. Due to high winds which were unaccounted for in the original design the door has become damaged and no longer latches closed on one side. The gap in the door also allows insects and rain to enter the lobby of the building. There have been numerous attempts to repair this door but ultimately it keeps breaking when the high winds pick up again. The failure to include the winds in the engineering plan for the building shows that the designers did not consider environmental factors in their design which is a failure of process. The current “solution” to this problem is to hold the door closed with a cinder block and a sign prompting users to use the other door. This has led to some problems as the cinder block is frequently moved by students or by delivery workers who need to open both doors to bring packages inside the building. On some occasions the card swipe to this door also fails to unlock the working door. In these cases it becomes necessary to move the cinder block and use the broken door to enter the building. However, it is not possible to return the cinder block to its original position after closing the door so the wind causes further damage to the door.

In my company we always make sure that our engineering plans include all relevant environmental factors.
